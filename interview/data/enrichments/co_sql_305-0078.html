Samsung's HR-systems team wants a roster of higher-paid employees with their department name attached. The query is a join plus a filter — about as basic as SQL gets — which is exactly why interviewers use it as a warm-up: they want to see clean join syntax, a sensible join type, and whether you notice the small things, like an employee whose department row is missing, or a filter threshold that looks suspiciously low.

The edge cases: Gus earns exactly 100, which is the boundary — > 100 excludes him. Eve at 90 is below the threshold. And the salary values are tiny (90–200), a strong hint the column is in thousands, or the > 100 literal is a placeholder — worth flagging.

2. The SQL — INNER JOIN + WHERE

SELECT e.emp_id, e.salary, d.dept_name
FROM employees e JOIN departments d ON e.dept_id = d.dept_id
WHERE e.salary > 100;

Join each employee to their department on dept_id, then keep only employees earning more than 100. The bare JOIN keyword is an INNER JOIN — only rows that match on both sides survive.

4. Strategic explanations

> vs >= — the boundary employee

Gus earns exactly 100. salary > 100 excludes him; salary >= 100 would include him. Neither is "right" in the abstract — it depends on whether the requirement is "more than 100" or "at least 100". The senior move is to confirm the intent before writing the operator, and to point at Gus as the row that makes the choice visible.

INNER JOIN vs LEFT JOIN — the missing-department trap

This INNER JOIN silently drops any employee whose dept_id has no matching row in departments — a contractor in a not-yet-created department, or a bad dept_id. If the roster must include every qualifying employee even when the department is unknown, use a LEFT JOIN:

SELECT e.emp_id, e.salary, d.dept_name
FROM employees e
LEFT JOIN departments d ON e.dept_id = d.dept_id
WHERE e.salary > 100;

Now an employee with an orphan dept_id still appears, with dept_name as NULL. Whether to use INNER or LEFT is a real decision — "do unmatched employees belong in this report?" — not a stylistic toss-up.

The filter belongs in WHERE, and that is fine here

salary lives on employees, the preserved side of any join, so WHERE e.salary > 100 is safe even if you switch to a LEFT JOIN. The trap to remember: a predicate on the right table in the WHERE clause would convert a LEFT JOIN back into an INNER JOIN. Filtering the left table never has that effect.

NULL salary or NULL dept_id

An employee with NULL salary fails salary > 100 (the comparison is unknown, not true) and is excluded — usually fine, but say so. An employee with NULL dept_id can never match a department, so the INNER JOIN drops them; under a LEFT JOIN they survive with a NULL department. NULLs quietly remove rows at both the join and the filter — name them rather than letting them disappear.

The suspicious threshold — a data-literacy flag

Salaries of 90 to 200 are not dollars; the column is almost certainly in thousands, or the 100 is a stand-in the question never fleshed out. A senior engineer flags the unit ambiguity: "is salary in thousands? is 100 the real cutoff, or a placeholder?" Writing the query is easy; noticing that the literal might be wrong is the value-add. Parameterise it rather than hard-coding: WHERE e.salary > :threshold.

Senior signal: confirm whether the cutoff is "more than" or "at least" and point at the boundary employee, choose INNER vs LEFT JOIN deliberately based on whether unmatched-department employees belong in the roster, know that a right-table WHERE predicate degrades a left join, and flag the suspiciously low salary threshold as a unit or placeholder ambiguity.
