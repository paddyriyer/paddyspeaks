SAP's web-analytics team wants a traffic-by-hour profile — the classic "when does our site get busy" chart. The reference solution buckets each visit into its hour-of-day and counts. It works, and the interview is about the hours that have no visits, and whether "hour 9" means 9am on one day or 9am across all days.

1. The source data — traffic

Visits cluster at hours 9, 14 and 23 — and every other hour of the day has zero visits. Also note visits 1, 2 and 7 are all in "hour 9" but span two different days. Both facts shape what the query should return.

2. The SQL — bucket by HH24

SELECT CAST(TO_CHAR(visit_time, 'HH24') AS INTEGER) AS hr, COUNT(*) AS visits
FROM traffic GROUP BY CAST(TO_CHAR(visit_time, 'HH24') AS INTEGER) ORDER BY hr;

TO_CHAR(visit_time, 'HH24') formats the timestamp's hour as a two-digit 24-hour string ('09', '14', '23'); the cast turns it into an integer to group and sort cleanly.

3. The resulting analysis

Three rows. Hour 9 shows 3 — it folded together two visits from May 1 and one from May 2, because the bucket is hour-of-day and ignores the date. And hours 0–8, 10–13, 15–22 are simply absent — GROUP BY can only emit rows for hours that actually occur in the data.

4. Strategic explanations

EXTRACT as the cleaner alternative

CAST(TO_CHAR(…, 'HH24') AS INTEGER) formats to text and parses back — a round trip. EXTRACT goes straight to a number:

SELECT EXTRACT(HOUR FROM visit_time) AS hr, COUNT(*) AS visits
FROM traffic
GROUP BY EXTRACT(HOUR FROM visit_time)
ORDER BY hr;

Identical result, no string detour, and it sorts numerically by construction. Both are correct; EXTRACT is the idiom an interviewer expects. (date_part('hour', visit_time) is the same thing.)

The missing hours — the trap that biases the chart

The output has three rows, but a 24-hour profile has 24 slots. GROUP BY never invents a row for an hour with no visits — "zero" is not in the data, so it cannot be in the result. A chart drawn from this jumps 9 → 14 → 23 with the quiet hours missing entirely, not shown as 0. To get a true 0–23 profile, generate the hours and LEFT JOIN:

SELECT h.hr, COUNT(t.visit_id) AS visits
FROM generate_series(0, 23) AS h(hr)
LEFT JOIN traffic t
  ON EXTRACT(HOUR FROM t.visit_time) = h.hr
GROUP BY h.hr
ORDER BY h.hr;

Now every hour 0–23 appears; empty hours show COUNT(t.visit_id) = 0 — note COUNT(t.visit_id), not COUNT(*), so the unmatched LEFT-JOIN row counts as 0 rather than 1.

Hour-of-day vs hour-on-a-date — clarify the grain

Hour 9 = 3 because the query collapses across dates. That is right for "what is our typical busy hour", aggregating a week into a 24-bucket shape. It is wrong for "show traffic for each hour of May 1" — that needs the date in the grouping key:

SELECT date_trunc('hour', visit_time) AS hour_bucket, COUNT(*) AS visits
FROM traffic
GROUP BY date_trunc('hour', visit_time)
ORDER BY hour_bucket;

date_trunc('hour', …) keeps the calendar day, so May 1 09:00 and May 2 09:00 are separate buckets. Confirm which question is being asked before writing the GROUP BY.

The timezone landmine

"Hour of day" is meaningless without a timezone. If visit_time is stored UTC but the business cares about user-local time, every visit is bucketed into the wrong hour — a 9am-local spike shows up at some other UTC hour. Convert explicitly before extracting: EXTRACT(HOUR FROM visit_time AT TIME ZONE 'America/Los_Angeles'). Naming this is a strong senior signal; silently extracting from raw UTC is the common miss.

Edge case: empty table

With no rows, the plain GROUP BY version returns zero rows — no hours, no zeros. The generate_series version still returns all 24 hours, each with a count of 0. If a dashboard must always render a full 24-bar chart, the generate_series form is the only one that survives an empty input.

Senior signal: reach for EXTRACT/date_part over the TO_CHAR-and-cast round trip, recognise that GROUP BY omits zero-traffic hours and fill them with a generate_series LEFT JOIN using COUNT(column), distinguish hour-of-day from hour-on-a-date, and raise the timezone question before bucketing.
