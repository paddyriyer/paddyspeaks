A finance team in the UK reports revenue by fiscal quarter, where the year starts on 1 April. Calendar-quarter functions give the wrong answer: April is the start of FQ1, and January–March belongs to the previous fiscal year. The interviewer is probing whether you can shift a date into a custom fiscal calendar with arithmetic on the month number, and whether you get the year-attribution boundary right.

The boundary cases are the whole interview: 2025-02-15 and 2026-03-30 are both January–March dates that must be attributed to the prior fiscal year, while 2025-04-10 and 2026-04-01 sit exactly on 1 April — the first day of a new fiscal year.

2. The SQL — month-based CASE shifting

SELECT
  CASE WHEN EXTRACT(MONTH FROM d) >= 4 THEN EXTRACT(YEAR FROM d)::INT
       ELSE EXTRACT(YEAR FROM d)::INT - 1
  END AS fiscal_year,
  CASE
    WHEN EXTRACT(MONTH FROM d) BETWEEN 4 AND 6   THEN 1
    WHEN EXTRACT(MONTH FROM d) BETWEEN 7 AND 9   THEN 2
    WHEN EXTRACT(MONTH FROM d) BETWEEN 10 AND 12 THEN 3
    ELSE 4
  END AS fiscal_quarter,
  SUM(amount) AS total_amount
FROM revenue
GROUP BY 1, 2
ORDER BY fiscal_year, fiscal_quarter;

The first CASE attributes the fiscal year: April–December stay in the calendar year, January–March roll back by one. The second CASE buckets the month into FQ1–FQ4. GROUP BY 1, 2 aggregates by the two computed expressions positionally.

4. Strategic explanations

Two shifts, not one — quarter and year

A fiscal calendar moves both the quarter boundaries and the year label. April is FQ1 (quarter shift), but a January date belongs to the fiscal year that started the previous April (year shift). Beginners shift the quarter and forget the year, landing March 2026 in FY2026 instead of FY2025. The two CASE expressions handle the two shifts independently — keep them separate and the logic stays readable.

The boundary is >= 4, and that is deliberate

The fiscal year starts on 1 April, so month 4 is the first month of the new fiscal year — the test is EXTRACT(MONTH FROM d) >= 4, inclusive of April. A frequent off-by-one is > 4, which would push all of April into the prior fiscal year. April–December (months 4–12) keep the calendar year; January–March (1–3) subtract one. Verify with a 1-April row, as the fixture does (2025-04-10 → FY2025).

Why month arithmetic beats a fiscal-calendar table here

For a fixed, simple offset (always 1 April), deriving the fiscal fields with EXTRACT + CASE is self-contained and needs no extra table. The moment the calendar gets irregular — 4-4-5 week patterns, 52/53-week years, mid-month starts — arithmetic breaks down and a date-dimension table (one row per date pre-tagged with fiscal_year and fiscal_quarter) becomes the right tool: you simply join to it. Knowing when to graduate from arithmetic to a calendar table is the senior judgement.

GROUP BY positional references and re-computation

GROUP BY 1, 2 refers to the first two SELECT items by position — concise, and it avoids repeating the long CASE expressions. Postgres also lets you GROUP BY a SELECT-list alias; the SQL standard technically does not, and MySQL/SQL Server differ, so positional or repeated-expression forms are the most portable. Either way the engine evaluates the CASE per row before grouping — there is no hidden cost to the positional shorthand.

Dialect — built-in fiscal helpers

Some engines shortcut this. SQL Server's DATEPART(QUARTER, DATEADD(MONTH, -3, d)) rotates the calendar by a quarter; Snowflake/BigQuery have no native fiscal-quarter function and use the same CASE idiom; Oracle can lean on NLS settings or TO_CHAR tricks. The explicit month-bucket CASE shown here is the most portable and the most transparent — an interviewer can read the boundaries straight off it.

Senior signal: a fiscal calendar needs two shifts — bucket the quarter and roll the year back for pre-April months — get the inclusive >= 4 boundary right, verify against a 1-April row, and know to switch from month arithmetic to a date-dimension table once the fiscal calendar stops being a clean monthly offset.
