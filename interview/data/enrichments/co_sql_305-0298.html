Samsung's merchandising team wants to know which product categories command the highest average price — the premium end of the catalogue, where TVs and appliances live versus accessories and cables. The query is a textbook group-and-rank. The interview value is in the quiet decisions: what "average" means when categories have wildly different sizes, what LIMIT 5 does at a tie, and whether a NULL price should drag the average down.

Six categories, nine products. Some categories have two products, "Appliances" has only one. That single-product category is the edge case: its "average" is just that one price, and a one-product category outranking a two-product category is a feature of the metric worth questioning.

2. The SQL — GROUP BY category, ORDER BY AVG DESC

SELECT category, AVG(price) AS avg_price FROM products
GROUP BY category ORDER BY avg_price DESC LIMIT 5;

One row per category, the mean price within it, sorted highest first, top five kept. Standard aggregate-then-rank shape.

4. Strategic explanations

A one-product category outranks a deep one — is that fair?

Appliances beats Phones with one data point versus two. An average over a tiny sample is volatile and easily distorted by an outlier. The senior move is to expose the sample size and let the consumer judge:

SELECT category, AVG(price) AS avg_price, COUNT(*) AS n
FROM products
GROUP BY category
HAVING COUNT(*) >= 3
ORDER BY avg_price DESC LIMIT 5;

The HAVING COUNT(*) >= 3 excludes categories too thin to average meaningfully. Whether to apply it is a product call — but you should raise it, not silently ship a ranking a single SKU can swing.

Ties at the LIMIT boundary are non-deterministic

If two categories shared the fifth-place average, LIMIT 5 would keep one and drop the other arbitrarily — the planner decides, and the choice can change between runs. To make it reproducible, add a deterministic tiebreaker (ORDER BY avg_price DESC, category), or switch to RANK() if you want all categories tied at the cutoff:

SELECT category, avg_price FROM (
  SELECT category, AVG(price) AS avg_price,
         RANK() OVER (ORDER BY AVG(price) DESC) AS rk
  FROM products GROUP BY category
) t WHERE rk <= 5;
NULL prices — AVG skips them

AVG(price) ignores NULL prices entirely: they drop out of both the sum and the count. A category with three priced products and two unpriced ones is averaged over three. Usually fine — but if an unpriced product means "free" or "price TBD", the average is misleading. And a category where every price is NULL yields AVG = NULL, which sorts to the end under DESC (Postgres puts NULLs first under ASC, last under DESC). Decide whether such a category should appear at all.

Mean vs median for skewed prices

Price distributions are right-skewed — one $5,000 flagship pulls the mean far above the typical product. If "highest average price" really means "highest typical price", the median is more honest:

SELECT category,
       PERCENTILE_CONT(0.5) WITHIN GROUP (ORDER BY price) AS median_price
FROM products GROUP BY category
ORDER BY median_price DESC LIMIT 5;

Naming the mean-versus-median choice signals you think about what the number is for, not just how to compute it.

The wider schema and weighting

The table also carries manufacturer and sku. A natural follow-up is "average price by category and manufacturer", which just adds a column to GROUP BY. A subtler one: should the average be weighted by units sold rather than treating every SKU equally? A simple AVG(price) answers "average sticker price"; a revenue-weighted average answers "average price a customer actually pays" — very different metrics from the same column.

Senior signal: expose COUNT(*) alongside the average so a one-SKU category cannot quietly rank second, make the LIMIT 5 deterministic at ties (tiebreaker or RANK()), note that AVG drops NULL prices, and raise mean-versus-median when prices are skewed.
