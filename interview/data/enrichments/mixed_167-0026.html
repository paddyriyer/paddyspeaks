A university registrar wants the re-enrollment rate: of all students who ever enrolled, what percentage came back for a later term. The reference solution self-joins enrollments to itself — left side is "a term a student took", right side is "a strictly later term by the same student" — then divides distinct returners by distinct students. The interviewer is probing whether you understand why DISTINCT is essential here and how the LEFT JOIN plus NULLIF guard against silent errors.

1. The source data — enrollments

student_idterm(narrative)
12025-Fallstudent 1, first term
12026-Springstudent 1 returns — counts as re-enrolled
22025-Fallstudent 2 — one term only, never returns
32025-Fallstudent 3, first term
32026-Springstudent 3 returns
32026-Fallstudent 3 returns again — three rows, one student
42026-Springstudent 4 — one term only
52025-Fallstudent 5 — one term only

Student 3 is the trap: three enrollment rows. The self-join will multiply that student's rows, so a naive count would over-count both the numerator and the denominator. Five distinct students; two of them (1 and 3) re-enroll.

2. The SQL — self-join with distinct-count ratio

SELECT 100.0 * COUNT(DISTINCT CASE WHEN later.student_id IS NOT NULL
                                   THEN e.student_id END)
            / NULLIF(COUNT(DISTINCT e.student_id), 0) AS reenroll_rate
FROM enrollments e
LEFT JOIN enrollments later
       ON e.student_id = later.student_id
      AND later.term > e.term;

Each enrollment row e is matched to every strictly later term by the same student. A student with no later term still appears once (the LEFT JOIN) but with later.student_id NULL.

3. The resulting analysis

reenroll_ratesignal
40.00000000000000002 of 5 distinct students re-enrolled

The answer is 40%. Students 1 and 3 each have at least one later term, so they enter the numerator; students 2, 4, and 5 do not. The denominator is 5 — the distinct student count — not the 8 enrollment rows, and the numerator is 2 distinct students, not the number of (earlier, later) pairs (student 3 alone contributes three such pairs). Both counts use DISTINCT, which is the only reason 40% comes out correct.

4. Strategic explanations

Why DISTINCT is load-bearing, not decorative

The self-join fans out: student 3's 2025-Fall row matches both 2026-Spring and 2026-Fall, and 2026-Spring matches 2026-Fall — three matched pairs from one student. Without DISTINCT, that student would count three times in the numerator and the row explosion would inflate the denominator too. COUNT(DISTINCT e.student_id) collapses every student to one regardless of how many terms or pairs they generated. The ratio is "students who returned / students who enrolled", so both halves must be counted per student.

The LEFT JOIN keeps non-returners in the denominator

An INNER JOIN would drop students 2, 4, and 5 entirely — they have no later term to match — and the denominator would shrink to just the returners, yielding a meaningless 100%. The LEFT JOIN preserves every enrollment row even when there is no later term; those rows get later.student_id = NULL. The CASE WHEN later.student_id IS NOT NULL in the numerator is what separates returners from non-returners after the join has kept everyone.

NULLIF(..., 0) — the divide-by-zero guard

If the table were empty, COUNT(DISTINCT e.student_id) would be 0 and the division would raise a divide-by-zero error. NULLIF(count, 0) turns a zero denominator into NULL, and dividing by NULL yields NULL — a clean "no data" result instead of a crashed query. This is a small, senior habit: any ratio in production should guard its denominator.

100.0 * forces a real percentage, not integer truncation

COUNT returns an integer; integer divided by integer truncates in SQL (2 / 5 would be 0). Multiplying by the numeric literal 100.0 promotes the whole expression to numeric arithmetic, so the result is a true 40.0, not a floored 0 or 40 with the fraction lost. Putting the 100.0 at the front, before the division, is the correct placement.

Term comparison is string ordering — a hidden assumption

later.term > e.term compares text. It works here only because the labels sort chronologically by luck of formatting — and "2026-Fall" > "2026-Spring" is true alphabetically but the seasons are out of calendar order ("Fall" < "Spring" lexically yet Fall is later in some academic calendars). Real systems store a sortable term key or a start date. Flagging that string comparison is not the same as chronological comparison is the senior catch; the safe fix is to join on a numeric term sequence or a date.

Senior signal: recognise that a self-join fans out multi-term students so both numerator and denominator must use COUNT(DISTINCT student_id), keep non-returners with a LEFT JOIN, guard the ratio with NULLIF(denominator, 0) and 100.0 for numeric division, and call out that comparing term strings is not a reliable chronological order.
