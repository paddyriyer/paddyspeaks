GitLab's revenue team wants to spotlight its most valuable customers — people who order both often and big — for a loyalty tier. "Often and big" is two conditions on aggregated values, which is exactly what HAVING is for. The interview is whether you know why these filters cannot live in WHERE, and how the two thresholds interact.

1. The source data — orders

1101–105300, 250, 200, 150, 200

Three customers, each engineered to fail a different way. Customer 1: 5 orders, $1,100 total — clears both bars. Customer 2: only 2 orders though they total $1,100 — big but not frequent. Customer 3: 5 orders but only $400 — frequent but not big. Only customer 1 should survive.

2. The SQL — GROUP BY + compound HAVING

SELECT customer_id, COUNT(*) AS orders, SUM(amount) AS total
FROM orders GROUP BY customer_id HAVING COUNT(*) >= 5 AND SUM(amount) >= 1000;

GROUP BY customer_id collapses orders into one row per customer; HAVING then filters those groups on two aggregate conditions joined by AND — both must hold.

3. The resulting analysis

One customer survives. Customer 2 is dropped by COUNT(*) ≥ 5 (only 2 orders) despite a healthy $1,100; customer 3 is dropped by SUM(amount) ≥ 1000 (only $400) despite 5 orders. The AND is strict — passing one test is not enough. That is the whole point of the compound predicate.

4. Strategic explanations

Why these filters cannot go in WHERE

WHERE is evaluated row-by-row before grouping — at that point COUNT(*) and SUM(amount) do not exist, because there are no groups yet. HAVING runs after GROUP BY, when each group has its aggregates computed, so it is the only clause that can filter on them. The logical pipeline is FROM → WHERE → GROUP BY → HAVING → SELECT → ORDER BY — reciting it explains every "where does this filter go" question.

WHERE and HAVING together — not either/or

They are complementary, and a good query uses both. WHERE filters rows before aggregation; HAVING filters groups after. To count only completed orders toward the thresholds:

SELECT customer_id, COUNT(*) AS orders, SUM(amount) AS total
FROM orders
WHERE status = 'completed'          -- drop non-completed rows first
GROUP BY customer_id
HAVING COUNT(*) >= 5 AND SUM(amount) >= 1000;

Putting the row-level status filter in WHERE is also faster — fewer rows reach the grouping step. Pushing it into HAVING would work only via a COUNT(*) FILTER (...) contortion and aggregates more rows than necessary.

The NULL-amount trap

If an order has a NULL amount, SUM(amount) skips it but COUNT(*) still counts the row. So a customer could clear COUNT(*) ≥ 5 on order volume while their SUM is quietly understated by the NULL orders — possibly failing the $1,000 bar they would otherwise pass. If a NULL amount should count as $0 that is fine; if it means "unpriced / pending", consider WHERE amount IS NOT NULL so volume and value measure the same set of orders.

The boundary — ≥ vs >

"At least 5 orders" is ≥ 5; "more than 5" is > 5. Customer 1 has exactly 5 and exactly... well, $1,100, but a customer with exactly $1,000 would be the live test for the amount bar. One-character changes that move real customers in and out of the loyalty tier — confirm both thresholds with the stakeholder rather than guessing.

You can HAVING on aggregates not in the SELECT

HAVING is not restricted to the columns you project. You could filter on HAVING AVG(amount) >= 150 or HAVING MAX(amount) > 500 without selecting average or max at all — useful when a threshold is an implementation detail the consumer should not see. And if the consumer wants the list ranked, add ORDER BY total DESC; HAVING filters but does not sort.

Senior signal: explain that aggregate filters require HAVING because WHERE runs before grouping, use WHERE and HAVING together (row filters before, group filters after) for both correctness and speed, watch that a NULL amount lets COUNT(*) and SUM disagree, and treat the ≥ thresholds as business decisions to confirm.
