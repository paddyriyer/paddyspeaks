Uber Business sees a distinctive pattern: corporate riders who go office → airport → office, or home → meeting → home. Detecting these round trips drives commute-bundle pricing, expense-report grouping, and dispatch pre-positioning. The query is a self-join on rider + temporal proximity.

2. The SQL — self-join on rider + same-day window

SELECT a.rider_id, a.ride_id AS first_ride, b.ride_id AS second_ride,
  a.origin_zone || ' -> ' || a.dest_zone AS first_leg,
  b.origin_zone || ' -> ' || b.dest_zone AS second_leg,
  CAST((julianday(b.started_at) - julianday(a.started_at)) * 24 AS INTEGER) AS hours_between
FROM rides a
JOIN rides b
  ON b.rider_id = a.rider_id
 AND b.ride_id != a.ride_id
 AND julianday(b.started_at) > julianday(a.started_at)
 AND julianday(b.started_at) - julianday(a.started_at) <= 1
ORDER BY a.rider_id, hours_between
LIMIT 20;

3. Strategic explanations

Why b.started_at > a.started_at not b.ride_id > a.ride_id

The strict time-ordering predicate establishes "a is the outbound, b is the return". Using ride_id < would dedup pairs but doesn't enforce temporal direction — and ride_ids aren't guaranteed monotonic in time across regions/shards.

The "true round trip" predicate

The strict reverse-zone form: b.origin_zone = a.dest_zone AND b.dest_zone = a.origin_zone. Real-world variation: a return trip might go to a slightly different zone (different door of the same airport, nearby restaurant). Use a zone_within_5km lookup table to relax:

JOIN zone_neighbors zn1 ON zn1.zone_a = a.origin_zone AND zn1.zone_b = b.dest_zone
JOIN zone_neighbors zn2 ON zn2.zone_a = a.dest_zone   AND zn2.zone_b = b.origin_zone
The 24-hour window — why this number

"Same day" is the colloquial phrasing; "within 24 hours" is the operational one. Uber Business specifically uses 4-12 hour windows: shorter than 4h is usually a course correction (driver took wrong route), longer than 12h is two trips not a round trip. Tighten the window for cleaner signal.

The expense-report grouping use case

Uber Business automatically groups round trips into a single expense entry. The query result feeds the bookkeeping logic: each (a, b) pair becomes one expense line with combined fare, total elapsed time, and "round trip" tag. Riders save admin time; Uber retains the relationship.

The fraud watch

The same pattern but with different riders flags driver-rider collusion: "driver picks up a 'rider' who is actually his cousin, drives to airport, drives back" is a fake-trip fraud pattern. Switch the predicate to find this:

JOIN rides b ON b.driver_id = a.driver_id  -- same driver, different rider, reverse route
