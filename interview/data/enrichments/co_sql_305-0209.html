Shopify's merchant-success team wants a cohort view of how each store owner's spend is trending: is a customer ramping up year over year, or quietly churning? "Year-on-year growth rate" sounds like a single percentage, but the interviewer is really watching three things — do you collapse to one row per customer-year first, do you handle the customer's first year (which has nothing to compare against), and do you guard against a divide-by-zero when last year's spend was nil.

Customer 101 has two orders in 2022 — so the table is at order grain, not customer-year grain, and we must SUM first. Customer 202 spent exactly the same in both years (a flat 0% case), and both customers have a first year with no prior to compare to.

2. The SQL — LAG over a pre-aggregated CTE

WITH spend AS (SELECT customer_id, year, SUM(amount) AS total FROM orders GROUP BY customer_id, year)
SELECT customer_id, year, total,
  100.0*(total-LAG(total) OVER (PARTITION BY customer_id ORDER BY year)) /
   NULLIF(LAG(total) OVER (PARTITION BY customer_id ORDER BY year),0) AS yoy_growth
FROM spend;

The CTE collapses orders to one row per customer-year. LAG then reaches back one year within each customer, and the growth is (this − prior) / prior × 100.

4. Strategic explanations

Why the CTE is not optional

If you skip the pre-aggregation and run LAG straight over orders, LAG steps back one row, not one year — with two 2022 orders for customer 101 it would compare order 2 against order 1, both inside 2022, and produce nonsense. The PARTITION BY customer_id ORDER BY year window only behaves like "year over year" once each year is exactly one row. The CTE guarantees that grain.

The first-year NULL — feature, not bug

A customer's earliest year has no predecessor, so yoy_growth is NULL. That is honest: you cannot compute growth without a baseline. If a dashboard needs a value, decide deliberately — COALESCE(…, 0) reads as "no growth" (misleading), so most teams keep the NULL and label it "n/a (first year)". Do not invent a number.

The divide-by-zero guard

NULLIF(prior, 0) is doing real work: if a customer spent 0 last year and something this year, the raw ratio divides by zero. In Postgres integer division by zero errors and aborts the whole query; numeric division by zero also errors. NULLIF converts the 0 denominator to NULL, so that single row's growth becomes NULL instead of killing the report. Note this also means a "0 → 5000" jump shows as NULL, not +∞% — usually acceptable, but call it out.

Gap years — the subtle trap

LAG returns the previous row in the partition, not literally "year − 1". If a customer ordered in 2022 and 2024 but not 2023, LAG pairs 2024 with 2022 and silently labels a two-year change as one-year growth. If true calendar adjacency matters, either generate a dense year spine and left-join, or compare explicitly:

... LAG(total) OVER (PARTITION BY customer_id ORDER BY year) AS prev_total,
    year - LAG(year) OVER (PARTITION BY customer_id ORDER BY year) AS year_gap
-- then only trust yoy_growth where year_gap = 1
Status filtering

The schema carries a status column. Cancelled or refunded orders inflate spend if left in. "Spend" almost always means realised revenue, so a WHERE status = 'completed' inside (or before) the CTE is the filter the interviewer expects you to raise — confirm the definition rather than assume.

Senior signal: pre-aggregate to customer-year grain before windowing so LAG means "year", treat the first-year NULL as correct rather than coalescing it away, explain that NULLIF prevents a divide-by-zero abort, and flag that LAG pairs adjacent rows — not adjacent calendar years — when gap years are possible.
