A merchandising team tracks per-product sales and wants the month-over-month growth rate — a standard "is this product accelerating or decaying?" question. The interviewer is probing two things: can you compare a row to its predecessor without a self-join (the LAG reflex), and can you protect the percentage formula from the division-by-zero that the very first month guarantees.

2. The SQL — LAG for the previous month, NULLIF for safety

WITH total_sale AS (
  SELECT product_id,
         to_char(sale_date, 'YYYY-MM') AS sale_year_month,
         EXTRACT(MONTH FROM sale_date) AS sale_month,
         SUM(quantity) AS total_quantity
  FROM product_sales
  WHERE EXTRACT(YEAR FROM sale_date) = 2099
  GROUP BY product_id, to_char(sale_date,'YYYY-MM'), EXTRACT(MONTH FROM sale_date)
),
previous_month_sales AS (
  SELECT product_id, sale_year_month, sale_month,
         total_quantity AS current_month_sales,
         LAG(total_quantity, 1, 0) OVER (PARTITION BY product_id ORDER BY sale_month ASC) AS previous_month_sales
  FROM total_sale
)
SELECT product_id, sale_year_month,
       current_month_sales, previous_month_sales,
       ROUND(1.0*(current_month_sales-previous_month_sales)/NULLIF(previous_month_sales,0)*100, 2) AS growth_rate
FROM previous_month_sales;

4. Strategic explanations

Why LAG beats a self-join

LAG(total_quantity, 1, 0) OVER (PARTITION BY product_id ORDER BY sale_month) reaches back one row within each product's month-ordered sequence. The alternative — joining the aggregated table to itself on month = month - 1 — is verbose, mishandles December→January wraparound, and breaks on gaps. LAG sorts once and reads the neighbour in a single pass.

The division-by-zero guard — NULLIF

Every product's first month has no predecessor. The third argument of LAG(..., 1, 0) supplies 0 as the default, so previous_month_sales is 0 there. Dividing by that raw 0 would throw. NULLIF(previous_month_sales, 0) converts the 0 to NULL, and division by NULL yields NULL — a clean "not applicable" instead of a crash. The 1.0 * prefix forces numeric (not integer) division so a result like 20.00 is not truncated to 20.

The missing-month trap — LAG sees rows, not the calendar

Product 1 skips March, yet April's "previous month" is February (180) — LAG reaches the previous row, not literally the previous calendar month. So April's −50% is really a Feb→Apr comparison. If the business wants true calendar adjacency, you must first generate a complete month spine (a generate_series calendar) and LEFT JOIN sales onto it, so absent months appear as explicit zero rows.

The ordering key — EXTRACT(MONTH) is single-year-safe only

Ordering by EXTRACT(MONTH FROM sale_date) (values 1–12) works because the WHERE clause restricts to a single year. Span two years and month 1 of 2100 would sort before month 12 of 2099. For a multi-year query, partition-order by the full date_trunc('month', sale_date) instead.

What to say next — first-month presentation

A NULL first-month growth is technically correct but a dashboard may want it shown as "—" or "n/a". Mention COALESCE for display only, and resist the temptation to coerce it to 0% — a brand-new product did not grow 0%, it simply has no baseline. Distinguishing "no data" from "zero" is the senior nuance.

Senior signal: reach for LAG over a self-join for period-over-period math, guard the denominator with NULLIF and force numeric division with 1.0 *, and flag that LAG walks rows not calendar months — gaps need a generated month spine for true adjacency.
