The three power-user questions every consumer-platform exec asks, every quarter, in three flavours:

  1. Marketing: "give me a single skew number for the board deck — how much more does our top 1% watch than the median?"
  2. Infra: "show me the long-tail distribution so I can size the edge cache."
  3. Content Strategy: "tell me who the whales are, but don't promote anyone whose metadata is still in-flight."

Three answers in one query family: PERCENTILE_CONT for the skew factor, NTILE(10) for the decile rollup, and an is_metadata_complete guard for the late-arriving-dimension trap.

1. The source data — viewing_activity

Pareto-shaped sample: a couple of whales (40h, 20h, 18h), a mid tier (5-10h), a long tail of minnows (<3h), plus one user whose metadata hasn't propagated yet (excluded from the analysis to avoid false-positive decile placement).

2. The three queries — and why they're related

Query A — the skew factor: PERCENTILE_CONT P50 vs P99
WITH user_metrics AS (
    SELECT user_id, SUM(watch_seconds) / 3600.0 AS hours
    FROM viewing_activity
    WHERE is_metadata_complete = TRUE
    GROUP BY user_id
)
SELECT
    PERCENTILE_CONT(0.50) WITHIN GROUP (ORDER BY hours) AS median_hours,
    PERCENTILE_CONT(0.99) WITHIN GROUP (ORDER BY hours) AS p99_hours,
    PERCENTILE_CONT(0.99) WITHIN GROUP (ORDER BY hours)
      / NULLIF(PERCENTILE_CONT(0.50) WITHIN GROUP (ORDER BY hours), 0) AS skew_factor
FROM user_metrics;

A skew_factor of 25× means the top 1% watches 25 times more than the median. At Netflix, the real number is ~30-50×, which is why Pareto's principle holds.

Query B — the decile rollup: NTILE(10) avg-per-bucket
WITH user_metrics AS (
    SELECT user_id, SUM(watch_seconds) / 3600.0 AS hours
    FROM viewing_activity
    WHERE is_metadata_complete = TRUE
    GROUP BY user_id
),
ranked AS (
    SELECT user_id, hours,
           NTILE(10) OVER (ORDER BY hours DESC) AS decile
    FROM user_metrics
)
SELECT decile,
       COUNT(*)            AS user_count,
       ROUND(AVG(hours), 2) AS avg_hours_per_user,
       ROUND(SUM(hours), 2) AS total_decile_hours
FROM ranked
GROUP BY decile
ORDER BY decile;

Decile 1 is "the whales" — total_decile_hours is massive. Decile 10 is "the minnows" — nearly zero. The shape of this 10-row table is the long tail.

Query C — combined: decile rollup + skew_factor as a constant column

The runnable solution in this question fuses A and B so one query produces both signals for the dashboard.

3. Why PERCENTILE_CONT beats AVG for skewed distributions

DistributionAVG(hours)MEDIAN(hours)Why median wins
Uniform (1, 2, 3, 4, 5)33They agree — neither is misleading
Skewed (1, 2, 3, 4, 1000)2023AVG is pulled up by one whale; median ignores them

Reporting "average watch hours per user = 202" is technically correct and operationally useless — nobody on the platform watches 202 hours. The median tells you what a typical user actually does.

4. The interviewer's edge-case probes

Probe 1 — "What's the difference between PERCENTILE_CONT and PERCENTILE_DISC?"
FunctionWhat it returnsUse when
PERCENTILE_CONT(0.5)Interpolated — between two adjacent values if N is evenContinuous metrics (revenue, hours, latency)
PERCENTILE_DISC(0.5)An actual value from the data — the row at or just past the 50% rankDiscrete metrics (counts), or when the value must be a real observed value
Probe 2 — "What if a decile has only one user? Does AVG = that user's hours?"

Yes — and that's correct but misleading. NTILE(10) tries to put roughly equal counts in each decile, but with very small datasets you can get 1-2 users per bucket. Senior signal: caveat the chart when total user count is small (say, < 1000) — decile statistics get noisy fast.

Probe 3 — "Why exclude is_metadata_complete = FALSE?"

A user who watched a brand-new show 30 minutes ago might have a row in viewing_activity but the content_name hasn't propagated to the global catalog yet. Their watch_seconds is correct, but during a brief window their hours can be under-counted if downstream consumers join to dim_content and drop rows on the inner join. The Lag-Tolerant View pattern: surface an is_metadata_complete flag and exclude incomplete rows from power-user / VIP / cohort reports. False negatives ("missed a whale for 6 hours") are cheaper than false positives ("promoted a non-whale to the campaign list").

Probe 4 — "AVG vs PERCENTILE_CONT — which one should the board deck show?"

Both, side-by-side. The board needs to see how skewed the platform is — that's the whole point. Show median_hours (the typical user), avg_hours (the headline number people instinctively look at), and skew_factor (the divergence). When skew_factor is large, the difference between AVG and MEDIAN is the story.

Probe 5 — "At Netflix scale (250M users), can you afford PERCENTILE_CONT on the raw distribution?"

No. PERCENTILE_CONT requires a full sort. At 250M rows that's hours. Production answers:

  • APPROX_PERCENTILE (Trino, Snowflake, Spark) — sketch-based, sub-second on billions of rows, ~1% error.
  • Pre-computed histogram — bucket users into 100 hour-bins nightly; the dashboard queries the 100-row histogram, not the 250M-row table.
  • Sample + extrapolate — compute PERCENTILE_CONT on 1% of users, multiply with confidence bands.

The senior-IC answer mentions both the exact form and the production swap-in.

Probe 6 — "How does decile 1 from this week compare to decile 1 from last week? Same users?"

The interesting analysis isn't the static decile rollup — it's the decile transition matrix:

SELECT prev.decile AS last_week_decile,
       curr.decile AS this_week_decile,
       COUNT(*)    AS user_count
FROM weekly_deciles prev
JOIN weekly_deciles curr ON prev.user_id = curr.user_id
WHERE prev.week = '2025-04-21' AND curr.week = '2025-04-28'
GROUP BY 1, 2
ORDER BY 1, 2;

Users staying in decile 1 week-over-week are persistent whales (campaign target). Users dropping from decile 1 → 5 are churn-risk whales (intervention target). The static decile rollup hides this — the transition matrix surfaces it.

Probe 7 — "What does decile 10 look like? Should we even keep them?"

Decile 10 watches 0-2 hours / week. Don't conclude they should be churned — they might be casual sign-ups in a free trial, gift-card recipients, or someone testing the platform for a partner. The skew analysis tells you the platform is healthy because of the top 20%, not that the bottom 80% is worthless. Marketing's job is to move users from decile 10 → 8 → 5, not delete them.

Senior signal: show PERCENTILE_CONT for the skew factor (P50 vs P99), NTILE(10) for the decile rollup, contrast AVG vs MEDIAN explicitly, pre-empt the LAD-tolerant view with is_metadata_complete, mention APPROX_PERCENTILE as the production swap-in at scale, and propose the decile transition matrix as the actually-interesting follow-up question. The interviewer is testing whether you've shipped this on a real consumer platform or only read about long-tail distributions in a stats textbook.
