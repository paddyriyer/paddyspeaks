A Community question on the HackerRank-style hackers/challenges schema: list the hackers who either created the most challenges, or created a number of challenges that no other hacker matched. Two acceptance rules, OR-ed together. The reference packs both into one CTE using a window over an aggregate — a COUNT partitioned by another COUNT — which is the part an interviewer will want you to unpack.

1. The source data

challenges — one row per challenge authored:

Per-hacker totals: hacker 1 = 3, hackers 2 and 3 = 2 each, hacker 4 = 1. Hacker 1 is the sole maximum. Hacker 4's count of 1 is unique — nobody else made exactly one. Hackers 2 and 3 share a count of 2, so they are neither the max nor unique. hackers just supplies names.

2. The SQL — window-over-aggregate

with t1 as(
select hacker_id,
      count(challenge_id) as n,
      count(1) over(partition by count(challenge_id)) as nw_window,
      rank() over(order by count(challenge_id) desc) as n_rank
from challenges
group by hacker_id
)

select t1.hacker_id, h.name, n
from t1 left join hackers h
on t1.hacker_id = h.hacker_id
where nw_window = 1 or n_rank = 1;

Inside t1: count(challenge_id) is the per-hacker total; count(1) OVER (PARTITION BY count(challenge_id)) counts how many hackers share each total; RANK() OVER (ORDER BY count(...) DESC) ranks hackers by volume.

Hacker 1 (Ann) qualifies via n_rank = 1 — the most challenges. Hacker 4 (Di) qualifies via nw_window = 1 — her count of 1 is unique to her. Hackers 2 and 3 are excluded: they share a count of 2 (nw_window = 2) and neither tops the leaderboard. The OR pulls in two hackers for two different reasons.

4. Strategic explanations

The window-over-aggregate — the move that confuses people

The line count(1) OVER (PARTITION BY count(challenge_id)) looks like nested aggregates, which SQL forbids — but it is legal. Postgres evaluates the GROUP BY aggregate count(challenge_id) first, producing one row per hacker; the window function then runs over those grouped rows. So PARTITION BY count(challenge_id) partitions hackers by their total, and count(1) within each partition tells you how many hackers landed on that total. Window functions execute after GROUP BY — that ordering is what makes this work.

Decoding the two filter conditions

nw_window = 1 means "exactly one hacker has this challenge count" — a hacker whose productivity nobody else equalled. n_rank = 1 means "this hacker is tied-or-sole top by volume". They are independent: the maximum could be shared (then n_rank = 1 for several but nw_window > 1), and a unique count could be a low one (Di's single challenge). The OR captures both senses of "stands out". Naming each clause's meaning out loud is the senior move.

Hackers with zero challenges — the silent omission

The query reads only challenges, so a hacker who authored nothing has no row and never appears — even though "0" might itself be a unique count. If the brief includes inactive hackers, you must drive from hackers and LEFT JOIN challenges:

SELECT h.hacker_id, COUNT(c.challenge_id) AS n
FROM hackers h LEFT JOIN challenges c ON c.hacker_id = h.hacker_id
GROUP BY h.hacker_id;

Then COUNT(c.challenge_id) correctly yields 0 for the idle ones. The current query's grain assumes "hacker" means "hacker who authored at least one challenge" — flag that assumption.

Why the join is LEFT, and the orphan risk

t1 LEFT JOIN hackers keeps a hacker_id even if it is missing from the hackers table — the name would come back NULL. That is defensive: a referential gap shows up as a visible NULL rather than a dropped row. With clean data an INNER JOIN behaves identically; the LEFT just refuses to lose a qualifying hacker to a dimension gap.

Edge cases: count-of-1 ambiguity and ordering

Watch the wording: nw_window = 1 selects hackers whose total is unique among hackers — not hackers who made exactly one challenge. The two coincide here only by accident of the data. Also, the query has no ORDER BY; for a stable leaderboard add ORDER BY n DESC, hacker_id.

Senior signal: explain that a window function can partition by a GROUP BY aggregate because windows run after grouping, decode nw_window = 1 as "unique challenge count" and n_rank = 1 as "top by volume", and flag that the query silently omits zero-challenge hackers unless you drive from the hackers table.
