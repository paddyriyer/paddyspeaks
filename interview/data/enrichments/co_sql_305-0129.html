Year-end at a tech company. The CFO needs the total bonus liability for the board meeting tomorrow morning. HR has agreed on a tiered scheme: high performers ('A') get bigger bonuses than steady performers ('B'); within each band, employees with 5+ years of tenure get a multiplier. The query has to be readable enough that legal can audit it on a one-page printout.

2. The SQL — nested CASE, ordered by specificity

The nested CASE walks rules from most specific to least. Order matters: a senior 'A' (perf=A, tenure≥5) must be evaluated before the generic 'A' rule, otherwise everyone with perf=A would land in the 15% bucket regardless of tenure.

SELECT
  emp_id,
  performance,
  tenure_yrs,
  salary,
  CASE
    -- Most specific rules first — order matters in CASE
    WHEN performance = 'A' AND tenure_yrs >= 5 THEN salary * 0.25
    WHEN performance = 'A'                      THEN salary * 0.15
    WHEN performance = 'B' AND tenure_yrs >= 5 THEN salary * 0.10
    WHEN performance = 'B'                      THEN salary * 0.05
    ELSE 0                                                          -- C and below: no bonus
  END AS bonus
FROM employees;

4. Strategic explanations

Why nested CASE beats a multi-table lookup (for now)

An alternative is a dim_bonus_rule table with rows like ('A', 5, 0.25), joined on perf and tenure-bucket. That's cleaner once the rules grow past ~6 — but for a one-time year-end run with stable rules, the inline CASE is auditable on a single screen, which legal prefers over a JOIN they have to chase across two tables.

Order-of-CASE matters — specific first, fallback last

Reverse the order and Employee E01 (perf=A, tenure=7) hits the generic "A" rule first, gets 15% instead of 25%, and the company underpays a senior performer. The pattern is: most-restrictive WHEN goes top, fallback WHEN goes last, ELSE catches the unhandled.

The "tenure cliff" — Employee E06's $920 problem

E06 has 4.99 years of tenure and is one day short of the senior-B bonus. Result: $4,600 instead of $9,200 — a $4,600 cliff over a single day's tenure difference. Boards usually don't care; HR usually does. Production systems either smooth this with a continuous formula (0.05 + 0.01 × LEAST(tenure_yrs, 10)) or codify the cliff explicitly in the offer letter.

The ELSE 0 matters more than it looks

Without ELSE 0, employees who don't match any WHEN get NULL in their bonus column. SUM(bonus) ignores NULLs (which is what you want), but downstream pipelines that check WHERE bonus > 0 silently miss them. The explicit zero makes the contract clear: every employee has a bonus row, even if it's $0.

When this query gets too big — refactor to a rules table

Rule of thumb: 6+ tiers, or rules that change quarterly, or rules that vary by org → move to a dim_bonus_rule table joined on (performance, tenure_band, org_id). The CASE becomes a JOIN; the query is a one-liner; the rules become editable without a deploy.

Senior signal: the interviewer wants to hear two things — order of CASE matters (specific-first, fallback-last) and this works until rules become data, then you refactor. Bonus points for spotting the tenure-cliff problem on E06 unprompted.
