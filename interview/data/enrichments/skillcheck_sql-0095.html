A data-quality audit of the customer table needs to surface accounts that are the same person typed inconsistently — Alice@Example.com, alice@example.com , and  ALICE@example.com are one identity. The interviewer wants every duplicate group reported with its canonical email and signup span, and is checking whether you normalise inside the GROUP BY rather than grouping on the raw, dirty values.

1. The source data — customer

customer_idemailsigned_up_at(narrative)

Two edge cases matter. Alice's three rows look textually different — case and whitespace — yet are one identity. bob@x.com is a genuine singleton and must be excluded. And Carol's two rows are inserted out of chronological order, so the query must pick the true min/max, not the first row it sees.

2. The SQL — normalise inside GROUP BY, gate with HAVING

SELECT LOWER(TRIM(email)) AS email_norm,
       MIN(signed_up_at) AS earliest,
       MAX(signed_up_at) AS latest,
       COUNT(*) AS n_accounts
FROM customer
GROUP BY LOWER(TRIM(email))
HAVING COUNT(*) > 1
ORDER BY email_norm;

LOWER(TRIM(email)) strips surrounding whitespace and folds case, producing a canonical key. Grouping on that expression — not the raw column — collapses the variants into one group; HAVING COUNT(*) > 1 keeps only the genuine duplicates.

All three Alice rows fold into one group of 3 despite their textual differences. Carol's earliest is 2025-04-01 — MIN ignores insert order entirely. bob@x.com is absent: a single account fails the HAVING COUNT(*) > 1 gate.

4. Strategic explanations

Group on the normalised expression, not the raw column

The whole trick is GROUP BY LOWER(TRIM(email)). GROUP BY email would treat Alice@Example.com and alice@example.com  as different keys — each becomes its own group of one and the duplicates are never detected. SQL lets you group by any expression, so the cleanup function is the grouping key. The same normalised expression must appear in the SELECT to report the canonical form.

Why MIN/MAX are correct — tables have no order

Carol's rows are physically inserted newest-first. A solution that grabbed "the first signup_at" via some row-order assumption would return the wrong earliest. MIN(signed_up_at) and MAX(signed_up_at) scan the whole group and are immune to physical order — which is the only safe assumption, because a SQL table is an unordered set. Never rely on insertion or physical row order for a "first" or "last" value.

The order of TRIM and LOWER — and why it does not matter here

LOWER(TRIM(email)) trims then lowercases; TRIM(LOWER(email)) lowercases then trims. For ASCII whitespace and ASCII letters the result is identical, because lowercasing never creates or removes spaces. It is still worth doing both steps: TRIM alone leaves Alice ≠ alice, and LOWER alone leaves the trailing space intact. Each function fixes one defect; you need both.

TRIM only removes the edges

TRIM strips leading and trailing whitespace, not interior whitespace, and standard TRIM only removes spaces — not tabs or non-breaking spaces. If the dirty data contained alice @example.com (an internal space) or a Unicode U+00A0, this normalisation would miss it. For deeper cleansing you would add REGEXP_REPLACE(email, '\s', '', 'g'). Knowing the limit of TRIM is the senior detail.

What this query does not do: choose a survivor

This reports the duplicate groups; it does not decide which customer_id to keep in a dedupe. The natural follow-up is "merge to the earliest account" — ROW_NUMBER() OVER (PARTITION BY LOWER(TRIM(email)) ORDER BY signed_up_at) to tag row 1 as the survivor and the rest as merge candidates. Volunteering that next step shows you understand audit reporting versus remediation.

Senior signal: normalise inside the GROUP BY with LOWER(TRIM(email)) so case/whitespace variants collapse, gate genuine duplicates with HAVING COUNT(*) > 1, use MIN/MAX rather than trusting row order, and know that TRIM only touches the edges — interior or Unicode whitespace needs a regex.
