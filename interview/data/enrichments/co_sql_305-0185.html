Bloomberg's marketing-ops team measures every campaign by ROAS — return on ad spend, revenue divided by spend. The data lives in two tables: campaigns holds the budget, sales holds the attributed revenue rows. The query joins them, sums revenue per campaign, and computes the ratio. The interviewer is watching the join type and the divide-by-zero guard — a campaign that spent money but drove zero sales is the case that breaks naive answers.

Two edge cases. Winter has spend = 0 — a divide-by-zero waiting to happen. And if a campaign had zero sales rows, an inner join would drop it entirely; the LEFT JOIN exists to keep it. Both are deliberate.

2. The SQL — LEFT JOIN + NULLIF

SELECT c.name, SUM(s.amount) AS revenue, c.spend, SUM(s.amount)/NULLIF(c.spend,0) AS roas
FROM campaigns c LEFT JOIN sales s ON c.campaign_id = s.campaign_id
GROUP BY c.name, c.spend;

Every campaign survives the join; revenue is summed per campaign; NULLIF(c.spend, 0) turns a zero spend into NULL so the division yields NULL instead of erroring.

4. Strategic explanations

NULLIF — the divide-by-zero guard

x / 0 raises division by zero in Postgres and aborts the whole query — one bad row kills every campaign's number. NULLIF(c.spend, 0) says "if spend equals 0, treat it as NULL", and dividing by NULL yields NULL, which is harmless. It is the standard, portable guard. The alternative CASE WHEN c.spend = 0 THEN NULL ELSE SUM(s.amount)/c.spend END is equivalent but wordier. Either way, the intent — "a campaign with no spend has no defined ROAS" — should be stated, because NULL is a real business answer here, not just an error dodge.

Why LEFT JOIN and not INNER JOIN

An INNER JOIN keeps only campaigns that have at least one matching sales row. A campaign that spent budget and drove zero sales would silently disappear — and that is exactly the campaign marketing-ops most needs to see (it has the worst possible ROAS: 0). LEFT JOIN keeps every campaign; a sales-less campaign gets NULL for s.amount, and SUM over only-NULLs returns NULL. If the report should show 0 revenue rather than NULL, wrap it: COALESCE(SUM(s.amount), 0) — and then ROAS becomes a real 0 / spend = 0.

The double-counting trap when a campaign joins many sales

Spring has two sales rows, so the join produces two rows for Spring before aggregation — correct here, because SUM is meant to add them (1500 + 2500 = 4000). But the trap appears the moment you add a second one-to-many join (say, clicks): the two joins multiply, and SUM(s.amount) double-counts. The fix is to pre-aggregate each side in its own subquery before joining:

SELECT c.name, c.spend, sr.revenue, sr.revenue / NULLIF(c.spend, 0) AS roas
FROM campaigns c
LEFT JOIN (SELECT campaign_id, SUM(amount) AS revenue FROM sales GROUP BY campaign_id) sr
  ON sr.campaign_id = c.campaign_id;
GROUP BY name is fragile — group by the key

The query groups by c.name. If two distinct campaigns share a name (a re-run "Spring" in a later year), their revenue silently merges into one row. Group by the primary key instead — GROUP BY c.campaign_id, c.name, c.spend — so each campaign stays its own row even with a duplicate name. Grouping by a non-unique label is a subtle correctness bug.

Edge case: a sales row with no parent campaign

If sales contains a campaign_id that does not exist in campaigns (orphaned attribution), the LEFT JOIN from campaigns simply never sees it — that revenue is dropped from every total. That may be desired (only known campaigns count) or a data-quality red flag. A quick anti-join audit — SELECT * FROM sales s WHERE NOT EXISTS (SELECT 1 FROM campaigns c WHERE c.campaign_id = s.campaign_id) — surfaces it.

Senior signal: justify the LEFT JOIN as the thing that keeps a zero-sales campaign visible, explain NULLIF as both a divide-by-zero guard and a meaningful "ROAS undefined" signal, group by the campaign key rather than the name, and pre-empt the fan-out double-counting that appears once a second one-to-many join is added.
