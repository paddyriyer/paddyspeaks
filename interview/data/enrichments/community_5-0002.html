A Community logistics team wants a fulfilment scorecard: on average, how long does each customer wait between ordering and delivery? The arithmetic is trivial — subtract two dates — but the interviewer is probing the things that quietly corrupt the number: undelivered orders sitting as NULLs, integer-vs-numeric rounding, and whether you average per order or per customer.

Customer 101 has two orders with very different lead times (3 days and 7 days) — their average should land between, at 5. Customer 103's single 12-day order is the slow outlier the report exists to surface.

2. The SQL — AVG(delivery_date - order_date)

WITH per_order AS (
  SELECT customer_id,
         (delivery_date - order_date)::numeric AS days_to_deliver
  FROM orders
)
SELECT customer_id,
       ROUND(AVG(days_to_deliver), 2) AS avg_delivery_days
FROM per_order
GROUP BY customer_id
ORDER BY avg_delivery_days DESC;

In Postgres, subtracting one DATE from another yields an integer count of days. The CTE computes that gap once per order and casts it to numeric so the later AVG/ROUND work in decimal, not integer, arithmetic.

4. Strategic explanations

The integer-division trap — why the cast is load-bearing

Drop the ::numeric and the column stays integer. AVG of an integer column in Postgres still returns numeric, so this particular query survives — but the same pattern in MySQL or SQLite would do integer division and report customer 102 as 1 or 2, never 1.5. Casting to numeric (or ::float) before averaging makes the intent explicit and portable. Never trust an average over an integer column without checking the dialect.

The NULL delivery_date — the question the interviewer is really asking

The schema carries a status column, which means some orders are not delivered yet — their delivery_date is NULL. NULL - order_date is NULL, and AVG silently skips NULLs. That is usually correct: an undelivered order has no lead time to average. But say it out loud, and if the business wants only completed orders counted, filter explicitly:

WITH per_order AS (
  SELECT customer_id, (delivery_date - order_date)::numeric AS days_to_deliver
  FROM orders
  WHERE status = 'delivered'      -- or: WHERE delivery_date IS NOT NULL
)
SELECT customer_id, ROUND(AVG(days_to_deliver), 2) AS avg_delivery_days
FROM per_order GROUP BY customer_id;

A customer with only pending orders then disappears from the result entirely — AVG over zero non-NULL values is NULL, and the group still appears with a NULL average. Decide whether that customer should show as NULL or be dropped.

Average of averages — the aggregation-grain mistake

This query averages per order, then groups — correct. A tempting wrong move is to pre-aggregate twice, e.g. average per order-month and then average those monthly figures. That is an average of averages and it is biased: a month with one slow order is weighted equally to a month with fifty fast ones. Always average the finest-grain values (one row per order) in a single AVG; never average numbers that are themselves averages unless you carry their weights.

Mean vs median — the outlier follow-up

Customer 103's 12-day order would drag their mean up even if every other order were fast. Interviewers often follow with "the mean is skewed — give me the median." Postgres has an ordered-set aggregate for exactly this:

SELECT customer_id,
       PERCENTILE_CONT(0.5) WITHIN GROUP (ORDER BY days_to_deliver)
         AS median_delivery_days
FROM per_order
GROUP BY customer_id;

Knowing that AVG answers "typical total throughput" while the median answers "typical single experience" — and naming which the business wants — is the senior distinction.

Same-day and negative gaps

An order delivered the same day yields a 0-day gap (fine). A delivery_date before order_date yields a negative gap — almost always a data-entry error that quietly deflates the average. A defensive query adds WHERE delivery_date >= order_date or flags the bad rows for review rather than averaging them in.

Senior signal: cast the date difference to numeric so the average is not silently truncated, state that NULL delivery dates are skipped by AVG and decide whether undelivered orders belong in scope, average at the finest grain to avoid an average-of-averages bias, and offer the median when an outlier could distort the mean.
