DoorDash's batched-delivery feature stacks two orders for one driver. The query detects when one dasher serviced two orders within an hour — the data signal of stacking.

2. The SQL — temporal self-join

SELECT a.dasher_id, a.delivery_id AS order_a, b.delivery_id AS order_b,
  CAST((julianday(b.started_at) - julianday(a.started_at)) * 60 AS INTEGER) AS minutes_apart
FROM deliveries a
JOIN deliveries b
  ON b.dasher_id = a.dasher_id
 AND b.delivery_id != a.delivery_id
 AND julianday(b.started_at) > julianday(a.started_at)
 AND julianday(b.started_at) - julianday(a.started_at) <= 1.0/24
ORDER BY a.dasher_id, a.started_at;

3. Strategic explanations

The 1.0/24 idiom

1 hour = 1/24 days = 0.0417. The constant comes from julianday's day-units.

Why stacking matters — economics

Two orders, one trip:

  • Dasher gets paid for both
  • DoorDash saves a delivery fee
  • Customer waits longer (avg +8 min for the second drop)

Customer experience trade-off; revenue boost. Production has a target rate (~25% of orders stacked).

True stack vs back-to-back

A dasher who finishes order A and immediately accepts order B is back-to-back, not stacked. True stacking has the dasher carry both at once. The query's 1-hour window captures both. Production splits with order_a.ended_at > order_b.started_at as the strict-stacking predicate.

The stacking-acceptance rate

Drivers can decline stacked offers. The acceptance rate per market signals dasher attitude — low acceptance indicates the stack-pay isn't compensating for added complexity.

Customer-tier impact

DashPass (subscription) customers shouldn't be stacked — they pay for premium delivery. Production excludes their orders from stacking offers.

Senior signal: use 1.0/24 for julianday-hour conversion, distinguish true-stack (overlapping) from back-to-back, name the 25% target rate, and exclude DashPass from stacking.
