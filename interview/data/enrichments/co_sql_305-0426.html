Datadog charges by metric volume. Per-service ingest rate is the billing signal AND the capacity-planning input.

2. The SQL — datapoints + active days

SELECT service_id, COUNT(*) AS total_datapoints,
  COUNT(DISTINCT DATE(ingested_at)) AS active_days,
  ROUND(COUNT(*) * 1.0 / NULLIF(COUNT(DISTINCT DATE(ingested_at)), 0), 0) AS avg_datapoints_per_day
FROM metric_datapoints GROUP BY service_id ORDER BY total_datapoints DESC;

3. Strategic explanations

The "service" granularity

Customers are billed at the host level, but troubleshooting needs service-level. Both grains exist; this query is service.

Bursty vs steady

A service with 1M datapoints over 30 days = 33K/day. A service with 1M in 1 day, then 0 = bursty (test? scheduled job?). active_days exposes the shape.

Cardinality explosion

One service emitting request_id as a tag = billions of unique time series, 100x cost. Production tracks "high-cardinality tags" and alerts customers before billing surprises.

Throttling at limit

Customers approaching plan limits get throttled. Per-service ingest rate is the input to the throttle decision.

Aggregation as a cost-saver

Pre-aggregate per-host counters into per-service before sending. Cuts ingest 100x. Datadog's StatsD agent does this; the query measures effectiveness.

Senior signal: distinguish bursty vs steady via active-days, watch for cardinality explosion as a cost crisis, connect to throttle-decisioning, and recommend aggregation as cost optimization.
