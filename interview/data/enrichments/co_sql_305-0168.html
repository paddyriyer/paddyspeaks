Databricks' growth team wants to power a "customers who bought X also bought Y" widget. The raw signal is which product pairs show up together in the same customer's order history. The interviewer is probing whether you can express "pairs within a group" as a self-join — and whether you avoid the two classic bugs: counting a product with itself, and counting each pair twice.

1. The source data — orders

Six customers, each shown with the products they bought (one row per product in the real table). Products 100 and 200 are bought together by customers 1, 2, 5 and 6 — the strongest pair. Product 400 appears for exactly one customer — a thin, low-confidence pair.

2. The SQL — self-join on customer

SELECT a.product_id AS p1, b.product_id AS p2, COUNT(*) AS pair_count
FROM orders a
JOIN orders b
  ON a.customer_id = b.customer_id
 AND a.product_id < b.product_id
GROUP BY a.product_id, b.product_id
ORDER BY pair_count DESC
LIMIT 10;

Join the table to itself on the same customer. The a.product_id < b.product_id predicate is the engine of the query — it does two jobs at once.

4. Strategic explanations

Why < and not <> — the double-counting trap

The inequality does two jobs. First, it excludes a product paired with itself (with = every customer's product would pair with its own copy). Second, and subtler: if you wrote a.product_id <> b.product_id, you would get both (100,200) and (200,100) — each pair counted twice, with the counts inflated and the result list cluttered with mirror rows. a.product_id < b.product_id keeps exactly one ordered representative of each unordered pair. This is the canonical "unordered pairs" idiom.

The interviewer follow-up: confidence, not just count

A raw co-purchase count favours popular products. Recommendation engines use lift or confidence — P(bought B | bought A) — to normalise:

SELECT a.product_id AS p1, b.product_id AS p2,
       COUNT(*)::numeric
         / (SELECT COUNT(DISTINCT customer_id) FROM orders o WHERE o.product_id = a.product_id)
         AS confidence
FROM orders a JOIN orders b
  ON a.customer_id = b.customer_id AND a.product_id < b.product_id
GROUP BY a.product_id, b.product_id
ORDER BY confidence DESC;

Confidence answers "of people who bought A, what fraction also bought B" — far more actionable than a count dominated by best-sellers.

The duplicate-purchase edge case

If a customer buys product 100 twice, orders has two rows for (customer, 100). The self-join then multiplies: that customer contributes two rows to the (100, 200) pair, double-counting. Guard against it by deduplicating first:

WITH cp AS (SELECT DISTINCT customer_id, product_id FROM orders)
SELECT a.product_id, b.product_id, COUNT(*)
FROM cp a JOIN cp b
  ON a.customer_id = b.customer_id AND a.product_id < b.product_id
GROUP BY a.product_id, b.product_id;

The reference query's demo table happens to have one row per (customer, product), so it is correct here — but on a real orders table with line items and quantities, the DISTINCT CTE is mandatory.

The thin-pair problem — threshold before you ship

Pairs (100,400) and (200,400) have pair_count = 1: a single customer. Recommending off one observation is noise. Add HAVING COUNT(*) >= 5 (or whatever the data supports) so the widget only surfaces pairs with real statistical weight.

O(n²) within each group

A self-join over a customer with k products generates roughly k²/2 pair rows. A customer with hundreds of purchases explodes combinatorially. At scale you would cap basket size, pre-aggregate, or move pair-mining to a batch job — this query is the right logic, but not always the right runtime.

Senior signal: use a.product_id < b.product_id to get each unordered pair exactly once (not <>, which double-counts), deduplicate (customer, product) before the self-join when line items repeat, threshold thin pairs with HAVING, and offer confidence/lift as the metric a recommender actually wants.
