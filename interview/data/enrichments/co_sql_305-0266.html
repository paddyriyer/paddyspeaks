Broadcom's people-analytics team wants annual turnover broken out by manager — how many of each manager's reports left, per year. It is a clean GROUP BY, but two decisions hide inside it: how do you treat employees who are still employed (no termination date), and what does "year" even mean — the year they left, or the year they were hired?

Four employees left; two (5 and 6) have a NULL term_date — they are still employed. They must not count as turnover. Manager 10 lost people in two different years (2023 and 2024), so that manager appears on more than one output row.

2. The SQL — GROUP BY manager, year-of-term

SELECT manager_id,
  CAST(TO_CHAR(term_date, 'YYYY') AS INTEGER) AS yr,
  COUNT(*) AS turnover
FROM employees WHERE term_date IS NOT NULL
GROUP BY manager_id, CAST(TO_CHAR(term_date, 'YYYY') AS INTEGER);

WHERE term_date IS NOT NULL drops still-employed staff before grouping. TO_CHAR(term_date,'YYYY') extracts the year as text, then a cast turns it into an integer for a clean numeric column.

Three rows. Manager 10 lost two reports in 2023 and one in 2024 — one manager, two rows, because the group key is (manager, year). Manager 20 lost one in 2023. The still-employed pair (5 and 6) vanished thanks to the WHERE; without it they would have grouped under a NULL year and inflated counts.

4. Strategic explanations

Why filter NULLs before grouping, not after

If you skip the WHERE, the two still-employed rows survive. TO_CHAR(NULL,'YYYY') is NULL, so they land in a (manager_id, NULL) group — a phantom "turnover" bucket for people who never left. COUNT(*) would count them. The WHERE clause is not cosmetic; it encodes the definition of turnover: turnover means a completed departure.

COUNT(*) vs COUNT(term_date)

Here COUNT(*) is safe because the WHERE already removed every NULL. If you dropped the filter and relied on COUNT(term_date) instead — which skips NULLs — the counts would be right but the yr column would still show a spurious NULL-year row. Filtering is the cleaner fix; COUNT(column) alone does not remove the bad group, it only stops counting inside it.

Cleaner year extraction — EXTRACT and DATE_TRUNC

TO_CHAR(…)::INTEGER round-trips a date through text and back to a number. Two more direct idioms:

-- EXTRACT returns numeric directly, no cast dance
SELECT manager_id, EXTRACT(YEAR FROM term_date)::int AS yr, COUNT(*)
FROM employees WHERE term_date IS NOT NULL
GROUP BY manager_id, EXTRACT(YEAR FROM term_date);

-- DATE_TRUNC keeps a real date, good for joining to a calendar dim
GROUP BY manager_id, DATE_TRUNC('year', term_date);

All three give the same buckets. EXTRACT is the most readable; DATE_TRUNC is best when the result feeds a time-series chart.

Turnover rate — the follow-up the count cannot answer

A raw count is misleading: a manager with 50 reports losing 5 is healthier than one with 6 reports losing 5. The interviewer will ask for a rate — departures over headcount. That needs the denominator, which the filtered query has thrown away. Compute both sides with conditional aggregation, no filter:

SELECT manager_id,
       COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE term_date IS NOT NULL) AS leavers,
       COUNT(*)                                     AS total_reports,
       ROUND(100.0 * COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE term_date IS NOT NULL)
                   / COUNT(*), 1) AS turnover_pct
FROM employees
GROUP BY manager_id;
Missing years and re-orgs

If a manager had zero departures in a year, that year simply does not appear — GROUP BY never invents empty groups. For a dense year-by-year table, cross-join managers against a year series and LEFT JOIN the counts, coalescing to 0. Also note manager_id reflects the current manager; if someone changed managers before leaving, the departure is attributed to whoever they reported to last — worth flagging as an assumption.

Senior signal: filter term_date IS NOT NULL before grouping so still-employed staff cannot form a phantom NULL-year bucket, prefer EXTRACT/DATE_TRUNC over a TO_CHAR-and-cast round trip, and pre-empt the "turnover rate not count" follow-up by keeping the headcount denominator with COUNT(*) FILTER.
