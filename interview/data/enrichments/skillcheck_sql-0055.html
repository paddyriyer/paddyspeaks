A finance team in the UK reports on a fiscal year that starts on 1 April, not 1 January: "aggregate revenue by fiscal quarter." An order in January 2025 belongs to FY2024 Q4, while April 2025 opens FY2025 Q1. The interviewer is probing whether you can shift the calendar — map a real date onto a fiscal year and quarter with arithmetic rather than a wall of CASE branches.

1. The source data — revenue

order_dateamountfiscal mapping

2026-01-15500Jan → FY2025 Q4 (boundary: prior fiscal year)
2026-04-15600Apr → FY2026 Q1

The Jan–Mar rows are the edge case: calendar January 2025 is fiscal Q4 of FY2024. Any solution that uses the raw calendar year or calendar quarter gets these wrong.

2. The SQL — modular date arithmetic

SELECT
  CASE WHEN EXTRACT(MONTH FROM order_date) >= 4
       THEN EXTRACT(YEAR FROM order_date)::int
       ELSE EXTRACT(YEAR FROM order_date)::int - 1
  END AS fiscal_year,
  ((EXTRACT(MONTH FROM order_date)::int - 4 + 12) % 12) / 3 + 1 AS fiscal_quarter,
  SUM(amount) AS total
FROM revenue
GROUP BY fiscal_year, fiscal_quarter
ORDER BY fiscal_year, fiscal_quarter;

The fiscal year is the calendar year, minus one if the month is before April. The fiscal quarter shifts each month back by 4 (April→0), wraps with + 12) % 12 so January–March land at 9–11, then integer-divides by 3 and adds 1 to give 1–4.

The two January rows land in different fiscal years (FY2024 and FY2025) and both in Q4 — exactly the calendar shift the question demands. A naive EXTRACT(QUARTER ...) would have put them in Q1.

4. Strategic explanations

Why the modular formula beats a 12-way CASE

You could write twelve WHEN month = 1 THEN 4 branches, but it is verbose and easy to mistype. The arithmetic ((month - 4 + 12) % 12) / 3 + 1 encodes the same mapping in one expression: subtracting 4 makes April the origin, + 12) % 12 handles the wrap for Jan–Mar without negative numbers, and dividing by 3 buckets three months per quarter. One formula, no branches to get out of sync.

The + 12) % 12 guard against negative modulo

For January, month - 4 = -3. In PostgreSQL -3 % 12 is -3 — the result keeps the sign of the dividend, unlike a true mathematical modulo. Without the + 12, (-3) / 3 + 1 = 0, a nonsense quarter. Adding 12 first makes the value non-negative (9) before the modulo, so the division is well-defined. This sign-of-modulo subtlety is a classic interview trap and differs across languages and SQL dialects.

Integer division is doing real work

9 / 3 must be 3, not 3.0. Because both operands are integers (the ::int casts matter), PostgreSQL performs integer division and truncates — that truncation is exactly the "bucket into quarters" step. If a stray cast made either side numeric, you would get 3.0 and + 1 would yield 4.0, breaking the integer quarter label.

Grouping by a SELECT alias

The query groups by fiscal_year and fiscal_quarter — the output aliases. PostgreSQL permits a GROUP BY to reference SELECT-list aliases (an extension to the SQL standard, also allowed in MySQL). Strict engines — SQL Server, Oracle — do not: there you must repeat the full CASE/arithmetic expressions in GROUP BY, or wrap the projection in a subquery and group outside.

Dialect notes

SQL Server has no EXTRACT; use MONTH(order_date) and YEAR(order_date). Many shops keep a dedicated date dimension table with a pre-computed fiscal_year/fiscal_quarter column — then this becomes a simple join, the fiscal-calendar rules live in one governed place, and irregular calendars (4-4-5, 53-week years) that pure arithmetic cannot express are handled by data.

Senior signal: shift the calendar with one modular formula instead of a 12-branch CASE, guard the negative-modulo case with + 12) % 12, rely on integer division to bucket months into quarters, and note that production fiscal reporting usually lives in a date-dimension table so irregular calendars are data, not code.
