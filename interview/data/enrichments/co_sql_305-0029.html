NVIDIA's sales-ops team wants a "customer context" report: for every customer, how many other orders they placed and what those add up to. The reference solution reaches for a self-join on customer_id — and that choice quietly inflates the numbers. An interviewer posing this is watching whether you notice the fan-out before the recruiter does.

1. The source data — orders

Customer 101 has three orders, 102 has two, and 103 has a single order. Those different group sizes are the whole interview — the single-order customer and the three-order customer behave very differently under this query.

2. The SQL — self-join on customer_id

SELECT a.customer_id, COUNT(b.order_id) AS related_orders, SUM(b.amount) AS total
FROM orders a JOIN orders b ON a.customer_id = b.customer_id AND a.order_id <> b.order_id
GROUP BY a.customer_id;

Every order a is joined to every other order b of the same customer. Then the rows are grouped back down per customer and counted and summed.

3. The resulting analysis

Look hard at customer 101. It has three orders worth $350 total, yet the query reports 6 related orders summing to $700 — exactly double. And customer 103 has vanished from the result entirely. Neither of those is a coincidence.

4. Strategic explanations

The fan-out: why a 3-order customer reports 6

With M orders, the self-join condition a.order_id <> b.order_id produces M × (M − 1) rows per customer: each of the 3 a rows pairs with the other 2 b rows, so 3 × 2 = 6. COUNT(b.order_id) then counts all 6, and SUM(b.amount) adds each amount (M − 1) times — here twice, so $350 becomes $700. The self-join didn't compute "other orders"; it computed ordered pairs of distinct orders, and the aggregate inherited that multiplicity.

The disappearing single-order customer

Customer 103 has one order. The condition a.order_id <> b.order_id can never be satisfied — there is no other order to pair with — so the inner JOIN drops it. If the report is meant to say "103 has 0 related orders, $0", an inner join is the wrong tool. A LEFT JOIN keeps the row:

SELECT a.customer_id,
       COUNT(b.order_id)            AS related_orders,
       COALESCE(SUM(b.amount), 0)   AS total
FROM orders a
LEFT JOIN orders b
  ON a.customer_id = b.customer_id AND a.order_id <> b.order_id
GROUP BY a.customer_id;

Now 103 appears with related_orders = 0 — COUNT(b.order_id) ignores the NULL from the unmatched side — and COALESCE turns the NULL sum into 0.

The fix a senior actually reaches for: plain GROUP BY

"How many orders and what total per customer" needs no join at all. The self-join is a wrong turn:

SELECT customer_id, COUNT(*) AS order_count, SUM(amount) AS total
FROM orders
GROUP BY customer_id;

This gives 101 → 3 orders, $350; 102 → 2, $380; 103 → 1, $500 — every customer present, every amount counted once. If the requirement genuinely is "excluding the current order", a window function expresses it without fan-out: COUNT(*) OVER (PARTITION BY customer_id) - 1 and SUM(amount) OVER (PARTITION BY customer_id) - amount.

The interviewer's probe: "are these numbers right?"

The strongest answer doesn't just run the query — it sanity-checks the output against the input. "Customer 101 placed three orders totalling $350, but the report says $700. A self-join over a one-to-many relationship multiplies the aggregate by the group size; I'd switch to a GROUP BY." Catching a 2× inflation by eyeballing is the senior move; trusting the query is the junior one.

Edge case: NULL amounts and duplicate order rows

If amount can be NULL, SUM skips those rows silently — fine for revenue, but state it. And if order_id is not unique, a.order_id <> b.order_id stops being a reliable "different order" test; two genuinely distinct rows sharing an id would be wrongly excluded. A trustworthy order_id primary key is an assumption worth voicing.

Senior signal: recognise that a self-join across a one-to-many relationship fans out and multiplies SUM/COUNT by the group size, notice the single-order customer silently dropped by the inner join, and replace the whole construction with a plain GROUP BY (or a window function) that counts every customer exactly once.
