Amazon's catalog team powers the star rating you see on every product page. The query behind it — average rating per product — looks like a textbook GROUP BY. The interview is in the second column: review_count. A 5.0 from one review and a 4.6 from ten thousand reviews are not the same signal, and an interviewer wants to see you understand why the count rides along with the average.

Product 103 has exactly one review — a perfect 5.0 on a sample size of one. Product 104 has four reviews averaging a mediocre 2.5. The single-review product is the edge case the review_count column exists to expose.

2. The SQL — AVG + COUNT per group

SELECT product_id, AVG(rating) AS avg_rating, COUNT(*) AS review_count
FROM reviews GROUP BY product_id;

One row per product; AVG(rating) is the mean star score, COUNT(*) is how many reviews backed it.

3. The resulting analysis

Product 103 tops the list at a flawless 5.0 — but on a single review, that "5.0" is statistically meaningless next to product 101's 4.0 across three. The long tail of digits (4.0000000000000000) is Postgres returning numeric: AVG of an integer column produces a high-scale numeric, not a clean float. Rows come back unordered without an explicit ORDER BY.

4. Strategic explanations

The single-review trap — why review_count is mandatory

Sort products by avg_rating alone and product 103's 5.0 wins, even though one rating proves almost nothing. Every credible "top rated" ranking guards against this with a minimum-reviews threshold:

SELECT product_id, AVG(rating) AS avg_rating, COUNT(*) AS review_count
FROM reviews
GROUP BY product_id
HAVING COUNT(*) >= 10          -- only products with enough signal
ORDER BY avg_rating DESC;

The truly senior answer goes further — a Bayesian (shrinkage) average that pulls low-count products toward the global mean, so a 5.0×1 ranks below a 4.7×5000. Mentioning that raw AVG is naive for ranking is an over-the-bar signal.

Rounding — presentation versus storage

4.0000000000000000 is ugly on a product page. Round at the edge:

ROUND(AVG(rating), 2) AS avg_rating   -- 4.00

Note ROUND(numeric, n) works directly, but if AVG ever returns double precision you must cast: ROUND(AVG(rating)::numeric, 2). Keep full precision in the warehouse and round only for display — rounding early loses information you cannot recover.

Products with zero reviews are missing entirely

reviews only has rows for products that have a review. A brand-new product with none simply does not appear in this output — GROUP BY cannot invent a group from no rows. If the catalog page needs "no reviews yet" instead of a blank, drive the query from the products table:

SELECT p.product_id,
       ROUND(AVG(r.rating), 2) AS avg_rating,
       COUNT(r.rating)         AS review_count
FROM products p
LEFT JOIN reviews r ON r.product_id = p.product_id
GROUP BY p.product_id;

Here COUNT(r.rating) — counting a column, not * — correctly returns 0 for unmatched products, and AVG returns NULL, which you can COALESCE.

COUNT(*) vs COUNT(rating) — NULL ratings

If a review row can exist with a NULL rating (a text-only review), COUNT(*) counts that row but AVG ignores it — so review_count would overstate the number of scored reviews. Use COUNT(rating) to count only ratings that actually fed the average, keeping the two columns consistent.

Senior signal: recognise that AVG alone is a misleading ranking key — pair it with COUNT, add a minimum-reviews HAVING, and ideally name a Bayesian average; round only for display; and know that zero-review products need a LEFT JOIN from the products table with COUNT(col) rather than COUNT(*).
