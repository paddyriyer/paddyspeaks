Twitch's content-analytics team tracks weekly box-office gross for the movies featured on streamer watch-parties. The signal they care about is momentum — is a film building or fading week over week? The interviewer wants to see LAG used cleanly, and is watching whether you handle a movie's very first week and keep one film's numbers from leaking into another's.

Movie 1 fades for two weeks then ticks back up in week 4. Movie 2 opens huge and collapses. The interesting parts: week 1 of each movie (no prior week to compare against) and the fact that movie 2's week 1 must not be compared to movie 1's week 4.

2. The SQL — LAG week-over-week delta

SELECT movie_id, week, gross,
  gross - LAG(gross) OVER (
    PARTITION BY movie_id
    ORDER BY week
  ) AS week_change
FROM box_office;

LAG(gross) reaches back one row to the previous week's gross; PARTITION BY movie_id resets that lookback at each movie; ORDER BY week defines what "previous" means.

Each movie's week 1 returns NULL — there is no prior week, so LAG has nothing to subtract. Movie 1's decline (−300, −200) then a recovery (+50) is visible. Movie 2's −1200 crash stands out. Crucially, movie 2's week 1 is NULL, not 2000 − 550 — the partition stopped LAG from reaching into movie 1.

4. Strategic explanations

The first-row NULL is correct — but the consumer may want 0

A movie's opening week has no predecessor, so week_change is genuinely undefined — NULL is the honest answer. But a chart that sums or averages week_change may want 0 there instead. LAG takes an optional default for exactly this:

gross - LAG(gross, 1, gross) OVER (PARTITION BY movie_id ORDER BY week)
-- when there is no prior row, LAG returns the 3rd arg (gross itself),
-- so week 1 change becomes gross - gross = 0

Choose NULL or 0 deliberately. They mean different things: NULL says "not comparable", 0 says "no change".

The PARTITION BY is load-bearing — drop it and the result is silently wrong

Without PARTITION BY movie_id, LAG walks the entire ordered result set, so movie 2's week 1 would subtract movie 1's last row: 2000 - 550 = 1450 — a meaningless cross-movie delta, with no error to flag it. The partition is what makes "previous week" mean "previous week of the same movie". This is the most common bug in window-function answers.

The gap trap — "previous row" is not "previous week"

LAG(gross) returns the prior row in the ordering, which is only the prior week if weeks are contiguous. If movie 1 has no week-3 row, LAG at week 4 silently reaches back to week 2 and the delta spans two weeks while still being labelled a weekly change. If week numbers can skip, either pre-fill the calendar or compute the gap explicitly with week - LAG(week) OVER (…) and divide. Naming this is the senior detail.

Percent change is usually the better metric

An absolute −1200 from movie 2 and a −300 from movie 1 are not comparable — different budgets, different scales. Analysts usually want the rate of decline:

ROUND(100.0 * (gross - LAG(gross) OVER w)
       / NULLIF(LAG(gross) OVER w, 0), 1) AS pct_change
-- w = (PARTITION BY movie_id ORDER BY week)

The NULLIF(…, 0) guards against a zero-gross prior week, which would otherwise divide by zero. Movie 1 week 2 is then −30%, movie 2 week 2 is −60% — now they are comparable.

LAG vs a self-join

The pre-window-function way is box_office b JOIN box_office p ON b.movie_id = p.movie_id AND p.week = b.week - 1. It works only when weeks are exactly sequential, needs an outer join to keep week 1, and reads worse. LAG is one ordered pass — clearer and faster. Knowing the join exists, and why LAG beats it, is the complete answer.

Senior signal: keep PARTITION BY movie_id so deltas never cross films, recognise the first-week NULL as correct and decide NULL-vs-0 with LAG's default argument, warn that "previous row" only equals "previous week" when weeks are contiguous, and offer percent-change (guarded by NULLIF) as the scale-free metric.
