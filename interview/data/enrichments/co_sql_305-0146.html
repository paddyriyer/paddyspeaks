Adobe's identity team is de-duplicating a customer table: two rows with the same email are almost certainly the same person who signed up twice. The classic tool is a self-join — join the table to itself on the shared key. It is two lines of SQL, but the a.id < b.id condition is doing three jobs at once, and an interviewer asking this wants you to articulate all three.

1. The source data — records

idemailphone
1a@x.com111
2b@x.com222
3a@x.com333
4c@x.com444
5a@x.com555
6b@x.com666

a@x.com appears three times (ids 1, 3, 5) — a triple, not just a pair. b@x.com appears twice (2, 6). c@x.com is unique. The triple is the interesting case: a group of three duplicates produces three pairs, not one.

2. The SQL — self-join on email, a.id < b.id

SELECT a.id, b.id, a.email
FROM records a
JOIN records b ON a.email = b.email AND a.id < b.id;

Two aliases over the same table. The join key is the shared email; the a.id < b.id predicate keeps exactly one ordering of each matching pair.

3. The resulting analysis

ididemail
13a@x.com
15a@x.com
35a@x.com
26b@x.com

Four rows. The a@x.com triple yields the three pairs (1,3), (1,5), (3,5) — that is "3 choose 2". b@x.com yields one pair. c@x.com produces nothing: with only one row it can never satisfy a.id < b.id against itself.

4. Strategic explanations

What a.id < b.id is doing — all three jobs

It is one predicate with three effects. One: it stops a row from matching itself (id 1 against id 1) — = on email would otherwise pair every row with itself. Two: it de-duplicates the pair — without it you would get both (1,3) and (3,5)... rather, both (1,3) and (3,1), the same duplicate reported twice in mirror order. Three: it imposes a canonical orientation, so downstream logic can treat the lower id as the "keep" row and the higher as the "merge". A naive a.id <> b.id fixes only the first job and leaves the mirror duplicates.

The combinatorial blow-up on large duplicate groups

A group of k identical emails produces k(k−1)/2 pair rows. Three duplicates → 3 rows; ten duplicates → 45; a thousand → ~500,000. If the goal is just to find duplicated emails rather than enumerate every pair, GROUP BY is dramatically cheaper and does not explode:

SELECT email, COUNT(*) AS dup_count, MIN(id) AS keep_id
FROM records
GROUP BY email
HAVING COUNT(*) > 1;

This returns one row per duplicated email — a@x.com (3), b@x.com (2) — and scales linearly. Reach for the self-join only when you genuinely need the pairwise mapping.

The NULL-email trap

If two rows both have email = NULL, the join condition a.email = b.email evaluates to unknown, not true — NULL never equals NULL in SQL. So rows with a missing email are silently never flagged as duplicates, even of each other. If a missing email should itself be a data-quality flag, handle it separately; do not assume the self-join caught it. (IS NOT DISTINCT FROM would treat two NULLs as equal — rarely what you want for identity matching.)

Multi-column duplicate definition

"Duplicate" is rarely just email. Often it is email or phone, or normalised name plus date of birth. Extend the join condition deliberately — and beware that OR across columns can both explode the row count and match unintended pairs:

JOIN records b
  ON a.id < b.id
 AND (lower(a.email) = lower(b.email) OR a.phone = b.phone)

Note lower() — Ada@X.com and ada@x.com are the same person; raw equality misses case-variant duplicates. Real de-dup almost always normalises (trim, lowercase, strip phone formatting) before comparing.

From detection to resolution

Finding duplicates is step one; the interviewer often asks "now keep one". The canonical-id idea from the self-join carries over: keep MIN(id) per email, delete the rest. ctid-based or ROW_NUMBER-based deletes are the usual patterns — DELETE FROM records a USING records b WHERE a.email = b.email AND a.id > b.id removes every non-minimal duplicate in one statement.

Senior signal: explain that a.id < b.id simultaneously blocks self-matches, kills mirror duplicates, and sets a canonical orientation; quantify the k(k−1)/2 blow-up and prefer GROUP BY ... HAVING COUNT(*) > 1 for pure detection; flag that NULL emails dodge the join; and normalise (lowercase, trim) before comparing.
