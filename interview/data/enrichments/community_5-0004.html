A Community real-estate marketplace wants its monthly "hot listings" report: which properties drew more than 50 views in October 2024? It is a join, a date filter, and a grouped count — but the interviewer is watching three things: whether your BETWEEN actually captures all of October, whether you count the right column off a LEFT JOIN, and whether the filter belongs in WHERE or HAVING.

1. The source data — property_views (joined to properties)

property_nameviews in Oct 2024note
Sunset Villa60all 60 land in October
Mountain Retreat55all 55 land in October
Beachside Cottage30below 50 — excluded
Lake View Suite0all views are in September

"Lake View Suite" has 70 view rows in the table, but every one is dated mid-September — the October filter zeroes it out. "Beachside Cottage" is genuinely viewed in October but only 30 times, so it must fail the > 50 threshold. Those two rows are the test: one filtered by date, one filtered by count.

2. The SQL — WHERE date-range + HAVING COUNT

SELECT
  p.property_name,
  COUNT(pv.view_id) AS total_views
FROM property_views pv
LEFT JOIN properties p ON pv.property_id = p.property_id
WHERE pv.view_date BETWEEN '2024-10-01' AND '2024-10-31'
GROUP BY p.property_name
HAVING COUNT(pv.view_id) > 50
ORDER BY total_views DESC;

WHERE restricts to October rows before grouping; the grouped COUNT tallies views per property; HAVING keeps only the popular ones; ORDER BY puts the busiest listing on top.

3. The resulting analysis

property_nametotal_views
Sunset Villa60
Mountain Retreat55

Two listings clear 50. "Beachside Cottage" (30 October views) is dropped by HAVING; "Lake View Suite" never enters — its September views are filtered out by WHERE and it has zero rows left to group. A property can vanish either way: too few in-window views, or none at all.

4. Strategic explanations

The BETWEEN boundary trap — DATE vs TIMESTAMP

view_date BETWEEN '2024-10-01' AND '2024-10-31' is safe here because view_date is a pure DATE — 2024-10-31 matches the whole final day. But if the column were a TIMESTAMP, the literal '2024-10-31' means 2024-10-31 00:00:00, and every view logged later that day (10:15, 18:40) would be silently excluded. The robust, type-agnostic form is a half-open range:

WHERE pv.view_date >= '2024-10-01'
  AND pv.view_date <  '2024-11-01'

Half-open (>= start, < next-period-start) needs no knowledge of month length and no fear of time-of-day. It is the senior default for any date-window filter.

Why WHERE here and HAVING there

Two filters, two different stages. The date range is a per-row predicate — a single view either happened in October or it did not — so it belongs in WHERE, and putting it there shrinks the data before the expensive grouping. The > 50 threshold is a predicate on an aggregate that does not exist until rows are grouped, so it must be HAVING. Moving the date filter into HAVING would still give the right answer but scan and group far more rows than necessary; moving the count into WHERE is a syntax error.

The LEFT JOIN + COUNT interaction

Driving from property_views and counting pv.view_id is correct: every counted value is a real view. If the report instead had to list all properties including those with zero October views, you would drive from properties, LEFT JOIN property_views with the date predicate moved into the join's ON clause, and count pv.view_id:

FROM properties p
LEFT JOIN property_views pv
  ON pv.property_id = p.property_id
  AND pv.view_date >= '2024-10-01' AND pv.view_date < '2024-11-01'
GROUP BY p.property_name

The trap: if that date predicate were left in WHERE instead of ON, the unmatched rows (NULL view_date) would fail the WHERE test and the LEFT JOIN would collapse back to an INNER JOIN — zero-view properties would disappear. And COUNT(pv.view_id) rather than COUNT(*) ensures a zero-view property reads as 0, not 1.

Counting views vs counting viewers

COUNT(pv.view_id) counts view events — one obsessed shopper refreshing 60 times scores 60. If "50 views" really means "50 distinct people", that is a different metric and needs a viewer column: COUNT(DISTINCT pv.viewer_id). Always confirm whether the business wants raw traffic or unique reach — bots and refreshers make the two diverge sharply.

Senior signal: prefer a half-open date range (>=/<) over BETWEEN so a future TIMESTAMP column cannot silently drop end-of-day rows, keep per-row filters in WHERE and aggregate filters in HAVING, know that a LEFT JOIN date filter must move to ON to preserve zero-count rows, and ask whether "views" means events or distinct viewers.
