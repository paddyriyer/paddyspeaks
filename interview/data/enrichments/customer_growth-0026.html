A customer-support team has a fixed budget to give white-glove treatment to its highest-velocity customers, and the analytics team must carve the customer base into spend cohorts. The interviewer is probing a specific senior judgement: NTILE(10) versus PERCENT_RANK(). They look identical on a uniform distribution but diverge sharply on a long-tailed one — and which you pick depends on whether the consumer needs equal-sized buckets or a true relative rank.

2. The SQL — NTILE deciles vs PERCENT_RANK continuous rank

WITH params(snapshot) AS (VALUES ('2025-04-01')),
agg AS (
    SELECT customer_id,
        COUNT(*)              AS l90_orders,
        ROUND(SUM(amount), 2) AS l90_spend
    FROM orders
    WHERE order_date >= date((SELECT snapshot FROM params), '-90 days')
      AND order_date <  (SELECT snapshot FROM params)
    GROUP BY customer_id
    HAVING COUNT(*) >= 2
),
ranked AS (
    SELECT customer_id, l90_orders, l90_spend,
        NTILE(10) OVER (ORDER BY l90_spend DESC) AS velocity_decile,
        ROUND(PERCENT_RANK() OVER (ORDER BY l90_spend ASC), 3) AS pct_rank
    FROM agg
)
SELECT customer_id, l90_orders, l90_spend, velocity_decile, pct_rank,
    CASE
        WHEN velocity_decile = 1                            THEN 'top_10'
        WHEN velocity_decile IN (2, 3) AND pct_rank >= 0.75 THEN 'next_15'
        WHEN velocity_decile BETWEEN 3 AND 7                THEN 'mid_50'
        ELSE                                                     'bottom_25'
    END AS cohort
FROM ranked
ORDER BY velocity_decile ASC, l90_spend DESC;

SQLite's date(..., '-90 days') is the dialect form; PostgreSQL writes snapshot - INTERVAL '90 days'. Note the solution computes PERCENT_RANK() over l90_spend ASC (highest spenders near 1.0) and the cohort CASE tests pct_rank >= 0.75 — that is the ascending-order equivalent of "top quartile by spend." The numbers come out correct; it is simply the inverse framing of a descending percentile.

4. Strategic explanations

NTILE vs PERCENT_RANK — the core senior call

NTILE(10) forces the rows into ten near-equal groups: with 18 customers each decile holds 1 or 2, regardless of how spend is distributed. That is exactly what a fixed budget needs — "the top 10% cohort" is always ceil(n/10) people. PERCENT_RANK() instead reports each row's relative position as a continuous (rank - 1)/(n - 1) in [0, 1]. On a long-tailed distribution the two disagree: a whale at $5,700 and the next customer at $3,100 are adjacent deciles but a huge percentile jump. For budget allocation, deciles; for trend and relative comparison, percentile. Showing both lets the consumer pick the framing that matches the decision.

Why PERCENT_RANK always starts at exactly 0

PERCENT_RANK() = (rank - 1) / (total_rows - 1). The first row (rank 1) always yields 0; the last always yields 1. Here customer 18, the lowest qualifying spender, gets pct_rank = 0.000 and customer 1 gets 1.000. That fixed [0,1] span is why percentile is good for cross-period comparison — the endpoints are anchored even as n changes. Contrast CUME_DIST(), which is (rows ≤ current) / total and never returns 0; mixing the two up is a classic interview slip.

The overlapping-CASE-branch subtlety

Decile 3 appears in two branches: velocity_decile IN (2,3) and velocity_decile BETWEEN 3 AND 7. That is not a bug — CASE evaluates top-down and stops at the first match, so a decile-3 customer is tested against the next_15 gate first; only if pct_rank < 0.75 does it fall through to mid_50. The ordering of CASE branches is the tie-break logic. Reordering them would silently reclassify customers — the interviewer may probe whether you noticed the overlap is intentional.

Dialect notes — date math and NTILE remainder distribution

SQLite's date(d, '-90 days') becomes d - INTERVAL '90 days' in PostgreSQL, DATE_SUB(d, INTERVAL 90 DAY) in MySQL, DATE_SUB(d, INTERVAL 90 DAY) in BigQuery. A subtler portability point: when row count is not divisible by the bucket count, NTILE puts the extra rows in the lower-numbered buckets — standard and consistent across PostgreSQL, SQL Server, Oracle, and Snowflake, but worth stating so nobody is surprised that decile 1 has one more customer than decile 10.

What a senior says next

"Deciles are unstable week to week — if three customers churn, everyone's decile shifts even though their spend did not. For a trend dashboard I would rank against a fixed spend threshold rather than a moving population, or report the raw pct_rank so movement reflects real behaviour, not just changes in the denominator."

Senior signal: articulate the NTILE-vs-PERCENT_RANK trade-off (equal buckets for fixed budgets, continuous rank for trends) and recommend exposing both, know that PERCENT_RANK spans exactly [0,1] while CUME_DIST never hits 0, recognise the overlapping decile-3 CASE branches as deliberate top-down tie-breaking, and flag that decile membership is unstable as the population changes.
