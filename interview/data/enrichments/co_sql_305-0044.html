Zoom's finance team wants a list of high-value accounts: every customer whose lifetime spend tops $1,000. The catch is that "total spent" must be computed before it can be filtered — and you cannot filter on an aggregate in a WHERE clause. This question is really about where the aggregation lives: a subquery in the FROM clause (a derived table), and whether you know the equivalent HAVING form.

1. The source data — orders

Customer 101 has two orders that individually stay under $1,000 but together total $1,300. Customer 104 also has two orders, but they sum to only $600. That is the trap: the threshold applies to the per-customer sum, not to any single order.

2. The SQL — derived table in FROM

SELECT customer_id, total_spent
FROM (SELECT customer_id, SUM(amount) AS total_spent FROM orders GROUP BY customer_id) t
WHERE total_spent > 1000;

The inner query aggregates orders into one row per customer with a total_spent. The outer query treats that result as a table t and applies an ordinary WHERE — because by then total_spent is just a column, not an aggregate.

3. The resulting analysis

Customer 101 makes the cut on the strength of two sub-$1,000 orders summing to 1,300 — proof the filter is on the aggregate. Customer 103 clears it with a single $1,200 order. Customers 102 ($400) and 104 ($600) fall short and are gone.

4. Strategic explanations

Why you can't just write WHERE SUM(amount) > 1000

SQL's evaluation order is the reason. WHERE filters individual rows before grouping happens, so an aggregate like SUM doesn't exist yet at WHERE time — the engine rejects it outright. You either filter after grouping (a derived table with WHERE, as here) or use the clause built for post-aggregation filtering: HAVING.

The HAVING equivalent — same result, less nesting

The idiomatic one-query form filters grouped rows directly with HAVING:

SELECT customer_id, SUM(amount) AS total_spent
FROM orders
GROUP BY customer_id
HAVING SUM(amount) > 1000;

Identical output. HAVING runs after GROUP BY, so the aggregate is legal there. Mentioning that the derived table and HAVING are interchangeable — and that HAVING is the cleaner choice when you don't need the subquery for anything else — is the senior beat.

When the derived table genuinely earns its place

HAVING wins for a simple threshold, but the subquery form is essential once you need to reuse or rank the aggregate — for example, filtering on it and ranking by it:

SELECT customer_id, total_spent,
  RANK() OVER (ORDER BY total_spent DESC) AS spend_rank
FROM (SELECT customer_id, SUM(amount) AS total_spent FROM orders GROUP BY customer_id) t
WHERE total_spent > 1000;

You cannot reference a window function in WHERE either, so computing the sum in a derived table and operating on it outside is the general pattern.

The derived table needs an alias

In Postgres a subquery in FROM must be given a name — the t in ) t. Omit it and you get a syntax error: subquery in FROM must have an alias. It's a tiny detail, but a frequent stumble under interview pressure.

Edge case: NULL amounts and the boundary

SUM ignores NULL amount values, and a customer whose orders are all NULL gets a NULL total_spent — which fails > 1000 (the comparison is unknown), so they're correctly excluded. Also confirm the boundary: > 1000 drops a customer at exactly $1,000; if "$1,000 and above" qualifies, it must be >= 1000.

Senior signal: explain that WHERE runs before aggregation so you must filter the sum after grouping, show the HAVING equivalent and call it cleaner for a plain threshold, reserve the derived table for when the aggregate must be reused or ranked, and remember the mandatory subquery alias.
