A property-management platform's finance team needs a single report per landlord showing how many properties they manage, how many active leases exist, and their total and average rental income — including landlords who currently have no payments at all. The interviewer is testing multi-level LEFT JOIN chaining, the COUNT(DISTINCT …) pattern for avoiding over-counting across multiple joined tables, and the correct use of COALESCE to turn NULL aggregates into zeros.

2. The SQL — four-level LEFT JOIN with COUNT DISTINCT and COALESCE

SELECT l.landlord_id, l.name,
       COUNT(DISTINCT p.property_id)    AS properties,
       COUNT(DISTINCT le.lease_id)      AS active_leases,
       COALESCE(SUM(pay.amount), 0)     AS total_income,
       COALESCE(AVG(pay.amount), 0)     AS avg_payment
FROM landlords  l
LEFT JOIN properties p   ON l.landlord_id = p.landlord_id
LEFT JOIN leases    le   ON p.property_id  = le.property_id
LEFT JOIN payments  pay  ON le.lease_id    = pay.lease_id
GROUP BY l.landlord_id, l.name
ORDER BY total_income DESC;

4. Strategic explanations

Why every JOIN must be LEFT JOIN

Inner joins would silently drop Clara (no properties) and would drop any landlord whose properties have no leases, or whose leases have no payments. The LEFT JOIN chain preserves all landlords through every level. This is the key insight: when you want "all rows from the anchor table, with aggregates from the right side defaulting to zero," every join in the chain must be a LEFT JOIN — not just the first one.

COUNT(DISTINCT …) vs plain COUNT — why it matters

Without DISTINCT, joining multiple payments per lease causes each payment row to duplicate the property and lease IDs. A plain COUNT(p.property_id) for Aria would return 4 (one per payment row), not 2. COUNT(DISTINCT p.property_id) collapses the duplicates introduced by the downstream join fan-out. This is one of the most common silent correctness bugs in multi-join aggregation queries — and spotting it is a reliable senior differentiator.

NULL propagation and COALESCE

SUM over zero rows returns NULL, not 0. AVG also returns NULL. COALESCE(SUM(pay.amount), 0) replaces that NULL with 0 for landlords with no payment history. Some teams prefer ISNULL (SQL Server / Snowflake alternative) or NVL (Oracle), but COALESCE is ANSI SQL and portable everywhere. The COUNT(DISTINCT …) functions return 0 for NULL inputs natively — they do not need wrapping.

Join fan-out arithmetic

Before aggregation, the raw join produces one row per (landlord, property, lease, payment) tuple. For Aria: 2 properties × ~2 leases each × ~2 payments each = up to 4 payment rows. The GROUP BY collapses them back; DISTINCT handles the inflated counts. Whenever you see more than one "to-many" relationship in a join chain, always ask: "will this inflate my aggregates?"

Performance in Snowflake

In Snowflake this pattern runs efficiently because the GROUP BY is pushed into the scan layer and the DISTINCT bitmaps are built during aggregation. For very large payment tables, a pre-aggregated CTE per level (first aggregate payments to lease-level totals, then join) can reduce the join cardinality before the GROUP BY step. This is a valid optimization to mention if the interviewer asks about scale.

Dialect portability

COALESCE, COUNT(DISTINCT), SUM, AVG, and LEFT JOIN are all ANSI SQL — identical on Snowflake, PostgreSQL, BigQuery, and MySQL. BigQuery's variant of this pattern sometimes uses COUNTIF or APPROX_COUNT_DISTINCT for performance at scale, trading exactness for speed.

Senior signal: immediately spot that every join in the chain must be LEFT JOIN (not just the first), explain why COUNT(DISTINCT) is mandatory once there are multiple "to-many" hops (plain COUNT inflates due to join fan-out), and know that SUM/AVG over zero rows return NULL — not 0 — so COALESCE is required for clean reporting.
