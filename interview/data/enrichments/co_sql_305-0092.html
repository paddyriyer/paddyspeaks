Meta's growth team runs a churn-prevention loop: any creator who has gone quiet for too long gets a re-engagement nudge. The PM hands you a one-liner — "find users who haven't posted in over 30 days" — and it sounds trivial. The interview lives in three quiet decisions: what counts as "now", whether the boundary is inclusive, and what to do with someone who has never posted at all.

1. The source data — posts

With CURRENT_DATE at 2026-05-15, user 3's most recent post is exactly 31 days old — sitting one day past the threshold. That boundary row is the whole interview: it proves whether your comparison is > or >=.

2. The SQL — HAVING on a date difference

SELECT user_id, MAX(posted_at::date) AS last_post,
  CURRENT_DATE - MAX(posted_at::date) AS days_silent
FROM posts GROUP BY user_id HAVING CURRENT_DATE - MAX(posted_at::date) > 30;

Per user, take the latest post date, subtract it from today to get an integer day count, then keep only groups whose gap exceeds 30. HAVING filters after the aggregate, because the condition depends on MAX.

3. The resulting analysis

Users 1 (3 days) and 4 (1 day) drop out — both posted this week. User 3 survives at exactly 31 days: strict > 30 includes day 31. Had the spec meant "at least 30 days", you would write >= 30 and the answer set could change. Notice days_silent is computed from MAX(posted_at::date), the same expression as in HAVING — SQL has no way to reuse the SELECT alias inside HAVING.

4. Strategic explanations

The user who never posted is invisible — and that may be the real churn risk

posts only contains users who have posted. A creator who signed up and never published has zero rows, so GROUP BY produces no group for them and they can never appear. For a re-engagement campaign that is backwards — the never-posted user is the most disengaged of all. If the audience is "all users", start from the users table and LEFT JOIN:

SELECT u.user_id,
       CURRENT_DATE - MAX(p.posted_at::date) AS days_silent
FROM users u
LEFT JOIN posts p ON p.user_id = u.user_id
GROUP BY u.user_id
HAVING MAX(p.posted_at::date) IS NULL
    OR CURRENT_DATE - MAX(p.posted_at::date) > 30;

Naming this gap unprompted is the senior move — the query as written silently scopes the campaign to people who already engage.

CURRENT_DATE vs NOW() — midnight versus this instant

CURRENT_DATE is today at 00:00; NOW() / CURRENT_TIMESTAMP is the exact moment, with hours and microseconds. Subtracting two date values yields a clean integer day count, which is why the solution casts with ::date. If you instead did NOW() - MAX(posted_at) you get an interval, and "> 30" no longer type-checks — you would compare against INTERVAL '30 days'. Casting to date first keeps the arithmetic in plain integers and makes "30 days" unambiguous.

Why HAVING and not WHERE

WHERE runs before grouping, on individual rows; HAVING runs after, on aggregates. The condition here references MAX(posted_at), which does not exist until the group is formed — so it must be HAVING. A frequent mistake is filtering WHERE posted_at < CURRENT_DATE - 30, which throws away recent posts before the MAX is taken and can mislabel an active user as silent.

-- WRONG: drops user 1's recent posts, then MAX sees only old ones
SELECT user_id FROM posts
WHERE posted_at::date < CURRENT_DATE - 30
GROUP BY user_id;
Reproducibility — freezing "today"

CURRENT_DATE makes the query non-deterministic: rerun it tomorrow and the answer shifts. For a batch job that must be auditable, parameterise the reference date (WHERE … :as_of_date) so a backfill for last Tuesday gives last Tuesday's answer. Interviewers like to hear that a "live" query and a "reproducible report" are different requirements.

Senior signal: use HAVING because the filter depends on MAX, recognise that ::date arithmetic yields integer days while NOW() yields an interval, pin down whether the 30-day boundary is inclusive, and flag that users with zero posts never appear — the most churned cohort is missing unless you LEFT JOIN from the users table.
