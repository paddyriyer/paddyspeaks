A retailer wants to identify customers with a broad basket — those who have bought more than one distinct product, not just placed more than one order. The interviewer is probing one precise distinction: filtering on COUNT(DISTINCT product_id) versus COUNT(*). The trap customer is the loyal repeat buyer who ordered the same single product several times — they have many rows but only one product, and must be excluded.

Customer 200 is the edge case: two orders of product 30 — many rows, one product. Customer 100 bought product 10 twice and product 20 once (two distinct products). Customer 300 ordered exactly once.

2. The SQL — GROUP BY with a HAVING COUNT(DISTINCT ...) filter

SELECT customer_id
FROM orders_tab
GROUP BY customer_id
HAVING COUNT(DISTINCT product_id) > 1;

Customers 100 and 400 are returned. Customer 200 is correctly excluded — two orders but a single distinct product. Customer 300 is excluded for ordering once. COUNT(*) would have wrongly admitted customer 200.

4. Strategic explanations

Why COUNT(DISTINCT product_id), not COUNT(*)

COUNT(*) counts rows in the group — for customer 200 that is 2, which would pass a > 1 test even though they only ever bought one product. COUNT(DISTINCT product_id) counts unique product ids, so customer 200 scores 1 and is excluded. The question says "more than one distinct product"; the word distinct is the entire spec, and DISTINCT inside the aggregate is the literal translation. Misreading it as "more than one order" is the single most common wrong answer.

Why the filter belongs in HAVING, not WHERE

WHERE filters individual rows before grouping — it has no access to a per-group aggregate like COUNT(DISTINCT ...). HAVING filters groups after aggregation, so it can test the count. Putting an aggregate in WHERE is a syntax error in every SQL dialect; the row-filter / group-filter split is fundamental and an interviewer expects you to articulate it.

NULL handling — COUNT(DISTINCT) skips NULLs

If a product_id is NULL (a malformed order), COUNT(DISTINCT product_id) ignores it — NULL is never counted and never treated as a distinct value, even across multiple NULL rows. A customer with one real product plus several NULL-product orders scores 1 and is excluded. That is usually the right behaviour, but state it: if a NULL product should count, you would need COUNT(DISTINCT COALESCE(product_id, -1)) or a separate clause.

Returning more than the id — the natural follow-up

Interviewers often extend this to "show the basket size too". Because the aggregate is already computed, just project it:

SELECT customer_id, COUNT(DISTINCT product_id) AS distinct_products
FROM orders_tab
GROUP BY customer_id
HAVING COUNT(DISTINCT product_id) > 1
ORDER BY distinct_products DESC;

You may repeat the expression in HAVING — standard SQL does not let HAVING reference a SELECT alias (Postgres permits it as an extension; MySQL does too, but portable code repeats it).

Performance — COUNT(DISTINCT) is not free

COUNT(DISTINCT) must deduplicate within each group, which typically needs a sort or hash per customer — heavier than a plain COUNT(*). On a large orders table an index on (customer_id, product_id) lets the planner stream the group and dedupe cheaply. It is the correct query regardless; just know the distinct count carries a cost a plain count does not.

Senior signal: pin the answer on the word "distinct" — COUNT(DISTINCT product_id) > 1, not COUNT(*) — so a customer who reorders one product many times is correctly excluded. Explaining the WHERE-vs-HAVING split and that COUNT(DISTINCT) silently skips NULL product ids rounds out the senior answer.
