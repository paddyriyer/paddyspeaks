Target's pricing engine recomputes shelf prices nightly. Most products carry their base price; a subset are on promotion and need a percentage knocked off. The query is a LEFT JOIN with an arithmetic adjustment — but an interviewer asking this is watching for two things: that you use a LEFT JOIN so non-promoted products aren't dropped, and that you handle the NULL discount_pct the join produces for them.

Only products 1 and 3 have a promotion row. Products 2 and 4 have no match in promotions — they are the edge case. They must still appear in the result, at their full base price.

2. The SQL — LEFT JOIN + COALESCE

SELECT p.product_id, p.base_price,
  p.base_price * (1 - COALESCE(pr.discount_pct,0)/100.0) AS adjusted_price
FROM products p LEFT JOIN promotions pr ON p.product_id = pr.product_id;

The LEFT JOIN keeps every product even with no promotion; for those, pr.discount_pct arrives as NULL. COALESCE(..., 0) turns that NULL into a 0% discount, and /100.0 forces decimal (not integer) division.

Product 1: 100 × (1 − 0.20) = 80. Product 3: 200 × (1 − 0.50) = 100. Products 2 and 4 have no promotion, so COALESCE supplies 0 and they keep their base price — 50 and 80. All four products are present; that is the proof the LEFT JOIN did its job.

4. Strategic explanations

Why LEFT JOIN and not INNER JOIN — the core trap

Swap in an INNER JOIN and products 2 and 4 vanish — only the two promoted products survive. A pricing feed missing two of its four products is a production incident. Whenever the task is "compute X for every row, applying an optional Y", the optional table goes on the right of a LEFT JOIN. INNER JOIN says "only rows that have a match", which is the opposite of what's wanted.

The NULL the LEFT JOIN manufactures

Keeping the unmatched rows means their promotions columns are NULL. Without the COALESCE, the arithmetic becomes base_price * (1 - NULL/100.0), and NULL propagates — products 2 and 4 would show a NULL adjusted_price. The LEFT JOIN and the COALESCE are a pair: one keeps the row, the other repairs the gap it leaves.

The integer-division trap

/100.0 is deliberate. If the literal were /100 and discount_pct were an INTEGER, some engines do integer division: 20/100 becomes 0, every adjusted price equals the base price, and the bug is silent. Forcing one operand to be decimal (100.0, or a ::numeric cast) guarantees real division.

Duplicate promotions — fan-out risk

The join assumes at most one promotion per product. If promotions can hold two rows for the same product (overlapping campaigns), the LEFT JOIN fans out — that product appears twice with two different prices. Defend against it by deduplicating to one discount first, e.g. the deepest:

SELECT p.product_id, p.base_price,
  p.base_price * (1 - COALESCE(pr.discount_pct,0)/100.0) AS adjusted_price
FROM products p
LEFT JOIN (
  SELECT product_id, MAX(discount_pct) AS discount_pct
  FROM promotions GROUP BY product_id
) pr ON p.product_id = pr.product_id;
Edge case: bad discount values

Nothing here stops a discount_pct of 120 (negative price) or −10 (a price increase). If the data isn't trusted, clamp it: LEAST(GREATEST(COALESCE(pr.discount_pct,0),0),100). Mentioning this shows you think about data quality, not just the happy path.

Senior signal: choose LEFT JOIN so unpromoted products survive, pair it with COALESCE to neutralise the NULL discount the join creates, force decimal division with 100.0 to dodge integer truncation, and flag the fan-out risk if a product can have multiple promotion rows.
