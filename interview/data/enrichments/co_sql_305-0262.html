Airbnb's travel-experiences team monitors flight reliability across routes its partners sell. Operations wants two numbers per route: the average delay, and a count of "late" flights past a threshold. The query is a textbook GROUP BY with a conditional aggregate, but the interview hides a real subtlety — the average and the late-count tell different stories, and one of them can mislead.

1. The source data — flights

flight_idroutedelay_minutes
1SFO-JFK45
2SFO-JFK10
3SFO-JFK60
4LAX-ORD5
5LAX-ORD0
6SEA-DEN35

SEA-DEN is a single-flight route — an aggregate over one row. SFO-JFK has a wide spread (10 to 60). And the threshold is 30 minutes: a 30-minute flight would be a boundary case, though none sits exactly there.

2. The SQL — AVG + conditional SUM per route

SELECT route, AVG(delay_minutes) AS avg_delay,
  SUM(CASE WHEN delay_minutes > 30 THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) AS late_flights
FROM flights GROUP BY route;

AVG(delay_minutes) averages every flight on the route. The SUM(CASE …) is a counting trick: each flight contributes 1 if its delay exceeds 30, else 0, so the sum is the count of late flights.

SFO-JFK: mean 38.3 min, 2 of 3 flights late. SEA-DEN: a single flight at 35 min — mean equals that one value, and it counts as 1 late. LAX-ORD: mean 2.5 min, 0 late. Rows came back in no particular order — no ORDER BY.

4. Strategic explanations

Why both metrics — the average hides the distribution

This is the senior insight. SFO-JFK's mean of 38 minutes looks like "typically a bit late". But the late-count says 2 of 3 flights blew past 30 minutes — the route is unreliable, not mildly tardy. One badly delayed flight can also drag a mean up while most flights were fine (the reverse failure). Reporting AVG alone would mislead ops; the late-count is the reliability signal. A senior answer might add a percentile:

PERCENTILE_CONT(0.9) WITHIN GROUP (ORDER BY delay_minutes) AS p90_delay
SUM(CASE…) vs COUNT(CASE…) vs FILTER — the trap

The conditional-count idiom has a notorious foot-gun. These are equivalent:

SUM(CASE WHEN delay_minutes > 30 THEN 1 ELSE 0 END)        -- correct
COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE delay_minutes > 30)                  -- correct, Postgres
COUNT(CASE WHEN delay_minutes > 30 THEN 1 END)              -- correct (NULL else)

But COUNT(CASE WHEN delay_minutes > 30 THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) is wrong — COUNT counts non-NULL values, and 0 is non-NULL, so it counts every row. With COUNT the ELSE must yield NULL (or be omitted). The SUM(… ELSE 0) form in the solution sidesteps this entirely; FILTER is the cleanest modern equivalent.

The threshold boundary — > vs >=

The condition is delay_minutes > 30, so a flight delayed exactly 30 minutes is not late. If the SLA says "30 minutes or more counts as late", it must be >= 30. None of the demo flights sits exactly at 30, so the choice is invisible here — which is precisely why you must confirm the SLA definition rather than guess.

The single-flight route — small-sample noise

SEA-DEN has one flight. Its avg_delay of 35 is not a trend, it is a single observation, and the late-count of 1 means "100% late" on a sample of one. Aggregates over tiny groups are statistically fragile. Consider a HAVING COUNT(*) >= n floor, or report COUNT(*) alongside so the consumer can see how much each average is worth.

Edge cases: NULL delays and missing routes

AVG skips NULL delay_minutes (a cancelled flight with no recorded delay), so the average covers only flights that actually flew — defensible, but say so. In the CASE, a NULL delay fails > 30 and lands in ELSE 0, counted as not-late. And a route with zero flights produces no row at all — GROUP BY cannot invent groups that have no rows. Add ORDER BY late_flights DESC to surface the worst routes first.

Senior signal: explain why the average and the late-count must be read together — a moderate mean can hide a route where most flights are late — use SUM(CASE…ELSE 0) or FILTER and know that COUNT(CASE…ELSE 0) silently counts everything, confirm the > vs >= SLA boundary, and treat single-flight routes as noise rather than signal.
