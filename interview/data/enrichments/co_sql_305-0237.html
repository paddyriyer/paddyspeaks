Google's education-products team measures course stickiness with a re-enrollment rate: of all students who ever enrolled, what fraction came back and enrolled again in a later term. The reference solution self-joins enrollments to itself on a later term and divides distinct counts. It looks reasonable — but the join quietly poisons the denominator, and the rate it reports is mathematically pinned at 100%.

Four distinct students. Students 1 and 3 re-enrolled in a later term; students 2 and 4 each appear in only one term and never came back. The correct re-enrollment rate is 2 of 4 = 50%. Hold that number.

2. The SQL — self-join, distinct-count ratio

The reference solution:

SELECT 100.0 * COUNT(DISTINCT b.student_id) / NULLIF(COUNT(DISTINCT a.student_id), 0) AS reenroll_rate
FROM enrollments a
JOIN enrollments b ON a.student_id = b.student_id AND b.term > a.term;

This is broken. The INNER JOIN only emits rows for students who have a later-term enrollment — students 2 and 4 produce no join rows at all. So a.student_id and b.student_id range over exactly the same set: the re-enrollers. Numerator and denominator are identical, and the rate is always 100% regardless of the data. The denominator must be all students, which means the non-re-enrollers have to survive the join. Use a LEFT JOIN:

SELECT 100.0 * COUNT(DISTINCT CASE WHEN later.student_id IS NOT NULL
                                   THEN e.student_id END)
            / NULLIF(COUNT(DISTINCT e.student_id), 0) AS reenroll_rate
FROM enrollments e
LEFT JOIN enrollments later
  ON e.student_id = later.student_id AND later.term > e.term;

Now every student appears via e (the denominator), and only those with a matched later term are counted in the numerator.

50% — matching the hand-count of 2 re-enrollers out of 4 students. Running the original solution against this same data returns 100, which is the giveaway: a metric that cannot move is not measuring anything.

4. Strategic explanations

Why the inner join breaks the denominator

The mental model "a = everyone, b = the comebacks" is wrong because the join condition b.term > a.term filters both aliases simultaneously — a row of a only survives if a partner row of b exists for it. A student with one term has no such partner, so they are absent from a too. Whenever a ratio's denominator is "the whole population", the population side must not be gated by an inner join.

The cleaner fix — aggregate per student first

A self-join is overkill. Counting distinct terms per student is more direct and obviously correct:

SELECT 100.0 * COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE terms > 1)
            / NULLIF(COUNT(*), 0) AS reenroll_rate
FROM (
  SELECT student_id, COUNT(DISTINCT term) AS terms
  FROM enrollments
  GROUP BY student_id
) per_student;

Each student becomes one row; terms > 1 means they enrolled in more than one term. No fan-out, no join, and the denominator is unambiguously every student. This is the version to reach for.

Define "re-enrollment" precisely

Does it mean "enrolled in any later term" (what the solution attempts) or "enrolled in the same course again"? The two give different numbers. If it is same-course retention, the join needs a.course_id = b.course_id as well, and the per-student version groups by (student_id, course_id). Pin the definition before writing SQL — this ambiguity is exactly what the interviewer is testing.

The term-ordering trap

b.term > a.term compares term as text. With values like '2025-S1', '2025-S2' lexical order happens to match chronological order — but '2025-S10' would sort before '2025-S2', and a 'Fall'/'Spring' scheme would sort alphabetically, not seasonally. For reliable comparison, terms need a real ordinal — a numeric sort key or a proper date. And the same-day case: if a student has two rows in the same term, > (strict) correctly does not count that as re-enrollment, but >= would — a subtle off-by-one.

Senior signal: spot that an inner self-join gates the denominator to only the re-enrollers, pinning the rate at 100%; fix it with a LEFT JOIN or, better, a per-student term count; nail down whether "re-enroll" means any later term or the same course; and never trust lexical ordering of term strings as a chronology.
