Samsung's e-commerce team launched a paid loyalty membership and the growth org wants the one number that justifies it: do loyalty members spend more per order than everyone else? The query is a single GROUP BY — but it quietly answers a narrower question than the business asked, and an interviewer is watching whether you notice the gap.

Four customers, six orders. Customer 1 (loyalty) placed two orders; the others one each. Notice the unit: the question is per-order, and customer 1 contributes twice to the loyalty bucket — orders, not customers, are being averaged.

2. The SQL — AVG grouped by flag

SELECT c.is_loyalty, AVG(o.amount) AS avg_order, COUNT(*) AS n
FROM customers c JOIN orders o ON c.customer_id = o.customer_id
GROUP BY c.is_loyalty;

An inner join attaches each order to its customer's loyalty flag, then GROUP BY is_loyalty splits orders into two buckets and averages each.

3. The resulting analysis

Loyalty members average 300 per order against 123.33 for non-members — a headline the growth team will love. But each bucket has only 3 orders, and the loyalty figure is dominated by one customer's two orders. The n column is doing essential work: it tells the reader the sample is too thin to bank on.

4. Strategic explanations

The inner join silently drops zero-order customers

Customers in the table with no orders never appear — the JOIN has nothing to match. That is correct for "average order value" (no orders means no order amounts to average), but it means this query cannot answer "what fraction of loyalty members ever order". If the follow-up asks about activation or conversion, you need a LEFT JOIN and to count customers, not orders.

Per-order average vs per-customer average

AVG(o.amount) averages every order equally, so a customer with ten orders sways the bucket ten times as much as a one-order customer. That is "average order value". A different, equally reasonable metric is average customer spend — total each customer first, then average the customers:

SELECT is_loyalty, AVG(customer_total) AS avg_customer_spend
FROM (
  SELECT c.is_loyalty, c.customer_id, SUM(o.amount) AS customer_total
  FROM customers c JOIN orders o ON c.customer_id = o.customer_id
  GROUP BY c.is_loyalty, c.customer_id
) t
GROUP BY is_loyalty;

These two numbers differ whenever order counts vary across customers. Naming which one the business wants — per order or per member — is the senior move.

The NULL three-way split in is_loyalty

is_loyalty is a boolean, and booleans have three states: true, false, and NULL (membership unknown). GROUP BY puts NULLs in their own group, so the result could surface a mysterious third row with no flag. Decide on purpose: either report it, or normalise with COALESCE(c.is_loyalty, false), or filter WHERE c.is_loyalty IS NOT NULL. Silently shipping a NULL row to a dashboard invites a "what is this?" Slack message.

Correlation is not causation — the analyst's caveat

The number says members spend more; it does not say loyalty causes spend. High spenders are exactly the customers who opt into a paid program — classic selection bias. An over-the-bar answer delivers the SQL and the caveat: "this is a correlation; to claim impact you need a pre/post comparison or a holdout group."

Filtering to completed orders

orders carries a status. Cancelled or pending orders sitting in the average distort it. If "order value" means realised revenue, add WHERE o.status = 'completed' — and confirm that definition before writing the query.

Senior signal: recognise this averages orders not customers and offer the per-customer alternative, handle the boolean's three-valued NULL group explicitly, note that the inner join excludes zero-order customers, and pair the number with the selection-bias caveat that correlation is not impact.
