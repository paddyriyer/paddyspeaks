Google's people-analytics team needs a quick roster of everyone in Engineering. It looks trivial — join employees to departments, filter on the name — but the interviewer is probing join hygiene: do you filter on the department name rather than hard-coding a fragile dept_id, do you know what happens to employees with no department, and do you understand why this particular filter behaves the same in WHERE as in ON.

Two employees sit in Engineering. The interesting row is Dev — an intern with a NULL dept_id, unassigned to any department. How the join treats that row is the heart of the question.

2. The SQL — INNER JOIN + name filter

SELECT e.emp_id, d.dept_name FROM employees e JOIN departments d ON e.dept_id = d.dept_id
WHERE d.dept_name = 'Engineering';

An inner join on dept_id pairs each employee with their department row, then the WHERE keeps only the Engineering pairs.

4. Strategic explanations

Filter on the name, not a magic number

A tempting "simpler" answer is WHERE e.dept_id = 1 — skip the join entirely. It returns the same rows today, but it hard-codes a surrogate key. If Engineering is re-keyed, merged, or the seed data differs across environments, the query silently returns the wrong team. Joining to departments and filtering on dept_name is self-documenting and survives re-keying. The interviewer wants the robust version.

ON vs WHERE — identical here, not in general

You could write JOIN departments d ON e.dept_id = d.dept_id AND d.dept_name = 'Engineering' with no WHERE. For an inner join the two are equivalent — both rows that survive must match every predicate. The distinction bites only with outer joins: a predicate in ON filters the matched side while preserving unmatched rows, whereas the same predicate in WHERE runs after the join and discards the NULL-extended rows. Knowing that this case is safe — and why — is the senior detail.

"Include unassigned employees" — the LEFT JOIN follow-up

If the ask becomes "list everyone, and show their department even if unassigned," switch to a left join — but then the filter must move into ON or you re-drop the NULLs:

SELECT e.emp_id, d.dept_name
FROM employees e
LEFT JOIN departments d ON e.dept_id = d.dept_id
WHERE d.dept_name = 'Engineering' OR e.dept_id IS NULL;

Putting d.dept_name = 'Engineering' in a plain WHERE after a LEFT JOIN quietly converts it back into an inner join — a classic interview trap.

Case sensitivity and trailing whitespace

= 'Engineering' is exact and case-sensitive in Postgres. Seed data with 'engineering', 'Engineering ', or 'ENGINEERING' would return zero rows with no error. Defensive form: WHERE lower(trim(d.dept_name)) = 'engineering' — though that also forfeits any index on dept_name, so prefer clean data plus a check constraint.

Duplicate department names

If departments has two rows both named "Engineering" (multi-region, no uniqueness constraint), the join multiplies: each engineer would appear once per matching department row. Confirm dept_name is unique, or aggregate with DISTINCT / disambiguate by location.

Senior signal: join and filter on the human-readable dept_name instead of hard-coding a surrogate dept_id, explain that the NULL dept_id intern is dropped by the inner join's equality, and know that the name predicate is interchangeable between ON and WHERE for inner joins but not for the left-join variant.
