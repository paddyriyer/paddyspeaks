A ride-hailing operations team wants to know whether peak-hour demand is genuinely different from the rest of the day — rush-hour rides come thick and fast but at lower fares, off-peak rides are sparse but often longer and pricier. The interviewer is probing whether you can derive a categorical bucket from a raw timestamp and then aggregate over it in one pass, without an external lookup table.

Rows 5 and 7 sit on the BETWEEN boundaries (hour 19 and hour 7) — they decide whether the window endpoints are inclusive.

2. The SQL — deriving a bucket with CASE, then aggregating

WITH peak_or_non_peak AS (
  SELECT *,
    EXTRACT(HOUR FROM ride_date::timestamp) AS hr,
    CASE WHEN EXTRACT(HOUR FROM ride_date::timestamp) BETWEEN 7 AND 10
           OR EXTRACT(HOUR FROM ride_date::timestamp) BETWEEN 17 AND 20
         THEN 'peak_hour'
         ELSE 'non_peak_hour' END AS time_period
  FROM rides
)
SELECT time_period,
       COUNT(ride_id) AS ride_count,
       AVG(ride_fare) AS average_fare
FROM peak_or_non_peak
GROUP BY time_period;

4. Strategic explanations

Why the CTE is just readability, not logic

The CASE expression could be inlined into the outer GROUP BY, but you cannot GROUP BY a column alias in standard SQL — you would have to repeat the whole CASE in both the SELECT and the GROUP BY. The CTE names the derived bucket once so the outer query stays clean. It carries no performance cost: the planner inlines a non-recursive CTE like this.

BETWEEN is inclusive — state your boundary intent

BETWEEN 7 AND 10 means hours 7, 8, 9, and 10 — four hours. A common interview slip is assuming it is half-open. If the business defines the morning peak as 07:00–09:59, you want hr >= 7 AND hr < 10 instead. Say which convention you are using; the boundary rows in the demo exist precisely to expose this.

The unmatched-bucket trap — ELSE as a catch-all

Here ELSE 'non_peak_hour' guarantees every row lands in a bucket, so COUNT over the two groups always sums to the table total. Drop the ELSE and any unmatched row would get a NULL time_period — which GROUP BY collects into its own silent NULL group. An explicit ELSE is the defensive habit.

COUNT(ride_id) vs COUNT(*) and NULL fares

COUNT(ride_id) skips rows where ride_id is NULL; since it is a key that never happens, it is equivalent to COUNT(*) here — but COUNT(*) states the intent ("count rows") more honestly. More importantly, AVG(ride_fare) ignores NULL fares: a free or voided ride would be excluded from the average's denominator, not treated as zero. If the business wants voided rides counted as $0, use AVG(COALESCE(ride_fare, 0)).

What to say next — bucket boundaries as parameters

Hard-coded hour ranges age badly. A senior answer mentions lifting the peak windows into a small time_band reference table joined on hour, so operations can re-tune rush hours per city without a code deploy. That also generalises cleanly to more than two bands (early-morning, peak, midday, evening-peak, late-night).

Senior signal: derive the categorical bucket with one CASE, name it in a CTE to avoid repeating it in GROUP BY, call out that BETWEEN is inclusive on both endpoints, and note that AVG silently drops NULL fares. Offer a reference-table refactor so the peak windows are data, not hard-coded SQL.
