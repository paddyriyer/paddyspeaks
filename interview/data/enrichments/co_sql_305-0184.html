TikTok Shop's quality team wants an early-warning list: products whose cancellation rate runs above 5%. A high cancel rate signals a listing problem — wrong sizing, bad photos, stockouts. The query is a ratio inside a GROUP BY with a HAVING filter, and the interviewer is watching for the two classic ratio bugs: integer division, and repeating the whole expression in HAVING.

1. The source data — orders

1completed110
1cancelled211
1completed312
1completed413
2cancelled514
2cancelled615
3completed716

Product 1 has 1 cancelled of 4 → 25%. Product 2 is 2 of 2 → 100%. Product 3 has a single order, not cancelled → 0%. Product 3 is the trap: with only one order it can never produce a fractional rate, and a 5% threshold on a one-order sample is statistically meaningless.

2. The SQL — ratio + HAVING

SELECT product_id,
  100.0*SUM(CASE WHEN status='cancelled' THEN 1 ELSE 0 END)/COUNT(*) AS cancel_rate
FROM orders GROUP BY product_id HAVING 100.0*SUM(CASE WHEN status='cancelled' THEN 1 ELSE 0 END)/COUNT(*) > 5;

Cancelled orders are counted with a conditional SUM, divided by the total order count, scaled to a percentage. The HAVING clause repeats that whole expression to keep only products above 5%.

3. The resulting analysis

product_idcancel_rate
2100.0000000000000000
125.0000000000000000

Products 1 (25%) and 2 (100%) clear the bar; product 3 (0%) is filtered out. Two things to flag: the row order is not guaranteed (no ORDER BY), and product 2's 100% comes from a 2-order sample — technically "above 5%" but not a trustworthy signal.

4. Strategic explanations

The 100.0 is load-bearing — the integer-division trap

SUM(CASE…) and COUNT(*) are both integers. In Postgres, integer ÷ integer does integer division and truncates: 1 / 4 is 0, not 0.25. Leading with the literal 100.0 (a numeric) forces the whole expression into numeric arithmetic, so the division keeps its fractional part. Drop the .0 — write 100 * SUM(…) / COUNT(*) — and product 1 reports 25 only by luck of operator order; SUM(…) / COUNT(*) * 100 would report 0. Always cast or use a numeric literal before the divide.

The repeated expression — why HAVING cannot see the alias

HAVING is evaluated before the SELECT list's aliases exist, so HAVING cancel_rate > 5 fails with "column does not exist" in Postgres. That is why the solution copy-pastes the entire ratio. It works but is brittle — change the formula once and you must change it twice. The clean fix is to compute it once in a subquery (or CTE) and filter the alias outside:

SELECT product_id, cancel_rate FROM (
  SELECT product_id,
    100.0 * SUM(CASE WHEN status='cancelled' THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) / COUNT(*) AS cancel_rate
  FROM orders GROUP BY product_id
) t
WHERE cancel_rate > 5
ORDER BY cancel_rate DESC;

One source of truth for the formula, and you get a free ORDER BY.

The small-sample problem — the senior follow-up

Product 2's 100% rests on two orders. A rate built on a tiny denominator is noise, not signal — any list of "problem products" sorted purely by rate will be topped by one-order flukes. A senior answer adds a volume floor:

... GROUP BY product_id
HAVING COUNT(*) >= 20
   AND 100.0 * SUM(CASE WHEN status='cancelled' THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) / COUNT(*) > 5

Naming the minimum-sample requirement — "5% of what?" — is what separates a metric from a real alert.

What counts as a cancellation? — the status taxonomy

The query treats only 'cancelled' as a cancellation. Real order tables also carry 'refunded', 'returned', 'failed', 'rejected'. And the denominator COUNT(*) includes pending orders — should an order still in flight count against the product? Pin the definition down: which statuses are cancellations, and is the denominator "all orders" or "all resolved orders"?

Edge case: a product with no orders

A product that never sold has no rows in orders, so GROUP BY never emits it — correct, it has no cancellation rate. There is no divide-by-zero risk here, because every group GROUP BY produces has COUNT(*) >= 1. (Divide-by-zero would only appear if the denominator were a conditional SUM that could be 0 — then you would need NULLIF(denominator, 0).)

Senior signal: explain that the 100.0 literal is what dodges integer-division truncation, recognise the repeated HAVING expression as a consequence of aliases not being visible there and refactor it into a subquery, and add a minimum-order-count floor so a 100% rate on two orders does not masquerade as a real quality alert.
