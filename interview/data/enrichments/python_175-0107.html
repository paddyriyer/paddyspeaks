Cloudflare's autoscaler doesn't care what day of the week it is — it cares whether the underlying traffic trend is up or down. The weekly seasonality (weekend dip on B2B sites, weekend spike on consumer sites) is loud, predictable, and noise from the trend's perspective. Deseasonalising lets the autoscaler provision against the slow-moving truth instead of yo-yoing with the day-of-week. The same algorithm shape powers retail demand forecasts (Walmart, Target), restaurant cover predictions (OpenTable), and any time-series where "is this a real change or just Tuesday?" is the question.

This is a synthetic seed because real Cloudflare data isn't shareable — but the signal-shape is verbatim what their public traffic dashboards show. The decomposition has to recover trend + seasonal + residual such that original ≈ trend + seasonal + residual (additive form).

2. The business question — what the autoscaler decides on

  • Capacity provisioning. The autoscaler reads the deseasoned curve, projects it forward 7 days, provisions to p95(forecast) × 1.3 headroom. Reading the raw curve would over-provision Mondays and under-provision Sundays.
  • Anomaly detection. An outage that drops Monday traffic by 30 % is invisible if you compare to the Monday-mean (looks normal). On the deseasoned curve, the same drop is a 30 % residual — easy to alert on.
  • Customer success. "Is this customer's traffic actually growing, or is it just the post-holiday seasonal bump?" The seasonal component answers it cleanly.
  • SLA forecasting. The trend tells you when you'll hit the customer's contracted capacity ceiling; the seasonal tells you which day-of-week it will happen first. Both are needed for the renewal conversation.

3. The pandas implementation — annotated

def deseasonalize(series, period):
    s = series.astype(float)

    # ── Step 1. TREND — centred rolling mean.
    # Window = period (7 for weekly) means the smoothing window covers
    # exactly one full cycle, so any phase-of-week effect averages out.
    # center=True puts the value at the middle of the window — without
    # it, the trend lags the data by 3-4 days.
    # min_periods=1 fills the edges with whatever partial window exists;
    # tighter alternatives are min_periods=period (NaN edges, exact)
    # or a Savitzky-Golay filter for smooth edge handling.
    trend = s.rolling(window=period, center=True, min_periods=1).mean()

    # ── Step 2. DETREND — subtract the trend to expose the seasonal +
    # residual. The "detrended" signal has zero mean over each period.
    detrended = s - trend

    # ── Step 3. SEASONAL — per-phase average of the detrended signal.
    # phase = position-in-period (0..6 for weekly). transform('mean')
    # broadcasts the per-phase mean back to row-level so the seasonal
    # array is the same length as the original series.
    phase = pd.Series(range(len(s)), index=s.index) % period
    seasonal_pattern = detrended.groupby(phase).transform('mean')

    # Re-centre to mean zero so trend + seasonal exactly recovers the
    # smoothed signal. Without this, the seasonal component carries a
    # constant offset that should belong to the trend.
    seasonal_pattern = seasonal_pattern - seasonal_pattern.mean()

    # ── Step 4. DESEASONED — original minus seasonal pattern. This is
    # the curve the autoscaler reads. RESIDUAL = original - trend -
    # seasonal = the unexplained noise (and outliers).
    deseasoned = s - seasonal_pattern
    residual = s - trend - seasonal_pattern

    return pd.DataFrame({
        'original':   s,
        'trend':      trend,
        'seasonal':   seasonal_pattern,
        'deseasoned': deseasoned,
        'residual':   residual,
    })

Why classical decomposition and not STL or X-13ARIMA? Classical (the textbook 1923 method) is good enough for 80 % of cases and runs in pure pandas. STL (Cleveland 1990) handles non-constant seasonality and gradually-changing periods — production-grade but needs statsmodels. X-13ARIMA-SEATS (US Census, the gold standard for official statistics) handles holidays and trading-day effects — needs the X-13 binary. The trade-off: classical is the right call when you control the seasonality model and want zero external dependencies (e.g. browser Pyodide).

Why center=True on the rolling mean? A trailing-only rolling mean lags the data — by hour 7 the trend reflects hours 1-7 averaged, anchored at hour 7. The visual effect is a trend curve shifted to the right of the actual trend. center=True anchors the window-mean at the centre of the window, so hour 4 reflects hours 1-7 (the true centre). The trade-off: the last period/2 rows are NaN unless min_periods permits partial windows.

Why additive (subtract) and not multiplicative (divide)? Additive: y = trend + seasonal + residual. Multiplicative: y = trend × seasonal × residual. Use additive when the seasonal amplitude is constant in absolute terms (Cloudflare requests/sec: weekend dip is ~700 rps regardless of total volume). Use multiplicative when the seasonal swing scales with the trend (retail sales: Christmas spike is 3× the autumn base, not "plus $X"). Multiplicative is np.log(y) first → additive decomposition → np.exp back.

5. Production patterns — how this scales

  • pandas → 10 M rows comfortable. The function above is one-pass linear and the rolling mean is a vectorised C call. For one customer zone for 5 years at minute cadence (~2.6 M rows), runs in seconds. For Cloudflare's full fleet (10 M zones × yearly minute data ≈ 5 trillion rows), no — production runs the same algorithm in Spark per-zone with applyInPandas.
  • The autoscaler reads the deseasoned curve. Cloudflare publicly discusses this: their capacity planner consumes deseasoned traffic from their internal metrics pipeline. The seasonal component itself is stored separately and used for SLA forecasting — "your weekend peak will hit our soft limit on Sept 14".
  • Walmart / Target retail forecasting uses STL or Prophet (Facebook's library) for the same decomposition, because retail seasonality has multiple overlapping cycles (weekly + yearly + holiday). The pandas function above handles single-period seasonality; multi-period requires sequential decomposition (extract weekly → on residuals extract yearly → on residuals extract holiday).
  • Hot key = popular zone. A B2C giant's zone has 1000× the traffic of an SMB zone. groupby(zone_id).apply(deseasonalize) in Spark sees the big-zone task take 1000× longer than the small-zone task. Fix: separate Spark jobs for "top 100 zones" and "long tail" with different parallelism settings. Salt isn't the right tool here because the per-zone computation isn't naively associative (rolling mean needs row order). See customer_growth-0027 for the patterns where salt works.
  • Twilio & SendGrid use the same shape for SMS/email delivery anomaly detection — deseasonalise the per-customer send rate and alert on residual spikes.

6. Interviewer probes

Probe 1 — "Why period=7 in the example?"

Daily data with weekly seasonality. If the data were hourly, period=24 (daily seasonality) or period=168 (weekly seasonality across hourly samples) — depends which cycle you're isolating. Real-world data has multiple overlapping cycles; you pick the one you care about, decompose, then optionally repeat on the residual. Naming the cycle and the cadence relationship is the senior signal.

Probe 2 — "Additive vs multiplicative — when to switch?"

Multiplicative when the seasonal swing scales with the trend. Quick diagnostic: plot raw data, then plot detrended residuals — if the residual variance grows with the trend (heteroscedasticity), use multiplicative. Equivalent: take log(y), run additive decomp, exponentiate back. Retail sales, web traffic during hyper-growth, and ad spend almost always need multiplicative; mature ops metrics usually fit additive.

Probe 3 — "What's wrong with .apply() for the seasonal pattern?"

detrended.groupby(phase).transform('mean') stays in C. A naive detrended.groupby(phase).apply(lambda g: g.mean()) + .map(phase) would re-enter Python — same answer, 10-100× slower on a long series. The transform idiom is the load-bearing vectorisation here; senior candidates name it.

Probe 4 — "Edge effects — the trend has NaN at the ends. How do you handle them?"

Three options. (a) min_periods=1 (what we used) — fills with partial-window means; biased but no NaN. (b) min_periods=period — leaves NaN at the edges; honest but propagates NaN to deseasoned. (c) Two-sided extension: mirror-reflect the first and last period/2 rows so the window is always full; gold standard for offline analysis. Pick based on whether the consumer can handle NaN; autoscaler can't, so go (a) or (c).

Probe 5 — "How would you incrementalise this for streaming?"

You can't, naively — the trend needs a full period of look-ahead (centre=True). Production compromise: emit a trailing trend in real-time (last-period rolling mean, anchored at the right), and re-emit a corrected trend with a 1-period lag. The autoscaler reads the trailing trend live (for now-decisions) and the corrected trend retrospectively (for SLA reporting). Two streams from one decomposition.

Probe 6 — "Holidays — Black Friday spikes 5× the usual Friday. Does this algorithm handle it?"

No — classical decomposition assumes the seasonal pattern is constant. Black Friday breaks that assumption. The residual will be enormous (5× normal Friday minus normal Friday seasonal = 4× residual), which is what triggers the alert. Better: add a holiday-effect term explicitly (Prophet, X-13, or a custom dummy variable per holiday). Classical → STL → Prophet is the typical maturation path for a forecasting team.

Probe 7 — "Concept drift — the weekly pattern changes over time (work-from-home weakened the weekend dip)?"

Classical decomposition averages over the entire series, so old weeks dilute the new pattern. STL handles this with locally-weighted regression — recent weeks weighted higher. Production pattern: re-fit the seasonal component on a sliding window (e.g., last 13 weeks) rather than the full history. Or use Prophet's changepoint_prior_scale to let the model detect when the pattern shifts.
