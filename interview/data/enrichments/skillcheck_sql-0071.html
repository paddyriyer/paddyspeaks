A retailer's merchandising team wants each category's two best-selling products by total 2025 revenue — a per-group top-N report. The twist: if two products tie for second place, both must be shown. The interviewer is probing whether you reach for DENSE_RANK rather than ROW_NUMBER or LIMIT, because only ranking-with-ties returns "all tied products" correctly.

2. The SQL — aggregate, DENSE_RANK per category, keep rank ≤ 2

WITH yr AS (
  SELECT category, product, SUM(spend) AS total_spend
  FROM purchases
  WHERE purchase_ts >= TIMESTAMP '2025-01-01' AND purchase_ts < TIMESTAMP '2026-01-01'
  GROUP BY category, product
),
rk AS (
  SELECT category, product, total_spend,
         DENSE_RANK() OVER (PARTITION BY category ORDER BY total_spend DESC) AS rnk
  FROM yr
)
SELECT category, product, ROUND(total_spend)::int AS total_spend
FROM rk
WHERE rnk <= 2
ORDER BY category, total_spend DESC, product;

yr sums 2025 spend per (category, product). rk ranks products within each category by descending total. The final filter rnk <= 2 keeps the top two rank levels; because DENSE_RANK assigns ties the same rank, a tie at second place yields multiple rows that all pass.

4. Strategic explanations

Why DENSE_RANK and not ROW_NUMBER

The requirement "if there is a tie for second place, include all tied products" is the deciding clause. ROW_NUMBER assigns 1, 2, 3, 4 unconditionally — it would arbitrarily pick one of two tied products for slot 2 and silently drop the other. DENSE_RANK gives tied rows the same rank, so WHERE rnk <= 2 admits every product tied at rank 2. This is precisely the behaviour the question demands.

DENSE_RANK vs RANK — a subtle but real difference

Both share ranks among ties, but they differ after a tie. If two products tie at rank 1, RANK assigns the next product rank 3 (gap), whereas DENSE_RANK assigns it rank 2 (no gap). With RANK, a tie at first place would mean rnk <= 2 returns only those two tied leaders and excludes the genuine runner-up. DENSE_RANK keeps "the top two distinct spend levels" intact regardless of ties higher up — the correct semantics for a "top 2 products" report.

Why PARTITION BY category makes it per-group

The window's PARTITION BY category restarts the ranking for every category independently — refrigerator's rank-1 has nothing to do with tv's rank-1. Without the partition you would get a single global ranking and the result would be the two highest-grossing products overall, not per category. The partition clause is what converts a global top-N into a per-group top-N.

Aggregate first, then rank — order of operations

You cannot rank raw purchase rows; the question ranks products by their summed spend. So the yr CTE must SUM and GROUP BY first, producing one row per (category, product), and only then does DENSE_RANK operate on those totals. Trying to combine SUM and a window function in a single SELECT level fails — window functions execute after GROUP BY, but you cannot also filter on the window result in the same level without another nesting.

The year filter and rounding

purchase_ts >= '2025-01-01' AND purchase_ts < '2026-01-01' is the half-open range, correct for a TIMESTAMP column — it catches a sale at 2025-12-31 23:59 while excluding anything in 2024 or 2026. ROUND(total_spend)::int rounds the NUMERIC sum to the nearest integer then casts; rounding before casting avoids truncation. The final ORDER BY category, total_spend DESC, product just controls presentation — it does not affect which rows survive.

Senior signal: when a top-N spec says "include all tied products", use DENSE_RANK — ROW_NUMBER drops ties and RANK leaves gaps that can exclude a real runner-up. Aggregate to one row per group first, rank with PARTITION BY to make it per-category, then filter on the rank level; and use a half-open timestamp range so boundary sales fall on the right side.
