YouTube ranks videos primarily by watch time and watch percentage. The query computes the average completion rate per video and total minutes watched. The senior framing: completion rate is creator-quality; total minutes is platform-attention. They reward different content.

2. The SQL — per-video AVG completion + total minutes

SELECT video_id,
  COUNT(*) AS views,
  ROUND(AVG(watched_seconds * 1.0 / NULLIF(total_seconds, 0)) * 100.0, 1) AS avg_completion_pct,
  ROUND(SUM(watched_seconds) / 60.0, 1) AS total_minutes_watched
FROM yt_views
GROUP BY video_id
HAVING COUNT(*) >= 2
ORDER BY avg_completion_pct DESC;

3. Strategic explanations

AVG-of-ratio vs SUM/SUM — which is right?

Two methodologies, different answers:

methodformulaweights
Mean of completion ratesAVG(watched/total)each view equal
Aggregate completionSUM(watched)/SUM(total)longer views weighted more

For "creator quality" use mean-of-ratios (each viewer's experience equally). For "platform-utilization" use aggregate (total minutes are what the platform sells). The query above uses the first.

The AVG-of-ratio Simpson's-paradox warning

If a video has 1 view at 100% (1-second clip) and 1 view at 10% (1-hour clip), AVG = 55% completion — but the platform got 6 seconds of value, not "55% of an hour". Be aware of the discrepancy when reporting.

The 50% completion threshold

YouTube's algorithm internally uses 50% as the "satisfied watch" gate. Videos with average completion > 50% earn algorithmic boost; below 50% are demoted. The query result feeds the rank — not just a dashboard.

The video-length confound

Short videos (Shorts: < 60 sec) have structurally higher completion than long-form (10+ min). Comparing them directly is unfair. Stratify:

GROUP BY video_id,
  CASE WHEN total_seconds <= 60 THEN 'short' WHEN total_seconds <= 600 THEN 'mid' ELSE 'long' END
The intro-engagement metric

"% of viewers still watching at second 30" is more diagnostic than average completion. A video with 80% completion across viewers who watched ≥ 30 sec is much stronger than one with 80% completion across all viewers. Production splits.

The skip-pattern signal

Average completion hides which parts get skipped. Production aggregates per-second engagement curves to find the "boring middle" or the "drop-off cliff". The query above doesn't expose this; it requires sub-event-level data (every 10-sec heartbeat).

Watch-time vs ad-time

YouTube monetizes ad views, not just content views. A video with low completion but pre-roll ad watched is still revenue. The full revenue calc joins to ad_impressions and weights accordingly.

Senior signal: name AVG-of-ratio vs SUM/SUM with their different semantics, surface the 50% algorithm gate, stratify by video length, and connect to per-second drop-off curves. The watch-time-vs-ad-time revenue tie-back is the over-the-bar reframe.
