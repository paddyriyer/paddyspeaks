Amazon's marketing-finance team wants a campaign P&L: revenue attributed to each campaign, the budget spent, and the net. It is a join plus an aggregate — but the interviewer is watching for the campaign that drove no sales. Does your query still report it, and if so, what does its net look like?

Campaign 3 spent $2000 but generated no sales rows at all. That orphan campaign is the whole interview — it is exactly what the LEFT JOIN is there to preserve, and it is where a NULL silently appears.

2. The SQL — LEFT JOIN campaigns to sales

SELECT c.campaign_id, SUM(s.amount) AS revenue, c.budget,
  SUM(s.amount) - c.budget AS net
FROM campaigns c LEFT JOIN sales s ON c.campaign_id = s.campaign_id
GROUP BY c.campaign_id, c.budget;

The LEFT JOIN keeps every campaign even when no sale matches; GROUP BY collapses each campaign's sale rows; SUM(s.amount) totals the revenue and net subtracts the budget.

4. Strategic explanations

The bug-shaped behaviour: SUM of no rows is NULL

For a campaign with matching sales, SUM works fine. For campaign 3 the LEFT JOIN produces one row with s.amount = NULL, and SUM of an all-NULL set is NULL, not 0. Then any arithmetic on it — NULL - c.budget — is NULL too, because NULL propagates through -. The fix is to coalesce the revenue before subtracting:

SELECT c.campaign_id,
  COALESCE(SUM(s.amount), 0) AS revenue,
  c.budget,
  COALESCE(SUM(s.amount), 0) - c.budget AS net
FROM campaigns c LEFT JOIN sales s ON c.campaign_id = s.campaign_id
GROUP BY c.campaign_id, c.budget;

Now campaign 3 correctly reports revenue = 0 and net = -2000 — the real loss surfaces. This is the answer an interviewer wants: the original query is not wrong, but it hides the most important number.

Why LEFT JOIN is non-negotiable here

Swap it for an inner JOIN and campaign 3 vanishes from the report entirely — the marketing team would never see that they burned $2000 for nothing. The direction matters too: campaigns LEFT JOIN sales keeps all campaigns. The whole point of a campaign P&L is to expose the zero-revenue campaigns, so the outer join is load-bearing.

Aggregate before joining — avoiding the fan-out, and a cleaner shape

Joining first then grouping works, but pre-aggregating sales is often clearer and dodges row multiplication if other joins are added later:

SELECT c.campaign_id, COALESCE(s.revenue, 0) AS revenue, c.budget,
       COALESCE(s.revenue, 0) - c.budget AS net
FROM campaigns c
LEFT JOIN (SELECT campaign_id, SUM(amount) AS revenue
           FROM sales GROUP BY campaign_id) s
  ON s.campaign_id = c.campaign_id;

Here there is no GROUP BY on the outer query at all — campaigns is already one row per campaign. Same numbers, and it scales better.

ROI as a ratio — and the divide-by-zero trap

Finance usually wants ROI, not just net: revenue / budget. If any campaign can have a $0 budget, that is a division by zero. Guard it with NULLIF: COALESCE(SUM(s.amount),0) / NULLIF(c.budget, 0) — a zero budget yields NULL ROI rather than an error.

Attribution and the duplicated schema columns

Note the schema gives sales its own budget column and campaigns its own amount column — redundant, and a trap. The query correctly takes budget from campaigns and amount from sales; pulling the wrong table's copy would double-count or mislabel. Always qualify columns (c.budget, s.amount) when names collide, and confirm which table is the source of truth.

Senior signal: recognise that SUM over a LEFT JOIN's unmatched rows is NULL and that NULL - budget hides the worst-performing campaign — coalesce revenue to 0 before the subtraction — keep the outer join so zero-revenue campaigns are reported, and guard ROI ratios against a zero budget with NULLIF.
