Amazon's retention team defines an "active" customer as anyone who bought something in the last 90 days, and wants the list for a re-engagement campaign. A one-line WHERE — but the interviewer is probing two things: do you know how CURRENT_DATE and interval arithmetic actually behave, and do you handle the customer who has never bought at all?

1. The source data — customers

Two rows are deliberately on edges. Customer 3 is exactly 90 days ago — in or out depends on ≥ vs >. Customer 5 has a NULL last_purchase_date: a customer who signed up but never purchased. Those two rows are the whole interview.

2. The SQL — WHERE on a date window

SELECT customer_id FROM customers
WHERE last_purchase_date >= CURRENT_DATE - INTERVAL '90 days';

CURRENT_DATE is today's date; subtracting INTERVAL '90 days' yields the cutoff. The predicate keeps every customer whose last purchase is on or after that cutoff.

Customers 1, 2, and 3 are active. Customer 3, exactly 90 days out, is included because the operator is ≥ — the window is "90 days or fewer". Customer 4 is too old. Customer 5 is absent, and that absence is the lesson: the NULL row was silently dropped, not flagged.

4. Strategic explanations

The NULL customer disappears — usually correct, always worth stating

NULL >= anything evaluates to unknown, and WHERE keeps only rows that are true. So customer 5 is filtered out. For "active customers" that is the right outcome — never-purchased is not active. But if the task were to find inactive customers to win back, naively negating the predicate to last_purchase_date < cutoff would also drop the NULL, missing the most inactive customer of all. Inactive needs last_purchase_date < cutoff OR last_purchase_date IS NULL.

The boundary — ≥ vs > is a real decision

"Last 90 days" is ambiguous English. ≥ CURRENT_DATE - 90 includes day 90; > excludes it. One row of difference here, potentially thousands on a real table. Confirm with the interviewer, and notice the column is a DATE — if it were a timestamp, CURRENT_DATE - INTERVAL '90 days' is midnight, so a purchase at 2pm on the cutoff day still passes; comparing timestamps to a date cutoff has its own subtleties.

Why not just hard-code the date?

Writing WHERE last_purchase_date >= '2026-02-14' works today and silently rots tomorrow — the report quietly measures a sliding-then-frozen window. CURRENT_DATE keeps the query correct on every run. The trade-off: it is non-deterministic, so two runs on different days give different results — fine for a live dashboard, a problem for a reproducible snapshot, where you would pass an explicit as-of date as a parameter.

Index-friendliness — keep the function off the column

This predicate is sargable: the column last_purchase_date stands alone and all arithmetic is on the constant side, so an index on last_purchase_date can be used directly. The anti-pattern is wrapping the column — WHERE CURRENT_DATE - last_purchase_date <= 90 — which computes a value per row and defeats the index. Same result, far worse plan on a large table.

"Last purchase" may be a stale denormalised column

last_purchase_date is a precomputed summary on the customer row. If it is maintained by a nightly job or an app trigger, it can lag reality — a purchase made an hour ago may not be reflected yet. The trustworthy alternative is to derive activity from the orders table directly: WHERE customer_id IN (SELECT customer_id FROM orders WHERE order_date >= CURRENT_DATE - INTERVAL '90 days'). Knowing the column might be stale is the senior instinct.

Senior signal: state that the NULL-purchase customer is filtered out and that the inactive query must explicitly add IS NULL, pin down ≥ vs > on the boundary, prefer CURRENT_DATE over a hard-coded date while flagging the reproducibility trade-off, and keep functions off the indexed column so the predicate stays sargable.
