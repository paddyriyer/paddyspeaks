SAP's logistics analytics team owns a supply-chain dashboard, and the operations director keeps asking the same two things about deliveries: "on average, how late are we?" and "how many shipments missed their date?" Both come from one scan of the shipments table — but an interviewer asking this is watching whether you understand that an average delay and a late count are measuring two different things, and that early shipments quietly drag the average down.

1. The source data — shipments

Three different cases live here: shipment 2 arrived exactly on time (delay 0), shipment 3 arrived two days early (delay −2), and the rest were late. The early shipment is the trap — a negative delay pulls the average down without lowering the late count.

2. The SQL — AVG(diff) + conditional SUM

SELECT AVG(actual_date - scheduled_date) AS avg_delay_days,
  SUM(CASE WHEN actual_date > scheduled_date THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) AS late_count
FROM shipments;

In PostgreSQL, subtracting one DATE from another yields an integer number of days. AVG takes the mean of those signed integers; the CASE emits 1 only for genuinely late rows, and SUM counts them.

3. The resulting analysis

avg_delay_dayslate_count
1.20000000000000003

The five day-deltas are +2, 0, −2, +5, +1. They sum to 6, and 6 ÷ 5 = 1.2. Note the −2 from the early shipment is in that sum — it cancels the +2 from shipment 1. Meanwhile late_count = 3 counts only the strictly-late rows; the on-time and early shipments contribute 0. The two numbers tell different stories: average is small, but more than half the shipments still missed.

4. Strategic explanations

Early shipments distort the average — clamp the delay

If "delay" should never be negative (a shipment can't be "−2 days late"), clamp it at zero so early arrivals count as on-time rather than as credit:

SELECT AVG(GREATEST(actual_date - scheduled_date, 0)) AS avg_delay_days
FROM shipments;
-- (2 + 0 + 0 + 5 + 1) / 5 = 1.6, not 1.2

Same data, a different number, because the early shipment no longer subsidises a late one. Confirm with the director which definition they want.

Average over all shipments vs over late shipments

"Average delay" is ambiguous. The query above averages over all 5 rows. The operations team often means "when we are late, by how much?" — a conditional average:

SELECT AVG(actual_date - scheduled_date)
  FILTER (WHERE actual_date > scheduled_date) AS avg_delay_when_late
FROM shipments;
-- (2 + 5 + 1) / 3 = 2.67

Two legitimate metrics from the same column; naming which one is asked for is the senior move.

NULL actual_date — the shipment still in transit

A shipment that hasn't arrived has a NULL actual_date. actual_date - scheduled_date is then NULL, so AVG silently drops it from both numerator and denominator, and the CASE falls to its ELSE 0 — meaning an overdue, undelivered shipment is counted as not-late. That is almost certainly wrong; an interviewer loves this one. Treat undelivered-and-overdue rows explicitly, e.g. compare COALESCE(actual_date, CURRENT_DATE) against the schedule.

On-time rate is the metric the director actually wants

A raw count is hard to compare across weeks of different volume. Express it as a rate, guarding against an empty table:

SELECT ROUND(100.0 * AVG(CASE WHEN actual_date <= scheduled_date THEN 1 ELSE 0 END), 1)
  AS on_time_pct
FROM shipments;

AVG of a 1/0 flag is the proportion directly — cleaner than SUM(...) / COUNT(*).

Senior signal: recognise that date subtraction is signed so early shipments lower the average, decide deliberately whether to clamp with GREATEST(..., 0), distinguish "average over all" from "average when late", and flag that a NULL actual_date on an overdue shipment is wrongly counted as on-time.
