Datadog's growth team reviews paid acquisition weekly, and the number every marketer lives by is CPA — cost per acquisition. Spend lives in campaigns; each customer a campaign converted is a row in conversions. CPA is spend divided by customers acquired. It is a one-line ratio, and the interview is entirely about the two ways it goes wrong: a campaign that converted nobody, and a customer counted twice.

1. The source data — campaigns and conversions

Two edge cases are baked in. Campaign 1 logged customer 103 twice — a re-touched conversion event, not two customers. Campaign 4 spent $400 and converted nobody — dividing by zero is one keystroke away.

2. The SQL — spend / NULLIF(COUNT DISTINCT, 0)

SELECT c.campaign_id, c.spend / NULLIF(COUNT(DISTINCT cv.customer_id),0) AS cpa
FROM campaigns c LEFT JOIN conversions cv ON c.campaign_id = cv.campaign_id
GROUP BY c.campaign_id, c.spend;

COUNT(DISTINCT cv.customer_id) collapses the duplicate; NULLIF(…, 0) turns a zero denominator into NULL so the division is safe; the LEFT JOIN keeps campaign 4 in the report.

4. Strategic explanations

Why COUNT(DISTINCT …) and not COUNT(*)

A conversions table often stores an event per row, not a customer. Re-attribution, multi-touch logging, or a late pixel fire all produce repeated customer_ids. COUNT(*) would count events and inflate the denominator, making CPA look cheaper than reality — the most expensive direction to be wrong for a budgeting decision. COUNT(DISTINCT customer_id) counts people. Always confirm the grain of the conversions table before choosing.

NULLIF is the divide-by-zero guard — know the alternative

NULLIF(x, 0) returns NULL when x = 0, and anything / NULL is NULL — no error. Without it, campaign 4 throws division by zero and the whole report fails. The deliberate decision is what the zero-conversion case should show: NULL ("undefined"), or perhaps the raw wasted spend. A CASE can encode either:

CASE WHEN COUNT(DISTINCT cv.customer_id) = 0 THEN NULL
     ELSE c.spend / COUNT(DISTINCT cv.customer_id) END
The INNER JOIN trap hides your worst campaign

Switch to INNER JOIN and campaign 4 disappears — the one campaign that burned $400 for nothing is exactly the one a growth lead must see. Reporting CPA per campaign means every campaign with spend belongs in the output, converting or not. The LEFT JOIN from campaigns is what guarantees that.

Why spend is in the GROUP BY

c.spend appears in the SELECT but is not aggregated, so Postgres requires it in GROUP BY. Since campaign_id is the campaign's key, spend is functionally dependent on it — grouping by the extra column changes nothing about the result, it just satisfies the grammar. (Grouping by a true primary key would let you skip this; grouping by campaign_id alone here does not, because the planner cannot prove uniqueness without the constraint.)

Integer division — check the column types

If spend were an integer column, integer / bigint truncates: a $1000 spend over 3 customers would return 333, silently dropping the cents. CPA usually wants the fraction — cast with c.spend::numeric / NULLIF(…, 0). Here spend is numeric so it is fine, but state the check.

Senior signal: count DISTINCT customer_id so re-logged conversion events don't deflate CPA, guard the denominator with NULLIF(…, 0) and decide on purpose what a zero-conversion campaign shows, and keep the join LEFT so a campaign that spent money and converted nobody still surfaces in the report.
