A merchandising analyst wants two contribution metrics for every product at once: "what share of revenue does each product hold within its segment, and within its category?" The interviewer is probing whether you can compute two independent group-totals in a single pass with window aggregates — without two self-joins or two separate GROUP BY queries stitched back together.

1. The source data — sales

product_idsegmentcategoryrevenue
1jeansmens1000
2jeansmens2000
3shirtsmens500
4shirtsmens1500

6topswomens2500
7jeanswomens1000

The crucial edge case: segment "jeans" spans two categories — mens (products 1, 2) and womens (product 7). Segment and category are crossing groupings, not nested. A product's segment-share and category-share have different denominators.

2. The SQL — two window aggregates, one scan

SELECT product_id, segment, category, revenue,
       ROUND(100.0 * revenue / SUM(revenue) OVER (PARTITION BY segment)::numeric, 2) AS pct_of_segment,
       ROUND(100.0 * revenue / SUM(revenue) OVER (PARTITION BY category)::numeric, 2) AS pct_of_category
FROM sales
ORDER BY category, segment, product_id;

Each SUM(revenue) OVER (PARTITION BY …) computes a group total without collapsing rows — every product row keeps its detail and also sees its segment total and its category total. The product's revenue divided by each total gives the two shares.

Product 1's segment total is 4000 — the whole "jeans" segment (1000 + 2000 + 1000), including product 7 from a different category — so its segment share is 1000/4000 = 25%. Its category total is 5000 (all of "mens"), giving 20%. The two denominators genuinely differ because the groupings cross.

4. Strategic explanations

Why window aggregates and not GROUP BY

A plain GROUP BY segment would collapse the jeans rows into one and lose product_id — you cannot then attach the total back to each product without a join. SUM(...) OVER (PARTITION BY ...) is the right tool precisely because it computes the group total and keeps every detail row. Two partitions, two totals, one table scan — no self-join, no stitching.

Segment and category are crossing, not nested — the conceptual trap

It is tempting to assume each segment lives inside one category. Here "jeans" appears under both mens and womens. PARTITION BY segment therefore pools all jeans regardless of category, and the segment-share denominator (4000) is not the sum of any single category. If the business actually meant "share within segment-and-category", the partition would be PARTITION BY segment, category — a different number. Confirm which grouping the stakeholder means; the column name alone is the literal answer.

Operator precedence in the percentage expression

100.0 * revenue / SUM(...) OVER (...)::numeric evaluates left-to-right for equal-precedence * and /: (100.0 * revenue) / total — correct. The ::numeric cast binds tightly to the SUM(...) OVER (...) immediately before it. Using the literal 100.0 (not 100) forces numeric arithmetic so the division does not truncate; with an integer 100 and integer revenue you would risk integer division flooring the share to 0.

Division-by-zero and NULL revenue

If a partition's revenue summed to 0 (every product free, or all-NULL revenue), the division raises division by zero. Guard with NULLIF(SUM(revenue) OVER (...), 0) — that turns a zero denominator into NULL, so the share becomes NULL rather than erroring. Separately, SUM skips NULL revenues, so a NULL-revenue product contributes nothing to the total and its own share is NULL — usually fine, but state it.

Dialect notes

Window aggregates with PARTITION BY are ANSI SQL and work in PostgreSQL, SQL Server, Oracle, Snowflake, BigQuery, and MySQL 8+. Pre-8.0 MySQL had no window functions — the fallback there is a join to two derived tables of group totals (GROUP BY segment and GROUP BY category), which is more code and more scans for the same result.

Senior signal: compute both group totals with SUM(revenue) OVER (PARTITION BY ...) in a single scan, recognise that segment and category are crossing groupings so their denominators legitimately differ, force numeric division with a 100.0 literal, and guard the denominator with NULLIF(..., 0) against an all-zero partition.
