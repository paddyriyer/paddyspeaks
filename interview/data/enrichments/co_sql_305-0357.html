Apple's refund rate is a quality signal — high refund rate signals user dissatisfaction or fraud. App Review's threshold for category-level investigation is > 3% refund rate.

2. The SQL — refund rate + dollar impact

SELECT category,
  COUNT(*) AS purchases,
  SUM(refunded) AS refunds,
  ROUND(100.0 * SUM(refunded) / NULLIF(COUNT(*), 0), 2) AS refund_rate_pct,
  ROUND(SUM(CASE WHEN refunded = 1 THEN amount END), 2) AS refunded_dollars
FROM iap_transactions
GROUP BY category
HAVING COUNT(*) >= 5
ORDER BY refund_rate_pct DESC;

3. Strategic explanations

Why refund-rate AND refund-dollars

Rate alone hides scale. A category with 5% refund rate and $5K refunded is much smaller than a category with 1% refund rate and $5M refunded. Production reports both — flag the dollar-heavy refunders even at low rate.

Refund reasons — the diagnostic

Refunds split into:

  • Buyer remorse — fast-acting, "didn't realize" — UX/pricing issue
  • Subscription auto-renewal forgot — friction issue, fixable with reminders
  • Fraud — chargeback-driven, fraudulent purchase
  • App quality — broken / not as described

Production joins to refund_reason for actionable splits.

Time-to-refund

Most legitimate refunds happen within 24 hours of purchase. Refunds 30+ days post-purchase are different (chargebacks, family-sharing disputes). Slice by time-since-purchase.

The Family Sharing twist

One purchase, multiple family members. If one disputes, Apple refunds the whole purchase to keep the family. The category aggregation can over-count if the same purchase appears in multiple family-member views.

Subscription vs one-time IAP refund pattern

Subs have higher refund rate (impulse trial signups + cancel). One-time has lower (intentional purchase). Mixing them inflates the average for one-time IAP categories.

Senior signal: emit rate + dollars together, split refund reasons for actionable diagnosis, slice by time-to-refund, and isolate sub-refund inflation from one-time. The Family Sharing double-count gotcha is the over-the-bar deep cut.
