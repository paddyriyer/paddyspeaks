An HR analyst wants the "above-average earners" in each department — not above the company average, but above their own department's average. The comparison threshold therefore differs row by row. The interviewer is probing whether you can compute a per-group aggregate and then filter individual rows against the matching group's value, and whether you get the strict-vs-equal boundary right.

1. The source data — salary

HR is the deliberate edge case: both employees earn exactly 70, so the department average is also 70 — nobody is strictly above it. A correct query must return zero HR rows, testing whether you use > or sloppily use >=.

2. The SQL — per-department average in a CTE, then a self-comparison join

WITH d AS (
  SELECT dept, AVG(amount) AS dept_avg FROM salary GROUP BY dept
)
SELECT s.emp_id, s.dept, s.amount, ROUND(d.dept_avg::numeric, 2) AS dept_avg
FROM salary s JOIN d ON d.dept = s.dept
WHERE s.amount > d.dept_avg
ORDER BY s.dept, s.emp_id;

The CTE d computes one average per department. Joining each employee back to d on dept attaches that employee's department average to their row; the WHERE s.amount > d.dept_avg filter then keeps only employees who beat their own department's number.

Three rows. Eng keeps employees 2 and 4; employees 1 (100) and 3 (90) fall below the 115 average. Sales keeps only employee 7. HR returns nothing — both earn exactly 70, the average is 70, and 70 > 70 is false. The dept_avg column shows each row was judged against its own department.

4. Strategic explanations

The threshold is per-group — that is why you cannot use one scalar

A single WHERE amount > (SELECT AVG(amount) FROM salary) compares everyone to the company-wide average — the wrong question. The bar must move per department: Eng's 115, Sales' 80, HR's 70. Computing the averages grouped by dept and joining on dept is what makes the comparison row-relative. This "compare a row to its own group's aggregate" shape is the core pattern the question is testing.

Why a CTE join over a correlated subquery

You could write WHERE amount > (SELECT AVG(amount) FROM salary s2 WHERE s2.dept = s.dept) — correct, but the subquery re-runs per row and naive engines recompute each department's average many times. The CTE computes every department average once, then joins. Same result, and the planner can hash-join the small aggregate against the base table. A window function — AVG(amount) OVER (PARTITION BY dept) — is a third equally good option that avoids the explicit join entirely.

The strict inequality decides HR — > not >=

"More than the average" means strictly greater. HR's two employees both sit on the average, so > excludes them — the intended answer. Switching to >= would wrongly return both HR rows, and in any uniform department every employee would qualify. The fixture's all-equal HR group exists precisely to catch this off-by-one; always probe the boundary with an "exactly equal" case.

An employee never inflates their own comparison — but they are in the average

Subtle point: the department average includes the employee being tested. Eng's 115 average counts employee 4's 150. So a single outlier raises the bar for everyone, themselves included — employee 4 still clears it here, but a lone high earner can pull the average up enough that mid-tier colleagues drop below. If the business wanted "above the average of everyone else", you would compute a leave-one-out average — a different, harder query. Confirm which definition the question means; the standard reading includes the row itself.

NULL salaries and the rounding cast

AVG ignores NULL amount values, so a NULL-salary employee neither shifts the department average nor passes amount > dept_avg (a comparison with NULL is unknown, so they are filtered out). That is usually the desired behaviour, but decide consciously. ROUND(d.dept_avg::numeric, 2) is purely cosmetic for the output column — the ::numeric cast is needed because Postgres's two-argument ROUND requires numeric; crucially the WHERE compares against the unrounded dept_avg, so rounding never distorts the filter.

Senior signal: compare each row to its own group's aggregate by grouping then joining on the group key (a CTE or AVG() OVER (PARTITION BY), not a company-wide scalar), use a strict > so an all-equal department correctly returns nobody, and note the tested row is included in its own average — flag a leave-one-out reading if the business needs it.
