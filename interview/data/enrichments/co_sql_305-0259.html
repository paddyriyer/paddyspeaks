Datadog's billing analytics team needs to find invoices that were paid late and by how many days — the input to dunning workflows and DSO (days sales outstanding) reporting. The query is a simple date subtraction with a filter, but the filter's boundary condition and the treatment of still-unpaid invoices are exactly what an interviewer probes.

Invoice 1 was paid early (Jan 10 vs Jan 15 due). Invoice 4 was paid exactly on the due date — the boundary case. The other four are genuinely late. The filter decides which of these count.

2. The SQL — paid_date - due_date where late

SELECT invoice_id, paid_date - due_date AS days_late
FROM invoices WHERE paid_date > due_date;

Subtracting two Postgres DATEs gives an integer day count. The WHERE paid_date > due_date keeps only invoices paid strictly after their due date — a strict >, so the on-time and early invoices are excluded.

4. Strategic explanations

The boundary — is "due date" the last on-time day?

Invoice 4 was paid on Feb 15, exactly its due date, and the strict > treats it as on time. That is the standard reading: the due date is the last acceptable day. But if "late" means "not paid before the due date", you would need >=, and invoice 4 would appear with days_late = 0. Confirm the convention — a 0-day-late row is either a precision win or noise depending on the definition.

The big one — unpaid invoices are invisible

The filter paid_date > due_date only ever sees invoices that have a paid_date. An invoice that is overdue and still unpaid has paid_date = NULL, the comparison is unknown, and the row is silently dropped — the very worst delinquents disappear from a "payment delay" report. That is usually the opposite of what billing wants:

SELECT invoice_id,
       COALESCE(paid_date, CURRENT_DATE) - due_date AS days_late,
       (paid_date IS NULL) AS still_open
FROM invoices
WHERE COALESCE(paid_date, CURRENT_DATE) > due_date;

This measures lateness against today for open invoices and flags them. Raising the unpaid-invoice blind spot unprompted is the senior signal.

The reference solution bundles its own setup

The provided answer ships a DROP TABLE / CREATE TABLE / INSERT ahead of the SELECT — a self-contained script. In an interview that is fine for a runnable demo, but note the schema it creates (invoice_id, due_date, paid_date) is narrower than the full invoices table in the question's schema (which also has customer_id, amount, status). The query logic is unaffected; just be aware the demo table is a slice.

No ordering — rank the worst offenders

For a collections worklist, the rows should lead with the most overdue:

SELECT invoice_id, paid_date - due_date AS days_late
FROM invoices WHERE paid_date > due_date
ORDER BY days_late DESC;
Edge cases: paid_date before due_date, and timestamp columns

Early payments yield a negative subtraction; the strict > filter already excludes them, so no negative days_late leaks out — good. If due_date or paid_date were timestamp rather than date, the subtraction would return an interval with hours, and an invoice paid at 2am on the due date might or might not count depending on time-of-day; cast both to ::date to keep the comparison purely calendar-based.

Senior signal: confirm whether the due date is the last on-time day (> vs >=), and above all flag that filtering on paid_date hides still-unpaid overdue invoices — the worst delinquents — offering a COALESCE(paid_date, CURRENT_DATE) rewrite plus an ORDER BY days_late DESC for the collections worklist.
