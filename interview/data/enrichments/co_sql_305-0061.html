BMW's connected-services team wants to identify "recurring" customers — people who have ordered in at least three distinct months, a signal of genuine engagement rather than a one-off buyer. The query hinges on a single subtle word: distinct months. Three orders in one week is not recurring; three orders across three months is. The interviewer is checking whether you count the right thing.

Customer 102 has two orders in the same month (both January) — three orders would not be enough if they all clustered like this. That is the trap: order count and distinct-month count are different numbers.

2. The SQL — HAVING COUNT(DISTINCT month)

SELECT customer_id FROM orders
GROUP BY customer_id
HAVING COUNT(DISTINCT DATE_TRUNC('month', order_date)::DATE) >= 3;

Group by customer; for each, DATE_TRUNC('month', ...) maps every order to its month, COUNT(DISTINCT ...) tallies how many different months appear, and HAVING ... >= 3 keeps only customers clearing the bar.

The DISTINCT is what enforces "recurring".

4. Strategic explanations

COUNT(*) vs COUNT(DISTINCT month) — the core distinction

COUNT(*) counts order rows; COUNT(DISTINCT DATE_TRUNC('month', order_date)) counts unique calendar months. They diverge the moment a customer orders twice in one month. "Recurring across months" demands the distinct count — a buyer who placed five orders on the same day is a one-month customer, not a recurring one. Choosing DISTINCT here is the entire correctness argument.

Why the filter belongs in HAVING, not WHERE

The condition is on an aggregate — a per-customer count — and aggregates do not exist yet when WHERE runs. WHERE filters individual rows before grouping; HAVING filters groups after. You cannot write WHERE COUNT(DISTINCT ...) >= 3; it must be HAVING. (A row-level predicate like order_date >= '2024-01-01' would still go in WHERE — the two clauses cooperate.)

Returning the order count alongside the flag

A bare list of ids is rarely the end of the conversation. Surface the evidence:

SELECT customer_id,
       COUNT(*) AS total_orders,
       COUNT(DISTINCT DATE_TRUNC('month', order_date)::date) AS active_months
FROM orders
GROUP BY customer_id
HAVING COUNT(DISTINCT DATE_TRUNC('month', order_date)::date) >= 3
ORDER BY active_months DESC;

Now the reader sees 101 with 3 months and 103 with 4 — the difference between "barely recurring" and "highly engaged" the bare-id query hides.

Consecutive months — the harder follow-up

"Recurring" sometimes means three consecutive months, not just any three. That is a different, harder problem — the classic gaps-and-islands. You rank the distinct months per customer and subtract the row number; consecutive months share a constant difference, forming an island:

SELECT customer_id FROM (
  SELECT customer_id, mon,
         mon - (ROW_NUMBER() OVER (PARTITION BY customer_id ORDER BY mon)
                * INTERVAL '1 month') AS grp
  FROM (SELECT DISTINCT customer_id,
               DATE_TRUNC('month', order_date)::date AS mon
        FROM orders) m
) t
GROUP BY customer_id, grp
HAVING COUNT(*) >= 3;

Clarify which definition the product means before reaching for this — the simple HAVING answers "any 3 months", not "3 in a row".

Status, NULL dates, and the time window

Cancelled orders should probably not count toward engagement — add WHERE status = 'completed' if so. An order with a NULL order_date truncates to NULL, and COUNT(DISTINCT) ignores NULLs, so it silently does not contribute a month — usually fine, worth stating. And "recurring" is often time-bounded ("3 months in the last year"): scope it with a WHERE on order_date.

Senior signal: use COUNT(DISTINCT month) rather than COUNT(*) so same-month orders do not inflate engagement, explain why the aggregate filter must live in HAVING not WHERE, return the supporting counts instead of a bare id list, and ask whether "recurring" means any three months or three consecutive ones.
