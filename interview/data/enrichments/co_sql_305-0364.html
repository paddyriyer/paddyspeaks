Airbnb's calendar-as-fact table has one row per (listing, night). The query aggregates booked vs available nights per city to compute occupancy — the headline metric for host quality and city saturation.

1. The source data — calendar_nights

listing_idnightavailablebookedcity
L_012024-01-0101Tokyo

2. The SQL — booked / (available + booked)

SELECT city,
  COUNT(*) AS listing_nights,
  SUM(booked) AS booked_nights,
  SUM(available) AS available_nights,
  ROUND(100.0 * SUM(booked) / NULLIF(SUM(available) + SUM(booked), 0), 1) AS occupancy_pct
FROM calendar_nights
GROUP BY city
ORDER BY occupancy_pct DESC;

3. Strategic explanations

Why "calendar-as-fact" — the modeling choice

Most companies have facts as events (orders, clicks). Airbnb's calendar is a periodic snapshot: one row per (listing, night) regardless of activity. This is unusual but powerful — every night exists in the table whether booked or blocked. Density-of-data answer.

The "blocked" vs "available" trap

Hosts can block nights without bookings (private use, repairs). The query above conflates "blocked" and "available". The honest occupancy rate is booked / not-blocked:

SUM(booked) / NULLIF(SUM(booked) + SUM(available), 0)
-- where available means "open for booking", excludes blocked nights
Demand-side vs supply-side

Low occupancy in Tokyo can mean:

  • Demand low — pricing too high, marketing weak
  • Supply too high — too many listings competing
  • Seasonal — winter in beach city

The query result is the symptom; pair with search-volume + listing-count for diagnosis.

Per-listing distribution

City average can hide a bimodal distribution: 20% of listings at 90% occupancy, 80% at 20%. The "stars vs strugglers" split is the host-quality story:

NTILE(10) OVER (PARTITION BY city ORDER BY occupancy_pct DESC) AS occupancy_decile
The seasonality cut

Most cities have 3-5x peak/trough seasonal occupancy. Slice by month for honest comparison:

GROUP BY city, substr(night, 1, 7) AS month
Holiday + event spikes

Olympics in Paris, Coachella in Indio — single-event spikes. The query above includes them; for "normal" baselines, exclude top-5 highest-occupancy weeks.

Senior signal: name "calendar-as-fact" as an unusual periodic-snapshot pattern, distinguish blocked vs available in the denominator, pair with search-volume for demand-vs-supply diagnosis, and decile-rank to expose the long-tail. Seasonal slicing and event exclusion are the over-the-bar refinements.
