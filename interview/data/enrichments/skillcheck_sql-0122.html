A cinema booking system stores one row per seat in a row with a free/taken flag. A group wants seats together, so the report needs every free seat that has at least one adjacent free seat. "Adjacent" means consecutive seat IDs — and the interviewer is checking whether you can express "the row next to me" as a self-join on seat_id ± 1 without assuming the IDs start at 1.

1. The source data — cinema

2Yfree; neighbour 1 free → qualifies

4Yfree, but neighbours 3 and 5 are both taken → isolated

7Yfree; neighbours 6 and 8 free → qualifies

Seat 4 is the edge case: it is free, but both neighbours (3 and 5) are taken — an isolated free seat that must not appear. Seat 7 is the opposite — free with free seats on both sides — and the DISTINCT must collapse its two matches into one row.

2. The SQL — self-join on consecutive IDs

SELECT DISTINCT a.seat_id
FROM cinema a
JOIN cinema b
  ON ABS(a.seat_id - b.seat_id) = 1
 AND a.free = 'Y' AND b.free = 'Y'
ORDER BY a.seat_id;

Each free seat a is joined to any other seat b whose ID differs by exactly 1 — that is, an immediate neighbour — and b must also be free. A seat survives the join only if at least one free neighbour exists; DISTINCT dedupes seats that have two.

Seat 4 is correctly absent: it is free, but no row with ID 3 or 5 is also free, so the join produces nothing for it. Seat 7 appears once despite matching both 6 and 8 — DISTINCT removes the duplicate.

4. Strategic explanations

Why ABS(diff) = 1 and not seat_id = seat_id + 1

ABS(a.seat_id - b.seat_id) = 1 matches a neighbour on either side in one predicate — the seat before or after. You could instead write b.seat_id IN (a.seat_id - 1, a.seat_id + 1); same result. What you must not assume is contiguous IDs starting at 1 — the question warns the row may start anywhere, so anything based on ROW_NUMBER position or hard-coded ranges is fragile. The arithmetic on seat_id itself is robust to the starting offset, though it does assume IDs are gap-free integers.

Why DISTINCT is required

A self-join produces one output row per matching pair. Seat 7 has two free neighbours, so it matches twice and would appear twice without DISTINCT. The question explicitly says "no duplicate output", so DISTINCT is mandatory, not stylistic. An alternative that needs no DISTINCT is EXISTS: WHERE a.free='Y' AND EXISTS (SELECT 1 FROM cinema b WHERE b.free='Y' AND ABS(a.seat_id-b.seat_id)=1) — it asks "is there at least one" and stops, naturally yielding one row per seat.

The join naturally excludes the self-pair

A seat is not its own neighbour: ABS(a.seat_id - b.seat_id) = 1 can never be satisfied when a and b are the same row (difference 0). So no explicit a.seat_id <> b.seat_id is needed — the distance predicate already rules out the self-match. Contrast a self-join keyed on equality, where you must add a.id <> b.id to avoid pairing a row with itself.

What a senior says next — the window-function alternative

The self-join is O(n²) in the worst case and assumes no ID gaps. A cleaner modern phrasing uses LAG/LEAD: order by seat_id, look at the previous and next row's free flag, and keep a free seat if either neighbour is free. That sorts once — O(n log n) — and, if you compare on seat_id proximity rather than blind row order, it also tolerates ID gaps gracefully. Offering the window-function version unprompted, with its complexity edge, is the senior signal.

Senior signal: model "adjacent" as a self-join on ABS(seat_id - seat_id) = 1 so it works regardless of the starting ID, add DISTINCT because a seat with two free neighbours matches twice, note the distance predicate already excludes the self-pair, and offer a LAG/LEAD rewrite that is O(n log n) and gap-tolerant.
