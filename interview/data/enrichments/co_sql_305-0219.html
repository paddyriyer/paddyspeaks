Stripe's merchant-insights team wants the five best-selling products on a storefront to feature them in a "top picks" widget. The query joins line items to the product catalogue, sums quantities, and takes the top 5 — but the interviewer is probing whether you sum units or revenue, whether you group on a stable key rather than the product name, and what your LIMIT 5 does when sales are tied at the boundary.

Products 1 and 5 each appear on two line items, so we must SUM per product. There are six products but only five slots — one product gets cut, and that boundary is the interview.

2. The SQL — SUM(qty) + ORDER BY + LIMIT 5

SELECT p.name, SUM(oi.qty) AS units
FROM order_items oi JOIN products p ON oi.product_id = p.product_id
GROUP BY p.name ORDER BY units DESC LIMIT 5;

Join line items to the catalogue, sum quantities per product, sort descending by units, keep the top five.

4. Strategic explanations

Units vs revenue — "best-selling" is ambiguous

The solution ranks by units sold. But a "top picks" widget for a payments company often cares about revenue contribution. The two rankings diverge sharply — a cheap high-volume item beats an expensive one on units but loses on revenue:

SELECT p.name, SUM(oi.qty * p.price) AS revenue
FROM order_items oi JOIN products p ON oi.product_id = p.product_id
GROUP BY p.name ORDER BY revenue DESC LIMIT 5;

Ask which metric the widget should rank on before committing. Naming the ambiguity is the senior move.

Group on product_id, not name

GROUP BY p.name works only because names happen to be unique here. Two distinct products can share a display name ("Gift Card", regional SKUs), and grouping by name would silently merge their sales into one inflated row. The robust key is the primary key:

GROUP BY p.product_id, p.name

Grouping by the id and carrying the name along is functionally safe and still lets you select the name.

The LIMIT-at-a-tie trap

ORDER BY units DESC LIMIT 5 is non-deterministic at the cut line. If the 5th and 6th products were tied at, say, 8 units each, the engine returns one of them arbitrarily — and a different one on the next run after a plan change. For a stable "top 5," add a deterministic tiebreaker, or use ranking to keep all ties:

SELECT name, units FROM (
  SELECT p.name, SUM(oi.qty) AS units,
         RANK() OVER (ORDER BY SUM(oi.qty) DESC) AS rk
  FROM order_items oi JOIN products p ON oi.product_id = p.product_id
  GROUP BY p.product_id, p.name
) t WHERE rk <= 5;
-- RANK keeps every product tied at the boundary; row count may exceed 5

Decide deliberately: exactly five rows (add ORDER BY units DESC, name) or "all products in the top 5 ranks" (use RANK).

Products that never sold — the JOIN choice

The inner join means a product with zero line items contributes nothing and cannot appear — correct for "best-selling," since a never-sold product is not a top seller. But if the ask flips to "rank all products, zero-sellers at the bottom," you need products LEFT JOIN order_items with COALESCE(SUM(oi.qty), 0) so the unsold products surface with 0.

NULL or negative quantities

SUM skips NULL qty rows silently — usually fine, but a product whose only line items are NULL would sum to NULL and sort unpredictably. And returns are sometimes stored as negative qty; if so, SUM nets them out (which may be intended as "net units") — confirm whether the widget wants gross or net volume.

Senior signal: ask whether "best-selling" means units or revenue, group on the stable product_id rather than a possibly-duplicated name, recognise that LIMIT 5 is non-deterministic at a tie and add a tiebreaker (or RANK), and know the inner join correctly excludes never-sold products.
