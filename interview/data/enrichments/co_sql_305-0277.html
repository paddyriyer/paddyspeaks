The growth team wants the list of VIP customers for a holiday-season targeted email campaign — defined as customers with both high frequency (more than 5 orders) and high spend (more than $500 lifetime). Either signal alone is too loose: a customer with 20 small orders is just a window-shopper; a customer with one big order is one-shot. The intersection is the right cohort.

2. The SQL — HAVING with double condition

SELECT
  customer_id,
  COUNT(*)    AS n,
  SUM(amount) AS total
FROM orders
GROUP BY customer_id
HAVING COUNT(*) > 5
   AND SUM(amount) > 500;

4. Strategic explanations

HAVING vs WHERE — the universal confusion

WHERE filters individual rows BEFORE aggregation. HAVING filters AGGREGATED groups AFTER. You can't write WHERE COUNT(*) > 5 — there's no aggregation yet at the WHERE phase. Use HAVING for aggregate-conditions; WHERE for row-conditions. Often both apply in the same query:

SELECT customer_id, COUNT(*) AS n, SUM(amount) AS total
FROM orders
WHERE order_date >= '2025-01-01'           -- per-row filter
  AND amount > 0                            -- per-row filter
GROUP BY customer_id
HAVING COUNT(*) > 5                         -- per-group filter
   AND SUM(amount) > 500;                   -- per-group filter
Both conditions intersect — AND vs OR matters a lot

Replace AND with OR and the cohort changes: anyone with EITHER 5+ orders OR $500+ spend qualifies. That'd include C_001, C_002 (frequent), C_003 (high spend), and C_004 (high spend) — almost everyone. The marketing team has to pick: AND for tight precision, OR for wide reach. The choice is product strategy, not SQL.

The dollar threshold trap — choose your time window

The query above measures lifetime spend. A customer who spent $500 over 5 years isn't a current VIP. The actionable version filters by recency:

WHERE order_date >= CURRENT_DATE - INTERVAL '90 days'  -- last 90 days only
GROUP BY customer_id
HAVING COUNT(*) > 5 AND SUM(amount) > 500;
Why this works without a CTE

The naïve attempt is to compute aggregates in a CTE first, then filter:

WITH stats AS (
  SELECT customer_id, COUNT(*) AS n, SUM(amount) AS total
  FROM orders GROUP BY customer_id
)
SELECT * FROM stats WHERE n > 5 AND total > 500;

This works fine and reads more clearly. HAVING is the equivalent that avoids the CTE wrapper. Pick CTE when you need to reuse the aggregates downstream (e.g. for a window function); pick HAVING when filtering is the only thing you need.

The over-the-bar finish: tier the VIPs

Sometimes the marketing team wants tiers, not a single bucket. Use CASE to assign tiers based on which thresholds get crossed:

SELECT customer_id,
  COUNT(*) AS n, SUM(amount) AS total,
  CASE
    WHEN COUNT(*) > 10 AND SUM(amount) > 1000 THEN 'Diamond'
    WHEN COUNT(*) > 5  AND SUM(amount) > 500  THEN 'Gold'
    WHEN COUNT(*) > 5  OR  SUM(amount) > 500  THEN 'Silver'
    ELSE                                          'Bronze'
  END AS tier
FROM orders GROUP BY customer_id
HAVING COUNT(*) > 5 OR SUM(amount) > 500;
Senior signal: articulate the WHERE-vs-HAVING distinction unprompted, name the AND-vs-OR cohort-size trade-off, and pre-empt the time-window question. The CTE-vs-HAVING equivalence and the tier-assignment finish are the over-the-bar moves.
