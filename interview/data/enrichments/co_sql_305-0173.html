CapitalOne's travel-rewards team runs a hotel-partner dashboard, and the headline tile is today's occupancy rate: what fraction of rooms are occupied right now? It looks like a one-liner. The interviewer is probing two things — whether you handle a room booked by more than one overlapping reservation without double-counting, and whether your date overlap logic is correct on the boundary days.

Room 1 has two bookings that both cover today — the double-count trap. Room 2's stay ended on the 14th — it is not occupied today. Room 3's stay starts today — a boundary case. Room 4 is empty.

2. The SQL — occupied / total × 100

SELECT 100.0 * COUNT(DISTINCT b.room_id)
              / (SELECT COUNT(*) FROM rooms) AS occupancy_pct
FROM bookings b
WHERE b.start_date <= CURRENT_DATE
  AND b.end_date   >= CURRENT_DATE;

The WHERE keeps bookings whose date range straddles today; COUNT(DISTINCT b.room_id) is the numerator; a scalar subquery counts total rooms for the denominator.

Rooms 1 and 3 are occupied today → 2 of 4 rooms → 50%. Room 1 had two overlapping bookings but counts once — that is the DISTINCT earning its place. Room 2 (checked out the 14th) and room 4 (no booking) are correctly excluded.

4. Strategic explanations

COUNT(DISTINCT room_id) — the double-count defence

Room 1 matches the WHERE clause twice — once for each overlapping reservation. Plain COUNT(b.room_id) would count it as 2, the numerator would be 3, and occupancy would read a nonsensical 75% — or worse, a number above 100% if a popular room had many bookings. COUNT(DISTINCT room_id) answers the real question: how many distinct rooms are occupied, not how many reservations exist. This is the single most important line in the query.

The boundary logic — <= / >= and the half-open trap

The overlap test uses inclusive comparisons: a booking covers today if it started on or before today and ends on or after today. That counts room 3 (starts today) as occupied. But hotels usually treat end_date as the checkout day — the guest is gone by then, the room is free. Under that model the predicate should be b.end_date > CURRENT_DATE (strict), so a guest checking out today does not count. Confirm whether end_date is the last night or the checkout morning — it shifts the number. Room 2 (ends the 14th, in the past) is excluded either way.

The denominator must come from rooms, not bookings

It is tempting to compute occupancy entirely from bookings, but the denominator is total inventory — and room 4 has never been booked, so it appears nowhere in bookings. Counting distinct rooms in bookings as the denominator would silently exclude empty rooms and inflate occupancy. The scalar subquery (SELECT COUNT(*) FROM rooms) is correct precisely because it sees the whole inventory.

Why 100.0 and not 100

COUNT(…) returns an integer, and integer ÷ integer does integer division in Postgres: 2 / 4 would be 0, so 100 * 2 / 4 evaluates left-to-right as 200 / 4 = 50 here — correct by luck of the multiplication order. Reorder it and you get 0. Leading with the numeric literal 100.0 forces the whole expression to numeric, making the result robust regardless of operator order. A safer habit is an explicit cast: COUNT(DISTINCT b.room_id)::numeric.

The zero-rooms edge case

If rooms were empty, the denominator is 0 and the query raises division by zero. Guard it with NULLIF((SELECT COUNT(*) FROM rooms), 0) so an empty hotel yields NULL rather than crashing the dashboard. Also note: with zero matching bookings the numerator is simply 0 and occupancy is a clean 0% — that direction is safe.

Senior signal: use COUNT(DISTINCT room_id) so a room with multiple overlapping bookings is not double-counted, draw the denominator from the rooms inventory so empty rooms are included, pin down whether end_date is the last night or checkout day before fixing the boundary operator, and force numeric division with 100.0 or an explicit cast.
