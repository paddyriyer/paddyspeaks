Robinhood's market-ops team wants a map of which cities have ever had a trade settle — a quick "where are our active users" snapshot. The query is one of the shortest you can write, and that is exactly the trap: an interviewer asking a one-line question is testing whether you understand why the line is correct, and what breaks it.

1. The source data — trades

trade_idcitystatus
1New Yorkcompleted
2New Yorkpending
3Chicagocompleted
4Austincancelled
5Chicagocompleted
6Denverfailed
7Miamicompleted

Chicago appears twice, both completed — the result must collapse it to one row. New York has a completed trade and a pending one. Austin and Denver have only non-completed trades and must vanish entirely.

2. The SQL — SELECT DISTINCT with a filter

SELECT DISTINCT city FROM trades WHERE status = 'completed';

WHERE keeps only completed-status rows; DISTINCT removes the duplicate city values that survive.

3. The resulting analysis

city
Chicago
Miami
New York

Three cities. Chicago's two completed trades collapsed to one row — that is DISTINCT doing its job. New York made the list on the strength of its one completed trade; its pending trade is irrelevant once the filter has run. Austin (cancelled) and Denver (failed) are gone. The output has no inherent order — DISTINCT does not sort — so add ORDER BY city if a human reads it.

4. Strategic explanations

The order of operations — filter first, dedupe second

Logically: WHERE runs before DISTINCT. Rows are filtered down to completed trades, and only then are duplicate cities removed. This matters because a city is included if it has at least one completed trade — New York qualifies even though most of its trades are not completed. If you wrote DISTINCT first and filtered after, you would need the city's status, which it no longer carries. The given order is the only one that works.

DISTINCT vs GROUP BY — identical here, and worth saying so

This query is exactly equivalent to:

SELECT city FROM trades WHERE status = 'completed' GROUP BY city;

For pure de-duplication they produce the same plan and the same result. DISTINCT reads better when you just want unique values; GROUP BY earns its place the moment you need an aggregate alongside — e.g. SELECT city, COUNT(*) ... GROUP BY city. Knowing they are interchangeable here, and when they stop being interchangeable, is the senior detail.

The status-value trap — exact string equality

status = 'completed' is case-sensitive and whitespace-sensitive. Real status columns drift: 'Completed', 'COMPLETED', 'completed ', 'complete', 'settled' all fail this equality and the city silently disappears. Defend against it:

WHERE LOWER(TRIM(status)) = 'completed'

Better still, ask whether status is a constrained enum or free text. If "filled" and "settled" also count as success, the filter should be status IN ('completed','settled','filled') — confirm the vocabulary before guessing.

NULL status and NULL city

A row with status = NULL fails status = 'completed' (NULL comparisons are never true), so it is excluded — usually fine. But a completed trade with a NULL city survives the filter and DISTINCT keeps NULL as one of its values, so you would get a stray empty/NULL row in the city list. If the map cannot render an unknown city, add AND city IS NOT NULL.

"Ever completed" vs "currently completed"

The query answers "cities with any completed trade, ever". If status is mutable — a trade can move pending → completed → reversed — the business may instead want cities where a trade is completed right now, or completed within a date range. That is a different question and needs a timestamp column. Surfacing that the table has no time dimension, and asking whether recency matters, separates a literal answer from a useful one.

Senior signal: explain that WHERE logically precedes DISTINCT so a city qualifies on a single completed trade, note that DISTINCT and GROUP BY coincide here but diverge once an aggregate is needed, and harden the brittle status = 'completed' string match plus the NULL-city case before declaring the one-liner done.
