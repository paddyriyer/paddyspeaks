Uber's Pricing team owns one number that drives both rider acquisition (low surge) and supply rebalancing (high surge): the surge multiplier. The query buckets each ride by surge band and aggregates volume + revenue. The senior conversation: surge isn't binary — it's a continuous response curve.

2. The SQL — bucketed surge band aggregation

SELECT area_id,
  CASE
    WHEN surge_multiplier < 1.5 THEN 'normal'
    WHEN surge_multiplier < 2.0 THEN 'mild_surge'
    ELSE 'heavy_surge'
  END AS surge_band,
  COUNT(*)            AS rides,
  ROUND(AVG(fare), 2) AS avg_fare,
  ROUND(SUM(fare), 2) AS total_fare
FROM rides
GROUP BY area_id, surge_band
ORDER BY area_id, surge_band;

3. Strategic explanations

Why bucket — not raw multiplier

Continuous surge multipliers (1.0, 1.2, 1.4, ...) produce 10+ groups per area — too many to read on a dashboard. Buckets compress the long tail: normal (90% of rides), mild_surge (8%), heavy_surge (2%). The 2% heavy-surge band drives a disproportionate share of revenue and complaints — that's the policy lever.

The CASE-in-GROUP BY idiom

SQLite (and most engines) accepts GROUP BY on a CASE expression directly. Some strict dialects (older PG) require wrapping in a CTE first. Portable form:

WITH banded AS (
  SELECT area_id, fare,
    CASE WHEN surge_multiplier < 1.5 THEN 'normal' ... END AS surge_band
  FROM rides
)
SELECT area_id, surge_band, COUNT(*), AVG(fare) FROM banded GROUP BY 1, 2;
The price-elasticity follow-up

Surge raises prices to balance the marketplace. The actual research question: "at 1.7x surge, how many riders abandon vs accept?" That requires the requested fare data, not just completed rides. Production: join to fare_estimates (shown to user before booking) and compute conversion rate per surge band.

The driver-side mirror

Surge attracts drivers to the area. The companion query: "at 1.7x surge, how many drivers came online within 10 min?" If the ratio is too low, surge isn't doing its supply-rebalancing job — the multiplier needs to be higher OR the alert mechanism is broken.

Geo-temporal heatmap follow-up

Real Uber dashboards plot area × hour heatmaps with surge color. Add hour to GROUP BY:

GROUP BY area_id, CAST(strftime('%H', started_at) AS INTEGER), surge_band
Senior signal: bucket the continuous variable for readability, surface the price-elasticity question (the missing denominator), and connect surge to both rider abandonment and driver supply response. The geo-temporal heatmap GROUP BY is the over-the-bar slice.
