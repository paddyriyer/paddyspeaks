Snap's growth team suspects the signup funnel is double-counting people — the same human registering more than once under the same email. The reference solution groups registrations by email and keeps the groups with more than one row. It is the canonical duplicate-finder, and the interview is about the predicate placement and what "the same email" actually means.

1. The source data — registrations

user_idemailregistered_at
1pat@x.com2024-01-01 09:00
2sam@x.com2024-01-02 10:00
3pat@x.com2024-02-15 11:00
4pat@x.com2024-03-20 12:00
5sam@x.com2024-04-01 13:00
6lee@x.com2024-04-02 14:00

pat@x.com appears three times, sam@x.com twice, and lee@x.com exactly once. The single-registration lee is the control: it must not appear in the output.

2. The SQL — GROUP BY … HAVING COUNT(*) > 1

SELECT email, COUNT(*) AS dup_count
FROM registrations GROUP BY email HAVING COUNT(*) > 1;

Collapse rows by email, count each group, and keep only groups whose count exceeds 1. The HAVING clause filters after the grouping.

3. The resulting analysis

emaildup_count
pat@x.com3
sam@x.com2

Two rows. pat registered three times, sam twice; lee is correctly absent because its group of one fails COUNT(*) > 1. The query answers "which emails are duplicated, and how badly".

4. Strategic explanations

HAVING vs WHERE — the question the interviewer always asks

WHERE filters individual rows before grouping; HAVING filters groups after the aggregate is computed. The duplicate condition is COUNT(*) > 1 — that count does not exist until grouping has happened, so it must live in HAVING. Writing WHERE COUNT(*) > 1 is a hard error. The reverse mistake is just as telling: a pre-grouping filter like "only registrations after 2024" belongs in WHERE, not HAVING — putting it in HAVING works but scans rows it could have skipped. The rule: row predicates → WHERE, aggregate predicates → HAVING.

"The same email" — case and whitespace

Email is case-insensitive by spec: Pat@x.com and pat@x.com are the same mailbox. But GROUP BY email on a plain text column groups them separately — so a real duplicate hides as two distinct groups of one and escapes the report. Normalise before grouping:

SELECT LOWER(TRIM(email)) AS email_norm, COUNT(*) AS dup_count
FROM registrations
GROUP BY LOWER(TRIM(email))
HAVING COUNT(*) > 1;

TRIM kills stray leading/trailing spaces; LOWER folds case. Without this, the query under-reports duplicates — the most dangerous kind of wrong, because it looks clean.

From "which emails" to "which rows to delete"

The given query names the bad emails but not the surplus rows. The natural follow-up — "keep the earliest signup, flag the rest" — needs ROW_NUMBER:

SELECT user_id, email, registered_at
FROM (
  SELECT user_id, email, registered_at,
         ROW_NUMBER() OVER (PARTITION BY LOWER(email)
                            ORDER BY registered_at) AS rn
  FROM registrations
) t
WHERE rn > 1;          -- rn = 1 is the keeper; rn > 1 are the duplicates

For pat this returns user_id 3 and 4 (the later two); user_id 1, the earliest, is kept. That is the row set a dedup job actually operates on.

NULL emails and the count semantics

COUNT(*) counts rows regardless of NULLs, so two rows with a NULL email do form a group of 2 and would surface as duplicates — even though SQL normally treats NULL as "not equal to anything". GROUP BY is the exception: it puts all NULLs in one bucket. Decide whether NULL-email rows are real duplicates or junk to filter with WHERE email IS NOT NULL first. Note also that COUNT(email) would behave differently — it skips NULLs — so the choice of COUNT(*) here is deliberate.

Edge case: no duplicates at all

If every email is unique, every group fails HAVING COUNT(*) > 1 and the query returns zero rows — not a NULL, not a zero. A monitoring job should treat "0 rows" as the healthy state, not as a failed query.

Senior signal: explain that COUNT(*) > 1 must sit in HAVING because the count only exists post-grouping, normalise email with LOWER(TRIM(…)) so case and whitespace variants do not hide real duplicates, and extend to ROW_NUMBER when the task shifts from "which emails" to "which rows to remove".
