The Pareto principle stated as SQL: "do 20% of users / products / customers really account for 80% of usage / sales / revenue?" Netflix's infra team asks this constantly because the answer drives caching strategy. If 20% of subscribers drive 80% of bandwidth, you can warm the edge cache for those 20% and the other 80% inherit the win. The SQL pattern is a running total joined to a global total — looks innocent, costs nothing on a tiny table, becomes the most-optimised query in the warehouse at scale.

1. The source data — walmart (or viewing_activity in a streaming context)

productidnamesales
P01Whale Product A$500,000
P02Whale Product B$300,000
P03Heavy Product C$100,000
P04Mid Product D$50,000
P05Mid Product E$25,000
P06 – P50Long-tail products~$500 each

The top 3 products contribute $900K out of a $1M total — that's 90% of revenue from 6% of products. Pareto-shaped curve.

2. The SQL — running total ÷ global total

WITH percent_sales AS (
    SELECT *,
        SUM(sales) OVER (ORDER BY sales DESC, productid ASC)     AS cumulative_sales,
        SUM(sales) OVER ()                                       AS total_sales
    FROM walmart
),
cum_percent AS (
    SELECT productid, name, sales,
        100.0 * cumulative_sales / total_sales                  AS cumulative_pct
    FROM percent_sales
)
SELECT * FROM cum_percent WHERE cumulative_pct <= 80;

Three moves: order by sales desc, running sum from biggest to smallest, divide by the global total. The rows where cumulative_pct <= 80 are the products that collectively make up 80% of revenue — the "vital few" in Pareto's terminology.

3. Strategic explanations — what the result actually means

The senior framing: this isn't a leaderboard, it's a curve diagnostic

If 20% of products give you 80%, you have a healthy Pareto curve — concentrate inventory / cache / marketing on the top tier. If 5% of products give you 95%, you have extreme skew — risk of single-product dependency (e.g. one show drives Netflix retention, cancel it and the platform empties). If 60% of products give you 80%, you don't really have a power-law distribution — every product matters, treat them uniformly.

4. The interviewer's edge-case probes

Probe 1 — "Why is the ORDER BY sales DESC, productid ASC tiebreaker there?"

Without a deterministic tiebreaker, the running sum is non-deterministic on ties. Two products with $500 each could land in either order between query runs — and the cumulative-pct boundary shifts. Always add a stable tiebreaker (productid) so the same input data produces the same output.

Probe 2 — "What if the boundary case falls partly inside 80%?"

Say the top 19 products give you 78%, and product 20 gives you another 6% — together 84%. Does product 20 belong in the "80%" cohort? Two answers, both defensible:

  • Inclusive boundary: include product 20 because without it you don't reach 80%. Use cumulative_pct <= 80 OR (LAG(cumulative_pct) OVER (...) <= 80).
  • Exclusive boundary: stop at product 19 even though you're at 78%. The classic WHERE cumulative_pct <= 80.

Surface this in the interview — most users mean "include the row that crosses the threshold".

Probe 3 — "Translate this from products to Netflix viewers. What's the question?"

"What percentage of Netflix subscribers account for 80% of total watch time?" Same query, swap the table:

WITH user_totals AS (
    SELECT user_id, SUM(watch_seconds) / 3600.0 AS total_hours
    FROM viewing_activity
    GROUP BY user_id
),
ranked AS (
    SELECT *,
        SUM(total_hours) OVER (ORDER BY total_hours DESC, user_id ASC) AS cum_hours,
        SUM(total_hours) OVER ()                                       AS global_hours,
        PERCENT_RANK() OVER (ORDER BY total_hours DESC)                AS user_pctile
    FROM user_totals
)
SELECT user_id, total_hours,
       100.0 * cum_hours / global_hours AS pct_of_global_impact,
       user_pctile
FROM ranked
WHERE user_pctile <= 0.20;     -- top 20% of viewers

If pct_of_global_impact sits at ~85% when user_pctile = 0.20, you've confirmed the 80/20 skew empirically and you have justification for the edge-cache investment.

Probe 4 — "At billions of rows, does this query still work?"

The SUM() OVER () unbounded window forces the engine to materialise the global total once — fine. The running SUM() OVER (ORDER BY sales DESC) sorts the entire partition — at billions of rows this spills to disk on most engines. Production rewrites:

  • Pre-aggregate — never run this on raw events; pre-roll up to fct_user_total_hours_daily first.
  • Approximate Pareto — use APPROX_PERCENTILE (Trino, Snowflake) or compute the running total on a SAMPLE of the user base (1% of users gives the curve within ±1pp).
  • Pre-bucket — assign decile via NTILE(10) at ETL time, then the 80/20 question becomes "what's the cumulative share of deciles 1+2?" — a constant-time query over a 10-row table.
Probe 5 — "What if the long tail has 10M+ products and you only need the top 20%?"

Don't run a window function over all 10M. Compute the global total in one pass, then top-N with a cumulative running total only over the top-K candidates:

WITH g AS (SELECT SUM(sales) AS total FROM walmart),
top AS (SELECT * FROM walmart ORDER BY sales DESC LIMIT 1000000)   -- top-K guess
SELECT productid, name,
       SUM(sales) OVER (ORDER BY sales DESC, productid ASC) AS cum,
       (SELECT total FROM g)                                AS total
FROM top
WHERE SUM(sales) OVER (ORDER BY sales DESC, productid ASC) <= 0.80 * (SELECT total FROM g);
Probe 6 — "How does this interact with late-arriving dimensions (LAD)?"

If a power-user's most-watched-show metadata is still propagating, their total hours can be temporarily underestimated — they look like a mid-tier viewer for a few hours. The 80/20 result then drifts. Lag-tolerant view pattern: include an is_metadata_complete flag on each user-day row; exclude incomplete rows from power-user reports. False-negatives ("we missed a whale for 6 hours") beat false-positives ("we promoted a non-whale and warmed the wrong cache").

Senior signal: name the deterministic tiebreaker, surface the boundary-inclusion question, contrast inclusive vs exclusive 80%, propose pre-aggregation + decile pre-bucketing as the scale-up answer, and pre-empt the LAD trap with a lag-tolerant view. Bonus: cite Pareto's original observation (1896 Italian land ownership) — interviewers love the history.
