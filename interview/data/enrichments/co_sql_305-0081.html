Snowflake's field-sales analytics team wants to know which regions are punching above their weight — regions whose total revenue beats the average region. That phrase "the average" hides a subtlety: average of what? The average of every sale, or the average of the per-region totals? The reference solution picks one, and the interviewer wants to hear you say which, and why it changes the answer.

1. The source data — sales

regionamount
North100
North200
South50
East900
West60
West40

Four regions. East has a single huge sale; South has a single tiny one; North and West each have two. The lopsided East total is the edge case — one outlier region drags the average up and decides who clears the bar.

2. The SQL — region total > average region total

SELECT region, total FROM (
  SELECT region, SUM(amount) AS total FROM sales GROUP BY region
) s WHERE total > (SELECT AVG(total) FROM (
  SELECT SUM(amount) AS total FROM sales GROUP BY region
) x);

The inner derived table s gives one total per region. The scalar subquery repeats the same per-region grouping, then averages those totals — the comparison baseline is the average of region totals, not the average of individual sales.

3. The resulting analysis

regiontotal
East900

Region totals are North 300, South 50, East 900, West 100. Their average is (300 + 50 + 900 + 100) ÷ 4 = 337.5. Only East (900) clears it. North at 300 falls just short — the lone East outlier pulls the bar above three of the four regions.

4. Strategic explanations

"Average of totals" vs "average of rows" — they are not the same

The solution compares against the average of the four region totals (337.5). A natural-sounding alternative compares against the average of all six individual sales:

SELECT region, SUM(amount) AS total
FROM sales GROUP BY region
HAVING SUM(amount) > (SELECT AVG(amount) FROM sales);
-- AVG of 6 raw sales = 1350/6 = 225; North(300) and East(900) both qualify

Same data, different result — North now clears the bar. Neither is "wrong"; they answer different questions. The senior move is to state the choice: "I'm benchmarking each region against the typical region, so I average the region totals."

Collapsing the repeated subquery with a CTE

The solution computes the per-region grouping twice — once for s, once inside the scalar subquery. A CTE names it once and reads far better:

WITH region_totals AS (
  SELECT region, SUM(amount) AS total FROM sales GROUP BY region
)
SELECT region, total FROM region_totals
WHERE total > (SELECT AVG(total) FROM region_totals);

Logically identical, but a reviewer sees the intent immediately and there is only one place to change the grouping.

The HAVING shortcut and why it cannot do the whole job

HAVING filters groups against an aggregate — but only an aggregate of the same query. It can compare a region's total to AVG(amount) over raw rows in one statement. It cannot compare a region's total to the average of other regions' totals, because that benchmark is itself an aggregate-of-aggregates. That two-level structure is exactly why the subquery (or CTE) is unavoidable for the solution's interpretation.

The outlier sensitivity — a metric-design red flag

One giant sale in East lifted the threshold from "above 100" territory to 337.5 and silently disqualified North. A mean is not robust to outliers. If the goal is "which regions are typical-or-better", a senior analyst raises whether the median region total is the fairer benchmark — with this data the median of {50, 100, 300, 900} is 200, and both North and East would clear it. Flagging mean-vs-median sensitivity is the over-the-bar observation.

NULL amounts

SUM skips NULL amount values, and a region whose every sale is NULL would produce SUM = NULL; NULL > 337.5 is unknown, so that region is excluded rather than treated as zero. If a region with no real revenue should count as 0 in the average, wrap the inner sum in COALESCE(SUM(amount), 0).

Senior signal: name the benchmark explicitly — average of region totals, not average of raw sales — since the two give different answers, fold the duplicated grouping into a single CTE, explain why HAVING alone cannot express an aggregate-of-aggregates, and raise mean-vs-median sensitivity when one outlier region distorts the threshold.
