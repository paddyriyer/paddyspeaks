A Community question in the Amazon-logistics mould: count shipments per calendar month. It looks like a two-second GROUP BY, but the schema hides a sharp edge — a shipment is identified by the pair (shipment_id, sub_id), not shipment_id alone. Miss that and you either double-count split shipments or collapse distinct ones. The interviewer is checking whether you read the grain before you write the aggregate.

1. The source data — amazon_shipment

Two things to spot. shipment_id = 1 appears with sub_id 1 and 2 — one order split into two parcels, so two distinct shipments. And row 4 is an exact duplicate of row 1: same (1, 1) pair. COUNT(DISTINCT (shipment_id, sub_id)) must treat the split as two and the duplicate as one.

2. The SQL — GROUP BY year, month

SELECT
    EXTRACT(YEAR  FROM shipment_date)::INT AS year,
    EXTRACT(MONTH FROM shipment_date)::INT AS month,
    COUNT(DISTINCT (shipment_id, sub_id)) AS shipment_count
FROM amazon_shipment
GROUP BY EXTRACT(YEAR FROM shipment_date), EXTRACT(MONTH FROM shipment_date)
ORDER BY year, month;

EXTRACT pulls the year and month numbers; grouping on both keeps January 2023 and January 2024 separate. COUNT(DISTINCT (a, b)) counts distinct row-value tuples.

4. Strategic explanations

Why group on year and month

EXTRACT(MONTH FROM …) returns 1–12 with no year attached, so grouping by month alone folds every January of every year into one bucket. "Shipments per month" almost always means per calendar month within a year — a time series, not a seasonal average. An equivalent, often tidier formulation is date_trunc('month', shipment_date), which yields one timestamp per month and sorts naturally:

SELECT date_trunc('month', shipment_date) AS month, COUNT(DISTINCT (shipment_id, sub_id))
FROM amazon_shipment GROUP BY 1 ORDER BY 1;
The grain trap — COUNT(*) vs COUNT(DISTINCT shipment_id)

Three tempting wrong counts: COUNT(*) counts rows, so it inflates Jan 2023 to 4 by including the exact duplicate. COUNT(DISTINCT shipment_id) counts orders, collapsing the (1,1)/(1,2) split into one and undercounting to 2. Only COUNT(DISTINCT (shipment_id, sub_id)) matches the stated unit — a parcel. The lesson: pin down what one row of the answer represents before choosing the count.

How COUNT(DISTINCT (a, b)) handles NULLs

Postgres treats the row constructor specially: a tuple is excluded from COUNT(DISTINCT) only if every field is NULL. A pair like (7, NULL) still counts. So a missing sub_id on a real shipment is preserved — usually what you want — but two rows that are both (7, NULL) are treated as one. Worth a sentence if sub_id is nullable.

The missing-month gap

March 2023 has no shipments, so it produces no row — the result skips straight from February to the next populated month. A dashboard plotting this will draw a misleading continuous line. If gap-free output is required, generate the month spine and LEFT JOIN:

SELECT g.m, COUNT(DISTINCT (s.shipment_id, s.sub_id)) AS shipment_count
FROM generate_series('2023-01-01'::date, '2024-01-01', '1 month') g(m)
LEFT JOIN amazon_shipment s
  ON date_trunc('month', s.shipment_date) = g.m
GROUP BY g.m ORDER BY g.m;

Aggregation can only count rows that exist; absent months need a spine.

Casting and ordering

EXTRACT returns numeric in Postgres; the ::INT cast just tidies the output type. And ORDER BY year, month is essential — without an explicit sort the result set has no guaranteed order, and a time series out of order is worse than useless.

Senior signal: identify the shipment grain as the (shipment_id, sub_id) pair and reject both COUNT(*) and COUNT(DISTINCT shipment_id), group on year and month so calendar years stay distinct, and pre-empt the missing-month gap with a generate_series spine when the output feeds a chart.
