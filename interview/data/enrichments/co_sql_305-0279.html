Okta's compensation team is building department-level pay bands for the next review cycle. They don't just want the average — they want a normal range: the salary band you'd expect most of a department to fall inside. The reference solution answers it with a one-pass standard-deviation band, mean ± σ — and the way it computes σ is the part worth slowing down on.

Three departments with deliberately different shapes: dept 10 has three salaries spread around $100K, dept 30 has two, and dept 20 has a single employee. That one-row group is the edge case that quietly breaks naive solutions.

2. The SQL — AVG(x*x) − AVG(x)*AVG(x)

WITH d AS (
  SELECT dept_id, AVG(salary) AS mu, AVG(salary*salary) - AVG(salary)*AVG(salary) AS var_pop
  FROM employees GROUP BY dept_id
)
SELECT dept_id, mu AS avg_salary,
  CASE WHEN var_pop > 0 THEN ROUND(mu - SQRT(var_pop), 2) ELSE mu END AS lower_bound,
  CASE WHEN var_pop > 0 THEN ROUND(mu + SQRT(var_pop), 2) ELSE mu END AS upper_bound
FROM d ORDER BY dept_id;

The CTE computes two things per department: the mean mu, and var_pop — the population variance — via the algebraic identity Var(X) = E[X²] − E[X]². The outer query turns that into a band mu ± SQRT(var_pop), which is the mean plus or minus one population standard deviation.

4. Strategic explanations

The E[X²] − E[X]² identity

Variance is defined as the mean squared deviation, E[(X − μ)²]. Expanding the square gives the equivalent E[X²] − E[X]² — the mean of the squares minus the square of the mean. The payoff is that it needs only AVG(salary) and AVG(salary*salary), both computable in a single pass over the rows, with no self-join back to the per-department mean. That is exactly why the formula appears in interview answers.

Why this is the population variance, not the sample variance

This identity divides the squared spread by N (the count of rows). The sample variance divides by N − 1 (Bessel's correction) to stay unbiased when the rows are a sample of a larger population. Pick deliberately: a department's roster is the whole population, so VAR_POP is defensible here. If an interviewer pushes, name both — and prefer the built-ins:

SELECT dept_id,
  AVG(salary)                          AS mu,
  STDDEV_POP(salary)                   AS sigma_pop,   -- divides by N
  STDDEV_SAMP(salary)                  AS sigma_samp   -- divides by N-1
FROM employees GROUP BY dept_id;

AVG(salary) ± STDDEV_POP(salary) says the same thing as the hand-rolled CTE, in one line, and the engine handles the numerics for you.

The single-employee department — and why var_pop > 0 is guarded

Dept 20 has one row, so its variance is genuinely 0 and SQRT(0) is a clean 0. So why the CASE WHEN var_pop > 0 guard at all? Floating-point. E[X²] − E[X]² subtracts two large, nearly-equal numbers — catastrophic cancellation — and can land on a tiny negative value like -0.0000001 for a department that is mathematically zero-variance. SQRT of a negative argument raises an error. The guard turns that failure into a sensible lower_bound = upper_bound = mu.

A wart to call out: inconsistent rounding

Look closely at the output. The bounds for depts 10 and 30 run through ROUND(…, 2), but dept 20 takes the ELSE mu branch — which returns mu unrounded. And avg_salary is never rounded at all. In a real engine those columns come back with a long decimal tail. A senior answer wraps the mean too: ROUND(mu, 2) AS avg_salary, and rounds inside both CASE branches so every row is formatted identically.

Senior signal: derive the band from E[X²] − E[X]² and explain the single-pass advantage, distinguish population from sample variance and reach for STDDEV_POP rather than reinventing it, pre-empt the SQRT-of-negative failure that the var_pop > 0 guard exists to absorb, and tidy the rounding so every column is formatted consistently.
