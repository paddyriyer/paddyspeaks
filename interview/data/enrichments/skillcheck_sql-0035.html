A pizza chain wants to find customers who have tried every pizza on the menu — the textbook relational division problem ("find rows related to all values in a set"). The interviewer is probing whether you know the count-matching idiom and whether you handle the two traps: a customer who ordered the same pizza twice, and an empty menu.

1. The source data — pizzas and orders

customer_idpizzas ordereddistinct pizzas(narrative)
11, 2, 3, 13all 3 — but pizza 1 ordered twice (the edge case)

The menu has 3 pizzas (Margherita, Meatlovers, Vegetarian). Customer 1 placed four orders but only three distinct pizzas — the duplicate Margherita must not be counted twice, or a customer could "qualify" by ordering one pizza three times.

2. The SQL — relational division by count matching

SELECT customer_id
FROM orders
GROUP BY customer_id
HAVING COUNT(DISTINCT pizza_id) = (SELECT COUNT(*) FROM pizzas)
ORDER BY customer_id;

Group orders by customer; COUNT(DISTINCT pizza_id) is how many different pizzas that customer has tried. The HAVING keeps a customer only when that count equals the total number of pizzas on the menu — i.e. they have covered the full set.

3. The resulting analysis

customer_id(commentary)
13 distinct pizzas = 3 on menu — duplicate Margherita collapsed by DISTINCT
33 distinct = 3 — qualifies

Customers 1 and 3 qualify. Customer 1 makes the point: four order rows, but COUNT(DISTINCT pizza_id) = 3, so the duplicate does not inflate the count and does not falsely exclude or include anyone. Customer 2 (only 2 distinct) and customer 4 (only 1) are correctly dropped.

4. Strategic explanations

Why DISTINCT inside COUNT is non-negotiable

Plain COUNT(pizza_id) counts order rows. Customer 1 has 4 rows, which would falsely exceed the menu size of 3 — and the = test would fail, wrongly excluding a customer who genuinely tried everything. Worse, a customer who ordered one pizza three times would have COUNT = 3 and falsely qualify. COUNT(DISTINCT pizza_id) counts coverage, which is what "at least one of every pizza" means.

Why count-matching implements "for all"

Relational division asks: does this customer's pizza set contain the menu set? Since a customer can only order pizzas that exist, their distinct-pizza set is always a subset of the menu. A subset that has the same cardinality as the superset must be the superset. So COUNT(DISTINCT pizza_id) = (SELECT COUNT(*) FROM pizzas) is exactly the "ordered all of them" test — no need to enumerate which ones.

The double-NOT-EXISTS alternative

The pure-logic form is "no pizza exists that this customer has not ordered": WHERE NOT EXISTS (SELECT 1 FROM pizzas p WHERE NOT EXISTS (SELECT 1 FROM orders o WHERE o.customer_id = c.id AND o.pizza_id = p.pizza_id)). It is the literal translation of universal quantification and handles the empty-menu case naturally, but it is harder to read and often slower. The count-matching idiom is the idiomatic, performant answer when the divisor is a simple table.

The empty-menu edge case

If pizzas is empty, (SELECT COUNT(*) FROM pizzas) is 0. The HAVING becomes COUNT(DISTINCT pizza_id) = 0 — but any customer in the GROUP BY has at least one order, so their count is ≥ 1, and nobody is returned. The spec says every customer should "trivially qualify" when the menu is empty. The count idiom does not deliver that — it only sees customers who appear in orders. The double-NOT-EXISTS form, driven off a customers table, correctly returns everyone (the inner "for all" over an empty set is vacuously true). Worth naming this gap in an interview.

Senior signal: solve relational division with HAVING COUNT(DISTINCT key) = (SELECT COUNT(*) FROM divisor) — the DISTINCT turns row counts into set coverage so duplicates can neither inflate nor exclude; know the double-NOT-EXISTS equivalent and that it, not count-matching, handles the vacuous empty-divisor case.
