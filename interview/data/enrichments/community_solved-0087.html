A product analytics team wants the two products that earn the most revenue per unique buyer — not raw revenue, but revenue normalised by how many distinct people bought. A product can gross a lot simply by having many customers; this metric surfaces the ones where each customer is worth the most. The interviewer is checking COUNT(DISTINCT) in a ratio and whether you handle the "previous year" window precisely.

Product 500 has a repeat buyer (user 10 appears twice) — the test of COUNT(DISTINCT). Product 800's huge sale is dated 2024, outside the rolling window, and must be excluded.

2. The SQL — revenue divided by distinct buyers

SELECT product_id,
       SUM(amount) AS total_amount,
       COUNT(DISTINCT user_id) AS unique_users,
       SUM(amount)/COUNT(DISTINCT user_id) AS revenue_per_unique_user
FROM purchases
WHERE purchase_date >= CURRENT_DATE - INTERVAL '1 year'
GROUP BY product_id
ORDER BY revenue_per_unique_user DESC
LIMIT 2;

4. Strategic explanations

Why COUNT(DISTINCT user_id) is the crux

The metric is revenue per unique buyer. COUNT(user_id) would count purchase rows, double-counting a repeat buyer — product 500 would show 3 "users" and a misleadingly low $150/user. COUNT(DISTINCT user_id) counts distinct people: 2. The whole question hinges on choosing the distinct count, and the repeat-buyer row exists precisely to catch a candidate who forgets it.

Integer vs numeric division — check the column types

SUM(amount)/COUNT(DISTINCT user_id) is safe here because amount is numeric, so the division is numeric. But if amount were an integer column, SUM would be a bigint and bigint / bigint truncates — $450 / 2 would still be 225, but $451 / 2 would silently become 225, not 225.5. Defensive habit: write SUM(amount) * 1.0 / COUNT(DISTINCT user_id) or cast, so the ratio is always exact regardless of column type.

The single-buyer edge — division by one, and division by zero

Product 600 has exactly one unique buyer, so the ratio equals the revenue — fine. The risk is a denominator of zero: with this aggregation it cannot happen (a product only appears in a group if it has at least one purchase, hence at least one user). But the moment the query is changed — an outer join, a different grouping — a zero denominator becomes possible. Wrapping it as / NULLIF(COUNT(DISTINCT user_id), 0) is cheap insurance and shows you anticipate the failure mode.

"Previous year" — rolling window vs calendar year

purchase_date >= CURRENT_DATE - INTERVAL '1 year' is a rolling trailing-12-months window: from 2026-05-16 it keeps everything back to 2025-05-16. But "previous year" often means the prior calendar year — all of 2025, i.e. EXTRACT(YEAR FROM purchase_date) = EXTRACT(YEAR FROM CURRENT_DATE) - 1. The two windows give different results (a purchase from 2025-03 is in the calendar year but not the rolling window). Always pin down which definition the stakeholder means — this ambiguity is a classic interview probe.

What to say next — LIMIT ties and the metric's blind spot

ORDER BY revenue_per_unique_user DESC LIMIT 2 is non-deterministic if products tie on the ratio — add a tie-breaker (, total_amount DESC, product_id) or use RANK() if all tied products should be returned. Deeper point: revenue-per-unique-user rewards niche products with one whale and punishes broad-reach products — product 600 "wins" on a sample of one customer. A senior answer flags this small-sample fragility and suggests a minimum-buyer threshold (HAVING COUNT(DISTINCT user_id) >= 10) before ranking.

Senior signal: use COUNT(DISTINCT user_id) so repeat buyers are not double-counted, force numeric division and guard the denominator with NULLIF, and pin down "previous year" — rolling 12 months vs prior calendar year are different windows. Flag that a per-unique-user metric is fragile on tiny buyer counts.
