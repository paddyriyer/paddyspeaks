Tesla's customer-insights team wants to flag purchases that are unusually large for that customer — a buyer who normally spends $100 placing a $300 order. The phrase "for that customer" is the whole problem: the comparison baseline is not a global average, it is each customer's own average, and that is exactly what a correlated subquery delivers.

Customer 1's average is 200, so only the $300 row clears it. Customer 2's two purchases are identical — both equal the average of 50, so neither is strictly above it. Customer 3 has a single purchase that is its own average. Those last two are the edge cases that decide the answer.

2. The SQL — correlated subquery per customer

SELECT customer_id, amount FROM purchases p
WHERE amount > (SELECT AVG(amount) FROM purchases
                WHERE customer_id = p.customer_id);

For each outer row p, the subquery recomputes the average over just that customer's purchases. The WHERE customer_id = p.customer_id link — the correlation — is what scopes the baseline to "this customer".

3. The resulting analysis

customer_idamount
1300

Exactly one row. Customer 1's $300 beats their $200 average. Customer 1's $100 and $200 do not. Customer 2's $50 purchases tie their $50 average — > is strict, so a tie fails. Customer 3's lone $80 equals its own one-row average of $80 — also a tie, also excluded. A single-purchase customer can never appear, because one value is always exactly its own mean.

4. Strategic explanations

The strict-vs-inclusive boundary

amount > AVG excludes purchases that exactly equal the average. That is why customer 2 and customer 3 produce nothing. Whether that is correct is a product decision — "above average" usually means strictly above, but confirm it. If at-average purchases should count, switch to >=; for our data that would add both of customer 2's rows and customer 3's row.

The window-function rewrite — one pass instead of N

The correlated subquery re-scans the table once per row. A window function computes every customer's average in a single pass:

SELECT customer_id, amount FROM (
  SELECT customer_id, amount,
         AVG(amount) OVER (PARTITION BY customer_id) AS cust_avg
  FROM purchases
) t
WHERE amount > cust_avg;

AVG(amount) OVER (PARTITION BY customer_id) attaches each customer's mean to every one of their rows; the outer query then filters. Same result, but O(n log n) instead of O(n·rows-per-customer). On a large purchases table this is the answer you want — the correlated form is the one to recognise, the window form is the one to ship.

Correlated subquery vs GROUP BY join

A third equivalent: aggregate once into a derived table and join back.

SELECT p.customer_id, p.amount
FROM purchases p
JOIN (SELECT customer_id, AVG(amount) AS cust_avg
      FROM purchases GROUP BY customer_id) a
  ON a.customer_id = p.customer_id
WHERE p.amount > a.cust_avg;

The join form is often what a planner rewrites the correlated subquery into anyway. All three return the single $300 row — knowing they are equivalent, and which one a query planner prefers, is the senior signal.

NULL amounts

If a purchase had a NULL amount, two things happen: AVG skips it (the baseline ignores NULL), and NULL > anything is unknown, so that row is filtered out. A NULL purchase silently disappears — usually fine, but state it. If NULL means "$0 sale" you would need COALESCE(amount, 0) in both the subquery and the comparison.

The self-comparison subtlety

The subquery's average includes the very row being tested. So a customer with two purchases, $100 and $300, has an average of $200, and the $300 clears it. If the business wants each purchase compared against the customer's other purchases (a leave-one-out average), you must exclude the current row — AND purchase_id <> p.purchase_id — which needs a unique key the bare purchases(customer_id, amount) schema does not provide. Flag that the schema lacks a row identifier.

Senior signal: read "for that customer" as a per-customer baseline, know that > excludes at-average rows so single-purchase customers never appear, and offer the AVG() OVER (PARTITION BY …) rewrite that replaces N table scans with one — while noting the baseline includes the row under test.
