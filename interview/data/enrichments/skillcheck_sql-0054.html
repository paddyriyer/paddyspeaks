A retention analyst wants to find customers who shop across the working week rather than always on the same day: "which customers placed orders on at least three distinct weekdays?" Five orders all on Mondays still count as one weekday. The interviewer is probing whether you can collapse dates down to a coarser granularity (day-of-week) and then count distinct values of that derived attribute — not distinct dates.

Customer 1 has four orders but two of them fall on a Monday — the dedup edge case. Customer 4 is a single-order group. Both test whether the count is over weekdays, not over rows or dates.

2. The SQL — COUNT(DISTINCT EXTRACT(ISODOW)) with HAVING

SELECT customer_id,
       COUNT(DISTINCT EXTRACT(ISODOW FROM order_date)) AS weekdays_covered
FROM orders
GROUP BY customer_id
HAVING COUNT(DISTINCT EXTRACT(ISODOW FROM order_date)) >= 3
ORDER BY weekdays_covered DESC, customer_id;

EXTRACT(ISODOW FROM order_date) maps each date to 1–7 (Mon–Sun). COUNT(DISTINCT …) over that derived value counts unique weekdays per customer; HAVING keeps only customers clearing the threshold of 3.

Customer 1 makes the cut at exactly 3 even with four orders — the second Monday collapses. Customers 2 (Mon/Tue = 2) and 4 (Mon = 1) fall below the threshold and are filtered out by HAVING.

4. Strategic explanations

Why COUNT(DISTINCT) is the heart of the query

The business rule is "distinct weekdays", and the order of operations is: first derive the weekday from each date, then deduplicate. A plain COUNT(*) would count rows (customer 1 → 4); COUNT(DISTINCT order_date) would count calendar days (still 4 — the two Mondays are different dates). Only COUNT(DISTINCT EXTRACT(ISODOW …)) counts the thing the question asks for. Choosing what to apply DISTINCT to is the problem.

Why ISODOW and not DOW

PostgreSQL has two day-of-week extractors. DOW returns 0–6 with Sunday = 0; ISODOW returns 1–7 with Monday = 1, Sunday = 7. The question explicitly specifies ISO numbering, so ISODOW is correct. The distinct count happens to be identical either way — both still distinguish seven weekdays — but if any downstream logic compares against specific numbers (e.g. "weekends"), picking the wrong one silently shifts every day by one slot.

HAVING vs WHERE

The threshold filters on an aggregate (a per-group count), so it must live in HAVING, which runs after GROUP BY. WHERE runs before grouping and cannot see COUNT(...) — putting the condition there raises "aggregate functions are not allowed in WHERE". A cleaner alternative that avoids repeating the expression is to compute it once in a subquery/CTE and filter the outer query: SELECT * FROM (…) t WHERE weekdays_covered >= 3.

The repeated expression — a real-world smell

The COUNT(DISTINCT EXTRACT(...)) expression appears twice: once in SELECT, once in HAVING. That works but is brittle — change the rule in one place and forget the other. PostgreSQL does not let HAVING reference the SELECT alias weekdays_covered. Wrapping the aggregation in a CTE and filtering outside removes the duplication and is what a senior would write for maintainability.

NULL dates

If order_date can be NULL, EXTRACT yields NULL and COUNT(DISTINCT) silently ignores it — a NULL-dated order simply does not contribute a weekday. Usually harmless here, but worth stating: a customer whose only "extra" orders have NULL dates will not reach the threshold on those.

Senior signal: recognise that the count must be over the derived weekday and deduplicated — COUNT(DISTINCT EXTRACT(ISODOW FROM order_date)), not rows or dates — use ISODOW for Monday-based 1–7 numbering, put the aggregate threshold in HAVING, and prefer a CTE to avoid repeating the expression in both SELECT and HAVING.
