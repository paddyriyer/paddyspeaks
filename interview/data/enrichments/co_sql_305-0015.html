Amazon's people-analytics team wants a hiring-velocity dashboard: for every department, how many people joined each month. It reads like a trivial GROUP BY, and it is — but the interviewer is watching for two things: how you bucket a raw hire_date into a month, and whether you join through departments for the human-readable name or just group on a bare dept_id.

1. The source data — employees & departments

Engineering hired twice in January (the 5th and the 20th) — different days, same month bucket. That collapse is the whole point: the query must treat any January date as one period.

2. The SQL — DATE_TRUNC + GROUP BY

SELECT d.dept_name, DATE_TRUNC('month', e.hire_date)::DATE AS month, COUNT(*) AS hires
FROM employees e JOIN departments d ON e.dept_id = d.dept_id
GROUP BY d.dept_name, DATE_TRUNC('month', e.hire_date)::DATE
ORDER BY month, d.dept_name;

DATE_TRUNC('month', …) snaps every date down to the first of its month, so the two January hires land in the same group. The ::DATE cast strips the time component that DATE_TRUNC returns. Grouping by department name and that truncated month gives one count per (department, month) pair.

4. Strategic explanations

The missing zeros — the gap-filling follow-up

This is the question the interviewer always asks: "Sales hired nobody in March — why doesn't the dashboard show a 0?" Because GROUP BY can only produce groups that have rows. A month with no hires for a department generates no row to count. If the dashboard needs a continuous grid, you must manufacture the (department × month) skeleton and LEFT JOIN the hires onto it:

SELECT d.dept_name, m.month, COUNT(e.emp_id) AS hires
FROM departments d
CROSS JOIN generate_series(
  DATE '2024-01-01', DATE '2024-03-01', INTERVAL '1 month'
) AS m(month)
LEFT JOIN employees e
  ON e.dept_id = d.dept_id
 AND DATE_TRUNC('month', e.hire_date) = m.month
GROUP BY d.dept_name, m.month
ORDER BY m.month, d.dept_name;

Note COUNT(e.emp_id), not COUNT(*) — on the unmatched (Sales, March) row the LEFT JOIN yields a NULL emp_id, and COUNT(column) skips NULLs to give a true 0; COUNT(*) would wrongly return 1.

Why DATE_TRUNC beats EXTRACT

A tempting alternative is GROUP BY EXTRACT(YEAR FROM hire_date), EXTRACT(MONTH FROM hire_date). It works, but you get two integer columns instead of one sortable date, and ORDER BY across a year boundary needs both. DATE_TRUNC returns a real date that sorts correctly and renders cleanly. TO_CHAR(hire_date, 'YYYY-MM') gives a tidy label too — but it is text, so December 2024 sorts before January 2024 only by luck of the YYYY-MM prefix; never group on a month name.

INNER JOIN drops empty departments

The solution uses an inner JOIN. A department in departments that has hired nobody — a brand-new cost center — produces zero employee rows and vanishes entirely from the report. If "show every department even at zero" is the requirement, the join must be departments LEFT JOIN employees. Confirm whether the dashboard is hire-driven or roster-driven.

Edge case: NULL hire_date and timezone

An employee row with a NULL hire_date truncates to NULL and forms its own ghost group — a row with a blank month. Filter with WHERE e.hire_date IS NOT NULL if that is noise. And if hire_date were a timestamptz, DATE_TRUNC buckets in the session's timezone — a hire at 11pm UTC on Jan 31 could land in February for a US-Pacific viewer. For pure dates this does not bite, but name it.

Senior signal: reach for DATE_TRUNC to get one sortable month column, explain that GROUP BY cannot invent zero-rows and offer the generate_series + LEFT JOIN + COUNT(col) gap-fill, and flag that the inner join silently hides departments that have never hired.
