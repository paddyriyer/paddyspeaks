TikTok's growth team measures friction in the signup funnel: after a user signs up, how long until they confirm their account? The metric is "average minutes to confirm". The SQL is a time-difference aggregate — and the interview is about handling the people who never confirmed, and about reading an expression that does more arithmetic than it needs to.

1. The source data — signups

User 4 signed up and never confirmed — confirmed_at is NULL. User 3 took a full day (1440 minutes), an outlier next to the others' few minutes. User 5's 2.5 minutes proves the metric must keep sub-minute fractions.

2. The SQL — EXTRACT(EPOCH ...) averaged

SELECT AVG(((EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (confirmed_at - signup_at)) / 86400.0) * 86400)/60)
       AS avg_minutes_to_confirm
FROM signups WHERE confirmed_at IS NOT NULL;

confirmed_at - signup_at is an interval; EXTRACT(EPOCH ...) turns it into seconds; the arithmetic converts seconds to minutes; AVG takes the mean over confirmed users only.

3. The resulting analysis

369.37499999999999999760

Four confirmed users: 5, 30, 1440, and 2.5 minutes — summing to 1477.5, divided by 4 = 369.375 minutes. User 4 contributed nothing: the WHERE confirmed_at IS NOT NULL filter removed them before the average. The trailing-9s tail is Postgres numeric precision noise. And notice the headline: one user's 24-hour delay dragged a "few minutes" reality up to six hours — the mean is fragile here.

4. Strategic explanations

The arithmetic is a no-op — read it carefully

Look at (EXTRACT(EPOCH ...) / 86400.0) * 86400: dividing by 86400 and then multiplying by 86400 cancels exactly. The whole expression reduces to EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (confirmed_at - signup_at)) / 60 — seconds to minutes, full stop. The detour through "days" computes nothing. The clean, equivalent query:

SELECT AVG(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (confirmed_at - signup_at)) / 60)
       AS avg_minutes_to_confirm
FROM signups WHERE confirmed_at IS NOT NULL;

An interviewer wants you to say the round-trip cancels — spotting that dead arithmetic is the senior read. (It is harmless, just noise; not a correctness bug.)

The non-confirmers — an averaging decision, not just a filter

The WHERE confirmed_at IS NOT NULL guard is necessary — without it, confirmed_at - signup_at is NULL for user 4 and AVG would skip it anyway, but the filter makes intent explicit. The deeper question: should non-confirmers be excluded? Excluding them answers "among people who confirmed, how fast?" But if 40% never confirm, that average flatters the funnel badly. The honest dashboard reports two numbers side by side:

SELECT
  AVG(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (confirmed_at - signup_at))/60)
    FILTER (WHERE confirmed_at IS NOT NULL) AS avg_minutes_to_confirm,
  COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE confirmed_at IS NOT NULL)::numeric
    / COUNT(*)                              AS confirm_rate
FROM signups;

Naming that "time to confirm" and "confirmation rate" are different metrics — and that reporting only the first hides churn — is the over-the-bar move.

Mean vs median — the 1440-minute outlier

User 3's one-day delay pulls the mean to 369 minutes when three of four users confirmed in under half an hour. For a skewed latency distribution, the median (or p90) is far more representative:

SELECT percentile_cont(0.5) WITHIN GROUP (
         ORDER BY EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (confirmed_at - signup_at))/60)
       AS median_minutes
FROM signups WHERE confirmed_at IS NOT NULL;

Latency metrics are almost always reported as percentiles, not means, precisely because of outliers like this.

Rounding and dirty timestamps

369.375... with a precision tail is not dashboard-ready — wrap in ROUND(..., 1) for display. Also consider data quality: if a clock skew or backfill produces confirmed_at < signup_at, the interval goes negative and lowers the average. A defensive query adds AND confirmed_at >= signup_at to drop impossible rows.

Senior signal: notice the ÷86400×86400 cancels and simplify to EXTRACT(EPOCH ...)/60, treat excluding non-confirmers as a reporting decision — pair the latency with a confirmation rate — and prefer a median or percentile over a mean for a skewed latency distribution.
