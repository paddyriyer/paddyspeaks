DoorDash's restaurant-quality team uses a weighted composite of order volume × rating to identify "stars" vs "strugglers". The query computes a 70/30 weighted score.

2. The SQL — composite score

SELECT r.restaurant_id, r.name, r.market,
  COUNT(o.order_id) AS orders,
  ROUND(AVG(o.rating), 2) AS avg_rating,
  ROUND(AVG(o.rating) * 0.7 + (COUNT(o.order_id) * 1.0 / 100) * 0.3, 2) AS composite_score
FROM restaurants r
LEFT JOIN orders o ON o.restaurant_id = r.restaurant_id
GROUP BY r.restaurant_id, r.name, r.market
HAVING COUNT(o.order_id) >= 3
ORDER BY composite_score DESC;

3. Strategic explanations

Why composite — single-axis lies

Rating-only ranks tiny restaurants with 5 orders × 5.0 rating above proven mid-volume operators. Volume-only ranks chains with mediocre service. Composite balances both.

The 70/30 weighting

Quality (rating) weighted higher than volume — DoorDash prioritizes customer experience over scale. Some markets prefer 50/50; the weights are configurable per region.

The volume normalization (×1/100)

Without dividing by 100, a restaurant with 1000 orders × 0.3 = 300 dwarfs the rating component (5 × 0.7 = 3.5). The /100 puts volume on the same scale as rating. Production uses log-volume for better scaling.

Bayesian smoothing — the rating fix

5.0 with 3 reviews is unreliable. Bayesian-smoothed rating moves toward 4.5 baseline as review count drops:

(SUM(rating) + 4.5 * 5) / (COUNT(*) + 5) AS smoothed_rating
Time-decay

A 4.8 average from a year of reviews vs 4.8 from last week — the recent one is more relevant. Add a recency-weighted average for active restaurant ranking.

Senior signal: argue for composite over single-axis, justify the 70/30 weighting toward quality, normalize volume to rating-scale (or log-volume), and apply Bayesian smoothing for low-review restaurants.
