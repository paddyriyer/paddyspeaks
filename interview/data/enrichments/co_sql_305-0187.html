Snap's revenue team wants to find the seasonal peaks in ad sales — which calendar months pull the most revenue, ranked. The query buckets every sale into a month-of-year, sums revenue, and ranks the months. The interviewer is watching how you extract the month, what happens when two months tie, and whether you noticed this collapses years together.

1. The source data — sales

January has two sales summing to 250; March has two summing to 450; November has one sale of 450. March and November tie at 450 — that tie is the whole interview, because it decides whether they share a rank or get arbitrary distinct ones.

2. The SQL — RANK over monthly SUM

SELECT CAST(TO_CHAR(sale_date, 'MM') AS INTEGER) AS mo, SUM(amount) AS total,
  RANK() OVER (ORDER BY SUM(amount) DESC) AS rk
FROM sales GROUP BY CAST(TO_CHAR(sale_date, 'MM') AS INTEGER);

TO_CHAR(sale_date, 'MM') formats the date as a two-digit month string, cast back to an integer. GROUP BY collapses each month; RANK() orders the monthly totals descending.

March and November both get rank 1 — equal totals, so RANK() assigns them the same position. Then February gets rank 3, not 2: RANK() skips the gap left by the tie. The peak season is "March and November", and the skipped 2 is a deliberate consequence of the ranking function chosen.

4. Strategic explanations

The tie behaviour — RANK vs DENSE_RANK vs ROW_NUMBER

With March and November tied, the three ranking functions diverge sharply:

monthtotalRANKDENSE_RANKROW_NUMBER
Nov450111
Mar450112
Feb3003 (skips 2)23
Jan250434

RANK leaves a gap after a tie (1, 1, 3). DENSE_RANK does not (1, 1, 2). ROW_NUMBER refuses to tie at all — it would hand March and November different numbers based on arbitrary row order, which for "peak months" is simply wrong. For "top N seasons", DENSE_RANK is usually the better default, since WHERE rk <= 3 then genuinely means "the three best totals".

This silently collapses all years together — the real trap

TO_CHAR(sale_date, 'MM') extracts only the month, discarding the year. Sales from January 2023 and January 2025 land in the same bucket. For a true "which season" answer that may be intended — you want every January pooled. But if the data spans uneven years (three Marches but only one November), the comparison is unfair: March looks big merely because it was sampled more often. Confirm the intent. If the question is "best month per year", group by both:

SELECT EXTRACT(YEAR FROM sale_date) AS yr,
       EXTRACT(MONTH FROM sale_date) AS mo,
       SUM(amount) AS total,
       RANK() OVER (PARTITION BY EXTRACT(YEAR FROM sale_date)
                    ORDER BY SUM(amount) DESC) AS rk
FROM sales GROUP BY 1, 2;
EXTRACT beats TO_CHAR-then-CAST

CAST(TO_CHAR(sale_date,'MM') AS INTEGER) does a date→string→integer round trip. EXTRACT(MONTH FROM sale_date) goes straight to a number — one step, no formatting locale to worry about, and the planner can reason about it better. The reference works, but EXTRACT (or date_part) is the idiomatic choice and worth naming.

The query has no ORDER BY — rank assigned, order not guaranteed

RANK() computes the value of the rank, but it does not sort the output. Without a final ORDER BY rk, the planner may return February before November. The result happening to come back sorted is luck. Always append ORDER BY rk (or ORDER BY total DESC) when the consumer expects a ranked list.

Edge case: months with zero sales

A month with no sales produces no row — GROUP BY only emits months that appear in the data. A "seasonality" chart usually wants all 12 months, including the dead ones at 0. That needs a calendar spine: generate_series(1, 12) left-joined to the aggregated sales, with COALESCE(total, 0).

Senior signal: spell out that two tied months share a RANK() and the next rank is skipped, flag that extracting only the month silently pools every year together (fine for "seasonality", wrong for uneven year coverage), prefer EXTRACT over the TO_CHAR/CAST round trip, and add the missing ORDER BY rk.
