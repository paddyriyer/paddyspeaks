Meta's hardware-partnerships team tracks device sales across manufacturers, and finance wants a clean annual roll-up: revenue per manufacturer, per calendar year. The query is a textbook two-key GROUP BY. The interview is in the date handling — how you extract a year, whether you trust the column's type, and what happens at year boundaries.

1. The source data — sales

Acme2023-02-115000
Acme2023-08-043000
Acme2024-01-207000
Globex2023-05-152000
Globex2024-11-309000
Initech2024-03-031500

Acme has two 2023 rows that must collapse into one annual figure, and Acme also spans two years — so the grouping key is the pair (manufacturer, year), not manufacturer alone. Initech sells in 2024 only: it should produce exactly one row, not a zero-filled 2023 row.

2. The SQL — GROUP BY manufacturer + year

SELECT manufacturer, CAST(TO_CHAR(sale_date, 'YYYY') AS INTEGER) AS yr, SUM(revenue) AS annual_rev
FROM sales GROUP BY manufacturer, CAST(TO_CHAR(sale_date, 'YYYY') AS INTEGER);

TO_CHAR(sale_date, 'YYYY') formats the date as a 4-character year string; the CAST turns it back into an integer so it sorts and joins numerically. The same expression appears in SELECT and GROUP BY because standard SQL groups by expressions, not by output aliases.

Acme's two 2023 sales (5000 + 3000) fuse into 8000, while its 2024 sale stands alone at 7000 — the proof that the year is part of the key. Five output rows from six input rows: exactly one collapse happened. The rows arrive unsorted because the query has no ORDER BY.

4. Strategic explanations

Use EXTRACT instead of round-tripping through text

TO_CHAR(... ,'YYYY') then CAST(... AS INTEGER) works, but it converts a date to a string and back — two conversions to get one number. EXTRACT goes straight to a numeric:

SELECT manufacturer, EXTRACT(YEAR FROM sale_date)::int AS yr, SUM(revenue) AS annual_rev
FROM sales GROUP BY manufacturer, EXTRACT(YEAR FROM sale_date);

date_part('year', sale_date) is the function form of the same thing. Both are clearer about intent and skip the locale-sensitive text formatting.

The interviewer's follow-up — ordering and the missing-year gap

The first ask is almost always "sort it" — add ORDER BY manufacturer, yr. The deeper follow-up: a manufacturer that did not sell in a year produces no row for that year. If finance needs a dense grid (every manufacturer × every year, zeros included), cross-join a manufacturers list with a years list and LEFT JOIN the aggregates, wrapping the sum in COALESCE(SUM(revenue), 0).

NULL revenue and the SUM contract

SUM skips NULLs, so a NULL revenue row is dropped from the total but still anchors the (manufacturer, year) group into existence. Result: a group can show annual_rev = NULL if every row in it is NULL, or an understated total if only some are. Decide whether a NULL means "$0" — SUM(COALESCE(revenue, 0)) — or genuinely missing data to investigate.

Fiscal year vs calendar year

EXTRACT(YEAR ...) assumes the year runs Jan–Dec. Many companies close books on a fiscal year (e.g. Feb–Jan). If Meta finance reports on a fiscal calendar, the year key must shift: something like EXTRACT(YEAR FROM sale_date + INTERVAL '1 month') for a December-start fiscal year. Always confirm which "year" the business means before grouping.

Time zones turn boundary sales into the wrong year

If sale_date were a timestamptz rather than a plain date, a sale at 11pm on Dec 31 in one zone is 4am Jan 1 in another. Extracting the year without first normalising to a canonical zone can park December revenue in the next year. With a pure DATE column this is moot — but say you checked.

Senior signal: prefer EXTRACT/date_part over a text round-trip for the year, repeat the grouping expression rather than relying on alias-in-GROUP BY, recognise that absent (manufacturer, year) pairs simply do not appear and need a calendar join to densify, and ask whether "year" means calendar or fiscal before writing the key.
