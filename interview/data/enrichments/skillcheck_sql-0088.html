A delivery-operations analyst wants each driver's daily average delivery time alongside their overall average — the daily figure shown per row, the overall repeated beside it for context. The interviewer is probing a subtle point: the "overall" average is a mean of daily means, not a mean of all deliveries. Computing it correctly — and knowing the two are different — is the whole exercise.

The edge case is the uneven day sizes. Driver 1's Jan 1 has two deliveries, Jan 2 has one. That imbalance is exactly what makes "average of daily averages" diverge from "average of all deliveries" — the single-delivery day gets equal weight to the two-delivery day.

2. The SQL — per-delivery duration, daily mean, then a window mean of means

WITH dur AS (
  SELECT driver_id,
         order_placed::date AS day,
         EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (delivered_at - order_placed))/60 AS minutes
  FROM delivery
),
daily AS (
  SELECT driver_id, day, AVG(minutes) AS daily_avg_min
  FROM dur
  GROUP BY driver_id, day
)
SELECT driver_id, day,
       ROUND(daily_avg_min::numeric, 2) AS daily_avg_min,
       ROUND(AVG(daily_avg_min) OVER (PARTITION BY driver_id)::numeric, 2) AS overall_avg_min
FROM daily
ORDER BY driver_id, day;

dur computes each delivery's duration in minutes. daily averages those per (driver, day). The final query keeps the per-day average and, in the same row, computes AVG(daily_avg_min) OVER (PARTITION BY driver_id) — the mean of that driver's daily means.

4. Strategic explanations

Mean of means ≠ grand mean — the central trap

Averaging daily averages weights each day equally regardless of how many deliveries it contains. Averaging all deliveries weights each delivery equally. They coincide only when every day has the same number of deliveries. Driver 1 proves the gap: mean-of-daily-means = 32.50, grand mean = 30.00. The question explicitly asks for "the average across all days," i.e. the mean of the daily figures — so the two-step aggregation is required, and you must be able to say why a single AVG over dur would answer a different question.

Why a window function, not a second GROUP BY

The output keeps every daily row and shows the driver-level overall beside it. A plain GROUP BY driver_id would collapse to one row per driver and lose the per-day detail. AVG(daily_avg_min) OVER (PARTITION BY driver_id) computes the driver-level average without collapsing — it broadcasts the aggregate across all of that driver's rows. Window aggregates are the tool whenever you need a group-level number alongside, not instead of, the detail rows.

Duration arithmetic and the date bucket

delivered_at - order_placed yields an interval; EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM ...) gives seconds, and /60 converts to minutes (kept fractional for accuracy). order_placed::date buckets each delivery into a calendar day — a modelling choice: you could bucket by delivered_at::date instead, which would matter for an order placed at 23:50 and delivered at 00:10. State which timestamp defines "the day"; here it is when the order was placed.

Round last, and cast for two-argument ROUND

Rounding happens once, in the outer SELECT, after both averages are fully computed — rounding the per-delivery minutes early would inject error into both downstream means. The ::numeric casts are mandatory, not decorative: AVG and EXTRACT produce double precision, and PostgreSQL's ROUND(value, scale) only accepts numeric — without the cast you get function round(double precision, integer) does not exist.

Dialect notes

Window aggregates (AVG(...) OVER (PARTITION BY ...)) are standard. The interval maths is dialect-specific: SQL Server uses DATEDIFF(MINUTE, order_placed, delivered_at) (integer minutes — loses fractional precision); MySQL TIMESTAMPDIFF(MINUTE, ...) or TIMESTAMPDIFF(SECOND,...)/60 for fractions. The two-argument ROUND needing a non-float type is a Postgres quirk — most other engines accept ROUND(float, 2) directly.

Senior signal: recognise that "average of daily averages" weights days equally and is not the grand mean over all deliveries — compute it as a two-step aggregation, then surface the driver-level figure beside each daily row with AVG(...) OVER (PARTITION BY driver) rather than a collapsing GROUP BY; round once at the end and cast to numeric so two-argument ROUND compiles.
