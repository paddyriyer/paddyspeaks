Netflix's SRE-CDN team owns one number that wakes them up at 3am: rebuffering rate by ISP. When Comcast Northeast spikes to 2% rebuf vs the 0.3% baseline, peering links are being throttled or last-mile is congested — and that's worth a phone call to Comcast Operations. The query is straight aggregation; the senior depth is the denominator choice and the partner-management implications.

2. The SQL — rebuf-time / total-time per ISP

SELECT isp,
  COUNT(*) AS sessions,
  ROUND(SUM(rebuf_seconds) * 100.0
      / NULLIF(SUM(watched_seconds + rebuf_seconds), 0), 3) AS rebuf_pct,
  ROUND(AVG(rebuf_count), 2) AS avg_rebufs_per_session
FROM qoe
GROUP BY isp
HAVING COUNT(*) >= 1000
ORDER BY rebuf_pct DESC;

3. Strategic explanations

The denominator that actually matters

Three plausible denominators give very different numbers:

denominatorformulawhat it measures
watched_seconds onlyrebuf / watched"per minute of content, how much rebuffering?" — biased low
watched + rebufrebuf / (watched + rebuf)"what fraction of session time was rebuffering?" — industry standard
session countrebufs / sessions"events per session" — useful but doesn't reflect duration

Always use watched + rebuf for percentage metrics. Rebuf-time / watched-time can exceed 100% on a bad session (more rebuf than playback) which makes no sense as a "%".

Average-of-averages trap (Simpson's paradox)

Computing rebuf-rate per session and then averaging across sessions:

-- WRONG — heavily biased by short sessions
AVG(rebuf_seconds * 1.0 / NULLIF(watched_seconds + rebuf_seconds, 0))

A 5-second session with 2 seconds of rebuf is 40%. A 90-min session with 10 seconds of rebuf is 0.2%. Simple-averaging weights both equally and gives a wildly inflated number. Always weight by duration: SUM(rebuf) / SUM(total).

The HAVING threshold — sample size matters

Long-tail ISPs with 50 sessions can show 5% rebuf rate that's pure noise. HAVING COUNT(*) >= 1000 filters those out. Add a per-row CI for visualization:

SQRT(rebuf_pct * (100 - rebuf_pct) / sessions) * 1.96 AS ci_pp_at_95
The 95th-percentile metric — tail matters more than mean

Average rebuf rate misses the catastrophic tail (the 5% of sessions that rebuffered every 30 seconds). The user-experience metric Netflix uses is "% of sessions with > 1% rebuf time" — the bad-session rate:

SELECT isp,
  100.0 * SUM(CASE WHEN rebuf_seconds * 1.0 / NULLIF(watched_seconds + rebuf_seconds, 0) > 0.01
              THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) / COUNT(*) AS pct_bad_sessions
FROM qoe GROUP BY isp;
The peering-link diagnosis — ISP × geo

"Comcast bad" isn't actionable; "Comcast in the Northeast bad" is — that's a peering-router or backbone issue. Always cross-tab:

SELECT isp, region, COUNT(*) AS sessions,
  100.0 * SUM(rebuf_seconds) / NULLIF(SUM(watched_seconds + rebuf_seconds), 0) AS rebuf_pct
FROM qoe GROUP BY isp, region
HAVING COUNT(*) >= 500
ORDER BY rebuf_pct DESC;

The top-of-leaderboard cells are the partner-management calls. Netflix's ISP Speed Index public page is essentially this query.

Time-of-day effect — primetime is the load test

Rebuf rates 7-11pm are systematically higher than 2-6am (concurrent demand). Flat-averaging across the day under-reports the primetime experience. Slice:

SELECT isp,
  CASE WHEN EXTRACT(HOUR FROM started_at) BETWEEN 19 AND 23 THEN 'primetime' ELSE 'offpeak' END AS daypart,
  ...
Codec and bitrate confounders

HEVC sessions rebuffer less than AVC (smaller bitstream → less to buffer). Mobile uses lower bitrates than TV. Without controlling for codec/device/bitrate, you compare apples to oranges. Senior-level QoE dashboards always pin a single bitrate-tier as the comparison axis.

The customer-impact escalation

The query result drives concrete action:

  • Top ISP × region with HIGH rebuf — partner-management call to ISP NOC
  • Top ISP × region with normal rebuf but HIGH bad-session-pct — peering-link investigation
  • One-time spike in time-series — PagerDuty
  • Sustained drift over weeks — capacity-planning RFP
The Open Connect cousin

Netflix's Open Connect appliances live inside ISP networks. A high-rebuf ISP without Open Connect is a sales target ("install our box in your network for free"). A high-rebuf ISP with Open Connect is an operational issue (the box is full, mis-configured, or the ISP is throttling around it). The query result feeds both teams.

Senior signal: pick SUM(rebuf) / SUM(total) over AVG-of-ratios with the Simpson's-paradox warning, name the bad-session-rate as the more-useful tail metric, and pre-empt the ISP×region peering-link diagnosis. Day-part slicing and codec/bitrate confounder control are the over-the-bar finish that turns the query into a partner-management tool.
