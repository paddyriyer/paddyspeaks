Sales forecasts drive hiring + investment decisions. Bias (forecast - actual) and MAPE per quarter measure accuracy. Forecasts always over-predict; calibrating the bias is the science.

2. The SQL — three error metrics

SELECT COUNT(*) AS opps,
  ROUND(AVG(forecast_amount - actual_amount), 2) AS avg_bias,
  ROUND(AVG(ABS(forecast_amount - actual_amount)), 2) AS mae,
  ROUND(100.0 * AVG(ABS(forecast_amount - actual_amount) / NULLIF(actual_amount, 0)), 1) AS mape_pct,
  ROUND(SUM(forecast_amount), 2) AS total_forecast,
  ROUND(SUM(actual_amount), 2) AS total_actual
FROM forecasts WHERE actual_amount IS NOT NULL;

3. Strategic explanations

Bias direction matters

+15% bias = chronic over-forecast. Reps "sandbag low" to avoid disappointing leadership; or "promise high" hoping for raises. Bias direction is itself a cultural signal.

MAPE vs MAE — which is right

MAPE is dimensionless (% error). MAE is in dollars. For comparing across deal sizes, MAPE; for absolute impact, MAE. Production reports both.

The "miss" classification

Forecast $1M, actual $900K = 10% miss. Forecast $100K, actual $0 = 100% miss but only $100K. Weighting matters. Production also computes $$ miss = SUM(ABS(forecast - actual)).

The category split

"Commit" deals (90% probability): expect < 5% bias. "Pipeline" (50%): expect 30-50% bias. Comparing them lumped is unfair. Slice by forecast_category.

Calibration over time

Forecasters who consistently bias +15% can be auto-corrected: subtract 15% from their next forecast. Production does this on rolling 4-quarter MAPE.

Senior signal: bias direction reveals culture (sandbag vs over-promise), MAPE for cross-size compare, MAE for absolute impact, slice by forecast_category, and apply auto-calibration based on historical bias.
