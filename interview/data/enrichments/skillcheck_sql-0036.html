A marketing team wants customers who bought something from a target set but are "clean" of a forbidden set — e.g. people who bought a core product yet never touched a competitor's SKU. This is an inclusion-minus-exclusion set query. The interviewer is probing whether you compose a positive filter with an anti-membership subquery, and whether you know the NOT IN NULL trap.

1. The source data — purchases

customer_idproducts(narrative)
1A, B, Cbought A and B, no X/Y/Z — qualifies
2A, Xbought A but also X — excluded by the forbidden set
3B, Cbought B, clean — qualifies
4B, D, Ebought B, only neutral extras — qualifies
5C, Dno A or B at all — fails the inclusion test
6A, Ybought A but also Y — excluded
7Bsingle purchase, B, clean — qualifies

The interesting cases: customer 5 bought neither A nor B (fails inclusion despite being "clean"), and customers 2 and 6 satisfy inclusion but are knocked out by exclusion. Customer 7 — a single-row, single-member group — must still qualify.

2. The SQL — positive filter minus a forbidden-set subquery

SELECT DISTINCT customer_id
FROM purchases
WHERE product IN ('A','B')
  AND customer_id NOT IN (
        SELECT customer_id FROM purchases WHERE product IN ('X','Y','Z')
      )
ORDER BY customer_id;

product IN ('A','B') keeps rows for the inclusion set — a customer surfaces if any of their rows match. customer_id NOT IN (…) removes anyone whose id appears in the subquery of forbidden-set buyers. DISTINCT collapses a customer who bought both A and B to one output row.

3. The resulting analysis

customer_id(commentary)
1has A,B; not in {X,Y,Z buyers}
3has B; clean
4has B; D and E are neutral, not forbidden
7has B; single-purchase customer still qualifies

Customers 2 and 6 are excluded despite owning A — their X / Y purchase puts their id in the forbidden subquery. Customer 5 never appears: no row matches product IN ('A','B'), so inclusion fails before exclusion is even consulted.

4. Strategic explanations

Why the positive filter and the anti-join are separate steps

Inclusion and exclusion operate at different grains. Inclusion is a row test — "does this row's product fall in {A,B}". Exclusion is a customer test — "did this customer ever buy a forbidden product, in any row". You cannot fold them into one WHERE product IN (...) AND product NOT IN (...): that would only check the current row, and customer 2's A-row would sail through because that single row is not X. The forbidden check must scan the customer's whole history — hence a subquery.

The NOT IN + NULL trap

NOT IN against a subquery is dangerous if the subquery can return a NULL. x NOT IN (1, NULL) evaluates to unknown for every x — so the whole query silently returns zero rows. Here customer_id is non-nullable so it is safe, but a senior candidate states the risk and prefers a NULL-immune form: NOT EXISTS (SELECT 1 FROM purchases f WHERE f.customer_id = p.customer_id AND f.product IN ('X','Y','Z')). NOT EXISTS is a pure row-existence test and never breaks on NULLs.

DISTINCT vs GROUP BY

Because a customer can have several matching rows (customer 1 has both A and B), the inclusion filter alone yields duplicate ids. DISTINCT deduplicates the output. Equivalently GROUP BY customer_id with no aggregate. If the question wanted "bought both A and B" rather than "either", you would switch to GROUP BY customer_id HAVING COUNT(DISTINCT product) FILTER (WHERE product IN ('A','B')) = 2 — a different requirement worth clarifying.

Anti-join alternatives and performance

NOT EXISTS, NOT IN, and LEFT JOIN ... WHERE forbidden.customer_id IS NULL all express the exclusion. Modern planners optimise NOT EXISTS and the LEFT JOIN anti-join to the same efficient plan; NOT IN can be worse and carries the NULL hazard. Default to NOT EXISTS for correctness and predictable performance — reserve NOT IN for a small, known-NULL-free literal list.

Senior signal: compose set queries as a row-level inclusion filter plus a customer-level anti-membership check — they sit at different grains and cannot be merged into one row predicate; prefer NOT EXISTS over NOT IN because a single NULL in a NOT IN subquery wipes the entire result.
