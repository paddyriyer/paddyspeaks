OpenAI's revenue team wants to know which customer segment actually makes money, not which one books the most revenue. A segment can post huge top-line numbers and still lose money once cost of delivery is netted out. The query is a join and a sum — but the interviewer is watching whether you net revenue against cost per order, and whether you notice that an inner join quietly drops every customer who has never ordered.

SMB books 700 in revenue but its margins are thin (50 + 200 profit). Consumer barely clears (30 profit). The Enterprise segment carries the business. The arithmetic per order is the point: aggregating revenue and cost separately would also work, but netting per-row keeps the intent obvious.

2. The SQL — SUM(revenue - cost) per segment

SELECT c.segment, SUM(o.revenue - o.cost) AS profit
FROM customers c JOIN orders o ON c.customer_id = o.customer_id
GROUP BY c.segment
ORDER BY profit DESC;

Join each order to its customer to pick up segment, compute per-order margin, sum within segment, rank descending.

Enterprise nets 1900 ((1000−400) + (500−300) + (2000−900)). SMB nets 250 on 700 revenue — a 36% margin. Consumer nets 30. The ordering by profit, not revenue, is what the question asked for and what makes the answer actionable.

4. Strategic explanations

SUM(revenue - cost) vs SUM(revenue) - SUM(cost)

Both are algebraically identical — summation distributes over subtraction — and both give 1900 / 250 / 30 here. The per-row form reads as "profit per order, then totalled"; the split form reads as "total revenue minus total cost". The one caveat: if a row has a NULL cost, revenue - cost is NULL and that whole order is dropped from the sum, whereas SUM(revenue) - SUM(cost) keeps the revenue and ignores only the NULL cost. They diverge precisely on NULLs — decide which behaviour you want and use COALESCE(cost, 0) to make it explicit.

The inner join drops customers with zero orders

Customer 4 has one order here, but imagine a fifth customer in a "Startup" segment who has never purchased. JOIN emits no row for them, so the Startup segment vanishes from the report entirely — not shown as zero profit, just absent. If the deck must show every segment, switch to LEFT JOIN and coalesce:

SELECT c.segment, COALESCE(SUM(o.revenue - o.cost), 0) AS profit
FROM customers c LEFT JOIN orders o ON c.customer_id = o.customer_id
GROUP BY c.segment
ORDER BY profit DESC;

Without the COALESCE, a segment with no orders shows profit = NULL, which sorts unpredictably and looks broken on a dashboard.

The order-status trap

The orders table carries a status column. Cancelled or refunded orders may still have revenue and cost populated, so they are silently inflating — or deflating — segment profit. Almost always profitability should count only realised orders: WHERE o.status = 'completed'. Confirm the definition before trusting the number.

The revenue vs amount ambiguity

The orders schema ships both revenue and amount. They are likely near-duplicates that have drifted — one gross, one net of discount; one with tax, one without. The solution uses revenue; that is probably right for a profit calculation, but say so and confirm which column the finance team treats as the source of truth.

Margin percentage, not just absolute profit

Expect the follow-up "which segment is most efficient?" Absolute profit favours big segments; margin reveals quality:

SELECT c.segment,
       SUM(o.revenue - o.cost) AS profit,
       ROUND(SUM(o.revenue - o.cost) * 100.0 / NULLIF(SUM(o.revenue), 0), 1) AS margin_pct
FROM customers c JOIN orders o ON c.customer_id = o.customer_id
GROUP BY c.segment;

NULLIF(SUM(o.revenue), 0) guards against divide-by-zero for a segment with zero revenue.

Senior signal: net revenue against cost rather than ranking on top-line, know that SUM(a-b) and SUM(a)-SUM(b) diverge only on NULLs, switch to LEFT JOIN + COALESCE when every segment must appear, apply the status = 'completed' filter, and offer margin percentage as the efficiency view.
