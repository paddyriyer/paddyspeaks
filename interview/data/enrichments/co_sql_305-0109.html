OpenAI's finance team reports revenue every year, and the headline slide is always year-over-year growth. The arithmetic is trivial — this year minus last year, over last year. The interview is whether you reach for a window function instead of a self-join, and whether you have thought about the year when last year's revenue was zero, or when there is no last year at all.

1. The source data — revenue

Three deliberate cases: 2020 has no prior year (the growth must be NULL, not zero); 2022→2023 is flat (exactly 0% growth, which must not be confused with NULL); and 2021→2022 is a decline (a negative percentage). One dataset, every edge.

2. The SQL — LAG over year

SELECT year, amount,
  100.0 * (amount - LAG(amount) OVER (ORDER BY year)) / NULLIF(LAG(amount) OVER (ORDER BY year),0) AS yoy_pct
FROM revenue;

LAG(amount) OVER (ORDER BY year) pulls the previous year's value into the current row. NULLIF(prev, 0) turns a zero denominator into NULL so the division yields NULL rather than a divide-by-zero error. 100.0 forces floating-point arithmetic.

4. Strategic explanations

Why LAG beats a self-join

The pre-window-function way is FROM revenue r JOIN revenue p ON p.year = r.year - 1. It works, but it is more code, it silently drops the first year (no match for year - 1), and crucially year - 1 assumes years are contiguous integers — a gap (no 2022 row) breaks the join. LAG takes the previous row in sort order regardless of the numeric gap, sorts once, and is the idiomatic answer.

The gap trap — "previous row" is not "previous year"

That same strength is a subtlety. If 2022 were missing entirely, LAG would compare 2023 against 2021 and label it a one-year change — it is really a two-year change. LAG does not know calendar years; it knows row adjacency. On data that may have missing years, either generate a dense year series and LEFT JOIN, or join explicitly on year - 1 and accept the gap-induced NULLs as honest.

The divide-by-zero guard is load-bearing

If a year had amount = 0, the next year's growth divides by zero. Without NULLIF that is a hard error in Postgres — the whole query fails, not just one row. NULLIF(prev, 0) converts the zero to NULL, and x / NULL is NULL: the report degrades gracefully to "undefined growth" for that one year. Growth from a zero base is genuinely undefined (infinite), so NULL is the honest answer.

The integer-division trap

The 100.0 multiplier is not cosmetic. If both amount columns are integers and you wrote 100 * (amount - prev) / prev, integer division truncates: a +20% change could compute as 100 * 200 / 1000 = 20000 / 1000 = 20 here, but 100 * 50 / 1000 would truncate to 5 instead of 5.0 in subtler cases — and intermediate truncation can drop the fractional part entirely. Forcing one operand to numeric/float (the 100.0) keeps the math exact.

NULL vs 0 — do not COALESCE them together

It is tempting to wrap the result in COALESCE(yoy_pct, 0) to avoid NULLs on a dashboard. Resist it: the 2020 NULL means "no comparison possible" and the 2023 zero means "measured, no change". Collapsing both to 0 tells finance that 2020 had flat growth — a factual error. Render the NULL as an em-dash in the UI, not as a number.

Senior signal: reach for LAG over a self-join, recognise that LAG follows row adjacency not calendar years so missing years silently distort the comparison, keep the NULLIF divide-by-zero guard, force float arithmetic to dodge integer truncation, and never collapse a "no prior year" NULL into a "0% change" zero.
