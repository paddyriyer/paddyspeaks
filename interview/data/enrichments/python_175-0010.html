"Design Twitter" is a Google favorite because it compresses a real product — a personalized, time-ordered feed — into one class. The interviewer is not testing whether you know Twitter; they are testing API design and the merge: given many users you follow, each with their own tweet timeline, produce the 10 most recent across all of them. That "merge K sorted lists, take the top 10" shape is the heart of the problem.

1. The input data

A sequence of API calls in arrival order:

tw = Twitter()
tw.postTweet(1, 101)   # user 1 posts tweet 101
tw.postTweet(1, 102)   # user 1 posts tweet 102
tw.follow(1, 2)        # user 1 follows user 2
tw.postTweet(2, 201)   # user 2 posts tweet 201
tw.getNewsFeed(1)

2. The approach

A global counter self.t increments on every postTweet, stamping each tweet with a strictly increasing logical time — this is what makes "most recent" well-defined across users. self.tweets is a defaultdict(list) mapping a user to their (time, tweetId) history; self.follows is a defaultdict(set) of followee IDs. getNewsFeed(u) gathers the relevant authors as self.follows[u] | {u} — everyone u follows, plus u themselves — then, for each author, pushes only their last 10 tweets ([-10:]) into a min-heap keyed on -ts. Negating the timestamp turns Python's min-heap into a max-heap, so the largest (newest) timestamp surfaces first. Finally it pops up to 10 entries, returning their tweet IDs newest-first. follow/unfollow just mutate the set, with follow guarding against self-follow.

3. The output

Tweets get timestamps 101→t1, 102→t2, 201→t3. User 1's feed merges authors {1, 2}:

getNewsFeed(1)  ->  [201, 102, 101]

Tweet 201 has the newest timestamp (t3), then 102 (t2), then 101 (t1). The heap holds (-3,201),(-2,102),(-1,101) and pops them in that order.

4. Strategic explanations

Complexity

Let f be the number of followees. getNewsFeed pushes at most 10×(f+1) entries into the heap and pops 10, so it is O(f log f) roughly — bounded because each author contributes only 10 tweets. postTweet, follow, unfollow are O(1). The [-10:] slice is the key optimization: without it a celebrity with a million tweets would flood the heap.

The idiomatic alternative — heapq.merge

Each user's timeline is already sorted by time. Instead of a manual heap, you can feed reversed per-user lists into heapq.merge(*lists, reverse=True) and take the first 10 via itertools.islice. That is the canonical "merge K sorted streams" tool and is worth naming. heapq.nlargest(10, all_tweets) is another one-liner if you do not mind materializing the candidates.

The edge case — fewer than 10 tweets, and self-follow

min(10, len(h)) handles a feed with fewer than 10 tweets without an IndexError. The {u} union guarantees you see your own tweets even with zero follows. And follow rejecting a == b prevents a user appearing twice in the author set — a duplicate that would otherwise double-count their tweets.

The follow-up — the celebrity fan-out problem

"A user follows 10 million accounts — is getNewsFeed still fine?" No: gathering and heap-merging 10M timelines per read does not scale. Production systems fan out on write — push a new tweet into each follower's precomputed feed — or use a hybrid: fan-out-on-write for normal users, fan-out-on-read for celebrities. This is the architectural payoff the interviewer is steering toward.

The trap — sorting by tweet ID

Ordering the feed by tweetId instead of the logical timestamp is a classic mistake: tweet IDs are not guaranteed monotonic per the API, and even if they were, you would still need a global clock to interleave two users fairly. The incrementing counter self.t exists precisely to provide that total order.

Senior signal: a senior caps each author at their last 10 tweets before heap-merging, uses a global counter for a total time order, and pivots to fan-out-on-write when asked about celebrity scale — a junior scans every tweet on every read or sorts by tweet ID.
