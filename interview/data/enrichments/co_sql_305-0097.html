Meta's compensation team wants a quick list of everyone paid above the company average — an input to a pay-band review. The query is a classic subquery-in-WHERE, and it works. The interview lives in two places: whether you know the subquery runs exactly once (not once per row), and whether you notice that "above average" is a moving target the moment anyone's salary changes.

1. The source data — employees

emp_idnamesalary
1A120000
2B90000
3C150000
4D60000

6F200000

Six salaries summing to 700,000, so the average is 116,666.67. The interesting rows sit near that line: employee 1 at 120K is just above it, employee 2 at 90K is well below. One high earner (F at 200K) is single-handedly dragging the average up.

2. The SQL — scalar subquery in WHERE

SELECT emp_id, salary FROM employees
WHERE salary > (SELECT AVG(salary) FROM employees);

The inner query computes one number — the company-wide average. The outer query keeps every employee whose salary exceeds it.

3. The resulting analysis

emp_idsalary
1120000
3150000
6200000

Three of six employees are "above average" — and that is the statistical lesson. The mean is not the midpoint: one 200K outlier pulls the average up to 116.7K, so fewer than half the staff clear it. Employee 1, at 120K, barely makes the cut. If F's salary jumped to 400K, the average would rise and employee 1 could fall below the line without their own pay changing at all.

4. Strategic explanations

The subquery runs once — it is not correlated

(SELECT AVG(salary) FROM employees) references nothing from the outer query, so the planner evaluates it a single time and treats the result as a constant. That is an uncorrelated scalar subquery — cheap. Contrast a correlated subquery, which re-runs per outer row:

-- correlated: re-evaluated for every employee, O(n²)
SELECT emp_id FROM employees e
WHERE salary > (SELECT AVG(salary) FROM employees x
                WHERE x.dept_id = e.dept_id);

Stating "this average is computed once, not per row" shows you read execution, not just syntax.

Mean vs median — "above average" is often the wrong metric

Salary distributions are right-skewed: a few large numbers stretch the mean above where most people sit. "Above average" therefore answers a different question than "above the typical employee". If the comp review really means the midpoint, use the median:

SELECT emp_id, salary FROM employees
WHERE salary > (SELECT percentile_cont(0.5) WITHIN GROUP (ORDER BY salary)
                FROM employees);

Raising the mean-versus-median distinction unprompted is a senior signal — it shows you understand what the number will be used for.

The strict > and the employee exactly at the average

> excludes anyone whose salary equals the average exactly. With integer-ish salaries that is rare, but it happens — and the spec's wording decides it. "Above average" implies strict >; "at or above" means >=. Confirm rather than assume; a single boundary employee can change a headcount.

NULL salaries — ignored twice, then excluded

If any employee has a NULL salary: AVG skips NULLs (so they do not distort the average), and WHERE salary > ... is NULL for them, which is not true, so they are excluded from the result. Usually fine — but if a NULL salary means "unknown, treat as 0" the average itself is wrong. Decide whether NULL means "skip" or "zero" and handle it explicitly with COALESCE if needed.

Showing the gap, and the wide schema

The follow-up is often "how far above average?" — return the delta with a window function so the average is computed inline:

SELECT emp_id, salary,
       salary - AVG(salary) OVER () AS above_avg_by
FROM employees;

Also note the table carries dept_id, job_title, manager_id. "Above average" is far more actionable scoped per department or per title — a junior earning above the company average may still be underpaid for their role. AVG(salary) OVER (PARTITION BY dept_id) is the natural next question.

Senior signal: identify the subquery as uncorrelated and therefore evaluated once, explain that a right-skewed salary distribution makes "above average" exclude most staff (offer median instead), pin down strict vs inclusive at the boundary, and suggest partitioning the average by department or title to make it actionable.
