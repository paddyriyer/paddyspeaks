Netflix's Content Acquisition team gets one chart on the dashboard per region: which 3 titles dominated watch hours this week?. The answer drives renewal negotiations (when a top-3 hit is up for license renewal), recommendation budgets, and the famous "Top 10 in [Country]" public top-10 row. The query is a textbook top-N per group — with the senior caveat that "3" really means "3 unique titles", ties handled correctly.

2. The SQL — DENSE_RANK + WHERE rk ≤ 3

SELECT region, title_id, hours
FROM (
  SELECT region, title_id,
    SUM(watch_minutes) / 60.0 AS hours,
    DENSE_RANK() OVER (PARTITION BY region ORDER BY SUM(watch_minutes) DESC) AS rk
  FROM plays
  GROUP BY region, title_id
) t
WHERE rk <= 3
ORDER BY region, hours DESC;

3. Strategic explanations

Why DENSE_RANK — not ROW_NUMBER or RANK
titlehoursROW_NUMBERRANKDENSE_RANK
Stranger Things50,000111

Lupin30,00033 (gap)2

Two titles tie at 50K. ROW_NUMBER picks one arbitrarily — you'd lose Squid Game from the top 3 in some runs and not others. RANK leaves a "gap" so Lupin is rank 3, not Wednesday — which means WHERE rk ≤ 3 returns Lupin and the tied pair (4 titles). DENSE_RANK keeps it tight: the top 3 distinct watch-hour values, ties emit together. Marketing wants the dense interpretation — "top 3 hits" should not arbitrarily exclude one half of a tie.

The SUM-inside-window trick

The DENSE_RANK() OVER (... ORDER BY SUM(watch_minutes) DESC) pattern works because the window function is evaluated after the GROUP BY: at that point the rows are already (region, title_id) groups with their summed totals, and the rank is computed over those. You can also write it as a CTE:

WITH per_title AS (
  SELECT region, title_id, SUM(watch_minutes) AS minutes
  FROM plays GROUP BY region, title_id
)
SELECT region, title_id, minutes / 60.0 AS hours,
  DENSE_RANK() OVER (PARTITION BY region ORDER BY minutes DESC) AS rk
FROM per_title;

Same plan, often more readable.

QUALIFY — the cleanest dialect

Snowflake, BigQuery, DuckDB, and Databricks support QUALIFY, which filters the result of a window function inline (no subquery):

SELECT region, title_id, SUM(watch_minutes)/60.0 AS hours
FROM plays
GROUP BY region, title_id
QUALIFY DENSE_RANK() OVER (PARTITION BY region ORDER BY SUM(watch_minutes) DESC) <= 3
ORDER BY region, hours DESC;
The watch-hours denominator trap — minutes vs hours vs DAU-weighted

"Top by watch hours" privileges long-form titles. A 60-min episode of Stranger Things logs 60 min of watch_minutes per viewer; a 22-min sitcom episode logs 22. Without normalization, dramas dominate the chart structurally. The senior follow-up is completion-weighted:

SUM(watch_minutes * 1.0 / NULLIF(title_runtime_minutes, 0))   -- "title-equivalents watched"

For comedies and drama-comedy mixed catalogs, Netflix internally uses this ratio — not raw minutes — for "most popular" rankings.

The "unique viewers vs minutes" axis

Two titles can have identical hours: one with 1M users watching 30 min each, the other with 100K hardcore fans watching 5 hours each. Same total, very different stories — the first is "broad hit", the second is "cult". Always emit both:

SUM(watch_minutes)/60.0       AS hours,
COUNT(DISTINCT user_id)        AS reach,
SUM(watch_minutes)/60.0 / NULLIF(COUNT(DISTINCT user_id), 0) AS hours_per_user
The release-bias trap

A title that launched Monday has 7 days of accumulation by Sunday; a title launched Saturday has 1 day. Top-3 by raw cumulative hours unfairly favors mature titles. The fix: rank by D7 hours (first-7-days from launch), not "this week".

The "Top 10 in [Country]" Netflix-public version

The public Top 10 row is the "TV (English)" / "Films (Non-English)" / etc. category split. Production query:

WITH ranked AS (
  SELECT region, content_type, title_id,
    SUM(watch_minutes) AS minutes,
    DENSE_RANK() OVER (
      PARTITION BY region, content_type
      ORDER BY SUM(watch_minutes) DESC
    ) AS rk
  FROM plays GROUP BY region, content_type, title_id
)
SELECT * FROM ranked WHERE rk <= 10
ORDER BY region, content_type, rk;
The "trending up" follow-up — week-over-week movement

Marketing's actual question is rarely just "top 3 today". They want "what's NEW in the top 3 this week vs last?" Lag the rank:

WITH weekly AS (
  SELECT DATE_TRUNC('week', played_at) AS wk, region, title_id,
    DENSE_RANK() OVER (PARTITION BY DATE_TRUNC('week', played_at), region ORDER BY SUM(watch_minutes) DESC) AS rk
  FROM plays GROUP BY DATE_TRUNC('week', played_at), region, title_id
)
SELECT region, title_id, wk, rk,
  LAG(rk) OVER (PARTITION BY region, title_id ORDER BY wk) AS prev_rk,
  LAG(rk) OVER (PARTITION BY region, title_id ORDER BY wk) - rk AS delta_up
FROM weekly WHERE rk <= 10
ORDER BY region, wk, rk;

A title that goes from rank 8 last week to rank 2 this week is the "climbing" badge on the public top-10 page. delta_up = +6.

The "unfair to small regions" trap

LATAM has fewer accounts than NA, so absolute watch-hours always look smaller. The right comparator for regional content investment isn't absolute hours but share of regional watch-time:

SUM(watch_minutes) * 100.0 /
  SUM(SUM(watch_minutes)) OVER (PARTITION BY region) AS share_of_region_pct

Now Squid Game in APAC and Lupin in EMEA can be compared apples-to-apples regardless of region size.

Senior signal: name DENSE_RANK as the right tie semantic for "top 3 hits", contrast against ROW_NUMBER/RANK, and offer QUALIFY as the cleanest dialect form. Raise completion-weighting (minutes/runtime) and reach-vs-hours-per-user as the "what does popular even mean?" senior reframe. The week-over-week LAG-on-rank for the "climbing" badge is the over-the-bar finish that connects the query to the actual UI.
