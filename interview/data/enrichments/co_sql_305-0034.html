Amazon's order-reporting team needs a flat line-item view: customer name, order id, product name, quantity — one row per item shipped. The reference solution chains four inner joins from customers through orders and order_items to products. It reads cleanly, and the interview is about what those inner joins quietly throw away.

Cy has placed no orders — the customer with nothing on the other side of the join. That is the row the interviewer is watching for.

2. The SQL — four-table inner join chain

SELECT c.name, o.order_id, p.name AS product, oi.qty
FROM customers c
JOIN orders o      ON c.customer_id = o.customer_id
JOIN order_items oi ON o.order_id = oi.order_id
JOIN products p     ON oi.product_id = p.product_id;

The join walks the chain: customer → their orders → the items on each order → the product behind each item. The grain of the result is one row per order item.

Four rows — one per line item. Ana appears three times because she has three items across two orders; order 500 appears twice because it has two products. Cy is absent entirely. That fan-out and that omission are both consequences of the join, not the data being wrong.

4. Strategic explanations

Inner joins drop the unmatched side — Cy disappears

An INNER JOIN emits a row only when both sides match. Cy has no order, so customers JOIN orders never produces a Cy row, and the rest of the chain has nothing to extend. For a line-item report that is fine — a customer with no items has no lines. But if the question were "list all customers and their items, zero or more", the inner join is wrong and you need LEFT JOIN:

SELECT c.name, o.order_id, p.name AS product, oi.qty
FROM customers c
LEFT JOIN orders o       ON c.customer_id = o.customer_id
LEFT JOIN order_items oi ON o.order_id = oi.order_id
LEFT JOIN products p     ON oi.product_id = p.product_id;

Now Cy returns one row with NULLs for order_id, product, qty. The rule: one inner join anywhere in the chain re-filters everything — if you want to preserve customers, every join from customers onward must be LEFT.

Row multiplication is correct here — know your grain

Ana appearing three times is not a duplicate bug; the result's grain is the order item, and she has three items. The trap is using this row set as a base for SUM or COUNT without remembering that. COUNT(DISTINCT c.customer_id) = 2 customers, but COUNT(*) = 4 lines. Always state what one row represents before aggregating on top of it.

Joining on the right keys — this schema is a minefield

Look at the schema: customers, orders, products and order_items each carry customer_id, order_id, product_id, qty, even name. A careless join like JOIN products p ON p.order_id = o.order_id would compile and return plausible-looking garbage. The solution is correct because it joins on the relationship keys: orders to items by order_id, items to products by product_id. Never join two tables just because a column name matches — join on the column that actually models the foreign key.

Ambiguous columns and the name collision

Both customers and products have a name column — that is why the query qualifies every reference (c.name, p.name) and aliases the product as AS product. An unqualified SELECT name would raise an "ambiguous column" error. Table aliases plus output aliases are mandatory hygiene in a multi-table join, not optional polish.

Edge cases: orphan items and explicit join syntax

If an order_items row points at a product_id that is missing from products (a deleted SKU), the final inner join drops that line silently — the report under-reports without warning. A LEFT JOIN products with a NULL product surfaces the orphan instead of hiding it. Also prefer explicit JOIN … ON over comma-joins with WHERE conditions: with four tables, a forgotten comma-join predicate becomes an accidental cross join — millions of rows from a missing line.

Senior signal: recognise that an inner-join chain filters out customers with no orders (Cy) and switch to LEFT JOIN end-to-end when the question wants every customer, name the result's grain before aggregating on it, and join strictly on the foreign-key relationship rather than on any same-named column in a deliberately redundant schema.
