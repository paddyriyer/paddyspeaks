Apple's retail-finance team wants a discount-impact summary: gross revenue at list price, total discounts given, and the net after discounts. Three SUMs, no GROUP BY — a one-row report. The interviewer is testing whether you see what a NULL discount does to the arithmetic, and whether the three numbers stay internally consistent.

1. The source data — orders

Order 4 has a NULL discount_amount — an order that simply was never discounted, and where the field was left blank rather than set to 0. Order 2 has an explicit 0. The difference between NULL and 0 is the entire interview, because they behave differently inside SUM(list_price - discount_amount).

2. The SQL — three SUMs over orders

SELECT SUM(list_price) AS gross,
  SUM(discount_amount) AS total_discounts,
  SUM(list_price - discount_amount) AS net
FROM orders;

No GROUP BY, so the result is a single row. gross sums list prices, total_discounts sums the discounts, and net sums the per-row difference.

3. The resulting analysis

grosstotal_discountsnet
53060390

Here is the catch. gross = 530 and total_discounts = 60, so you would expect net = 530 − 60 = 470. But net comes back as 390. The three columns do not reconcile — and a finance report whose numbers do not add up is a serious problem.

4. Strategic explanations

Why net is wrong: NULL propagates inside the row expression

For order 4, list_price - discount_amount is 80 - NULL, which evaluates to NULL — any arithmetic with NULL yields NULL. SUM then skips that NULL row entirely. So net is the sum over only orders 1, 2, 3: (100−10) + (200−0) + (150−50) = 90 + 200 + 100 = 390. Order 4's full $80 of revenue silently vanished from net — even though it is counted in gross. Meanwhile SUM(discount_amount) skips order 4's NULL and gives 60. The columns are computed over different row sets, so they cannot reconcile.

The fix: coalesce the discount before subtracting

Treat a missing discount as $0 — almost certainly the intended business meaning — so the subtraction never sees a NULL:

SELECT SUM(list_price) AS gross,
  SUM(COALESCE(discount_amount, 0)) AS total_discounts,
  SUM(list_price - COALESCE(discount_amount, 0)) AS net
FROM orders;

Now net = (100−10)+(200−0)+(150−50)+(80−0) = 470, and gross - total_discounts = 530 − 60 = 470. The identity holds. The senior instinct is to coalesce every nullable term in a financial calculation up front, not to discover the leak after the report ships.

An even safer rewrite — derive net from the other two

Because net is by definition gross - total_discounts, you can compute it once and guarantee consistency:

SELECT SUM(list_price) AS gross,
       SUM(COALESCE(discount_amount, 0)) AS total_discounts,
       SUM(list_price) - SUM(COALESCE(discount_amount, 0)) AS net
FROM orders;

This makes the three numbers reconcile by construction — there is no second, independent definition of net that could drift. When a derived metric must equal a relationship between others, expressing it as that relationship is more robust than recomputing it row-by-row.

NULL list_price and the realised-revenue filter

If list_price can also be NULL, all three sums skip those rows — usually fine, but state it. And every row here is completed; on real data a discount-impact report almost always wants WHERE status = 'completed' so cancelled or returned orders do not inflate gross. Confirm the universe before summing.

The schema's redundant amount column

The table also carries an amount column alongside list_price and discount_amount. Is amount the already-netted price? If so, net could be cross-checked as SUM(amount) — and a mismatch against SUM(list_price - discount) would reveal dirty data. Don't guess which column is authoritative; ask, and reconcile.

Senior signal: spot that list_price - discount_amount goes NULL when the discount is NULL, that SUM then drops the whole row, and that this makes net fail to equal gross - total_discounts — coalesce every nullable term, or derive net directly from the other two sums so the report reconciles by construction.
