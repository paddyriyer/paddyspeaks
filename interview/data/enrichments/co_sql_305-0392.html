LinkedIn's growth team measures network density = avg connections per member, per industry. Tech and Finance have densely-connected networks; emerging industries have sparse ones — they're the recruitment opportunity.

2. The SQL — connections / members per industry

SELECT m.industry,
  COUNT(DISTINCT m.member_id) AS members_in_industry,
  COUNT(c.connection_id) AS total_connections,
  ROUND(COUNT(c.connection_id) * 1.0 / NULLIF(COUNT(DISTINCT m.member_id), 0), 1) AS avg_connections_per_member
FROM members m
LEFT JOIN connections c ON c.member_id = m.member_id
GROUP BY m.industry
HAVING COUNT(DISTINCT m.member_id) >= 2
ORDER BY avg_connections_per_member DESC;

3. Strategic explanations

Why LEFT JOIN

Some members have 0 connections. INNER JOIN drops them, inflating the avg. LEFT JOIN keeps the zero-connection members in the denominator — honest avg.

Mature vs emerging — density bands
industry maturitydensity
Mature (Tech / Finance)500+ avg connections
Mid (Healthcare / Edu)200-400
Emerging (Climate / AI startups)50-150
The growth-targeting use

Low-density industries are growth targets — viral coefficient compounds: more connections per user → more PYMK suggestions → more signups. Marketing budget allocated to industries below the median.

Bidirectional vs directional

LinkedIn connections are bidirectional. COUNT(c.connection_id) double-counts (each connection = 2 rows). For "true degree" divide by 2:

COUNT(c.connection_id) / 2.0 / COUNT(DISTINCT m.member_id) AS avg_degree
The "second-degree reach"

Avg connections × avg connections = 2-hop reach. Tech: 500 × 500 = 250K reachable; Climate startup: 100 × 100 = 10K. The 25x difference drives recruiter-tool ROI.

Senior signal: LEFT JOIN to keep zero-degree members, name the bidirectional double-count, classify industries by maturity bands, and connect density to growth-targeting + 2-hop reach (the killer use case).
