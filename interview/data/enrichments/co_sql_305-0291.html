Twilio's voice team tracks "long calls" — conversations over ten minutes — as a proxy for support escalations and sales demos. Leadership wants a month-over-month growth rate, not just a count. The query looks like a clean two-step: bucket the calls, then diff consecutive months. The interview lives in two details: how you classify a "long" call, and what the growth number does for the very first month, when there is nothing to compare against.

The 601-second call is the boundary probe — > 600 counts it, >= 600 or > 10*60 would too, but a "ten minutes or more" reading needs >= 600. March falls from 3 to 1, so the growth rate goes negative — a good test that the sign is right.

2. The SQL — LAG over monthly long-call counts

WITH m AS (
  SELECT DATE_TRUNC('month', call_at)::DATE AS mo,
    SUM(CASE WHEN duration_sec > 600 THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) AS long_calls
  FROM calls GROUP BY DATE_TRUNC('month', call_at)::DATE)
SELECT mo, long_calls,
  100.0*(long_calls - LAG(long_calls) OVER (ORDER BY mo))/NULLIF(LAG(long_calls) OVER (ORDER BY mo),0) AS growth_pct
FROM m;

The CTE collapses raw calls into one row per month with a conditional count. The outer query reaches back one month with LAG and computes the percentage change. NULLIF(…,0) guards against dividing by a month that had zero long calls.

4. Strategic explanations

The first-month NULL is correct, not a bug

An interviewer will ask what the NULL means. The honest answer: there is genuinely no growth rate for the first observed month, because growth is defined relative to a previous period that does not exist. NULL is the right representation of "undefined". If the dashboard needs a value, decide deliberately — COALESCE(growth_pct, 0) reads as "flat", which is a lie; better to leave it NULL or label it "—". Never invent a 0% that implies the metric was measured.

Gap months silently disappear — the real trap

The CTE only produces rows for months that had at least one call. If April had no calls at all, there is no April row, and LAG would compare May directly to March — quietly mislabelling a two-month jump as one month of growth. On a sparse table you must densify against a calendar spine first:

WITH months AS (
  SELECT generate_series(date_trunc('month', MIN(call_at)),
                         date_trunc('month', MAX(call_at)),
                         interval '1 month')::date AS mo
  FROM calls
)
SELECT months.mo, COALESCE(m.long_calls, 0) AS long_calls, …
FROM months LEFT JOIN m ON months.mo = m.mo;

Now every month is present, empty months read as 0, and LAG always steps exactly one month.

Dividing by a zero-long-call month

NULLIF(prev, 0) is doing real work. If a month had zero long calls and the next had five, the "growth rate" is five-over-zero — mathematically infinite. NULLIF turns the denominator into NULL so the whole expression is NULL rather than a division-by-zero error. That is defensible, but tell the interviewer the product question: a jump from 0 to 5 is often reported as "new" or "n/a", not as a percentage at all.

The boundary of "long"

duration_sec > 600 means strictly longer than ten minutes — an exactly-600-second call is not long. If the spec says "ten minutes or more", use >= 600. This is a one-character decision that shifts every monthly count, so confirm it. Also consider using COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE duration_sec > 600) instead of SUM(CASE…) — identical result, and it reads as intent rather than arithmetic.

Whole-table window vs partitioned

There is no PARTITION BY here, which is correct: the whole table is one time series. The moment the question becomes "growth per region" or "per product line", the CTE gains that dimension in its GROUP BY and the window becomes LAG(long_calls) OVER (PARTITION BY region ORDER BY mo) — otherwise January of one region would diff against December of another.

Senior signal: recognise the first-month NULL as the honest value rather than papering it with a fake 0%, flag that months with no calls vanish from the CTE and break LAG unless you densify against a calendar, and treat the > 600 threshold and the divide-by-zero NULLIF as explicit product decisions, not incidental syntax.
