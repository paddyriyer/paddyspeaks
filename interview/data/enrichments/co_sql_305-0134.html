EY's sales-ops team wants a per-employee order count for a productivity review — including the reps who closed nothing this period, because a zero is itself a finding. That last clause is the entire interview: it forces a LEFT JOIN, and it sets a trap around what you put inside COUNT.

1. The source data — employees and orders

Anil owns three orders, Bina and Chandra one each, and Divya owns none. Divya is the row the interviewer cares about: a naive join would drop her, and the review would silently miss an under-performer.

2. The SQL — LEFT JOIN + COUNT

SELECT e.emp_id, COUNT(o.order_id) AS order_count
FROM employees e
LEFT JOIN orders o ON e.emp_id = o.emp_id
GROUP BY e.emp_id;

The LEFT JOIN keeps every employee even with no matching order. COUNT(o.order_id) — counting a column from the right side — is the deliberate choice that makes the zero come out right.

All four employees appear, and Divya (emp 4) shows 0. For an employee with no orders, the LEFT JOIN produces one row with o.order_id = NULL; COUNT(o.order_id) ignores NULLs, so it counts zero. The result is unordered — add ORDER BY emp_id for a stable report.

4. Strategic explanations

The COUNT(*) vs COUNT(column) trap

This is the question's payload. Swap in COUNT(*) and Divya's count becomes 1, not 0:

SELECT e.emp_id, COUNT(*) AS order_count   -- WRONG for this question
FROM employees e
LEFT JOIN orders o ON e.emp_id = o.emp_id
GROUP BY e.emp_id;

COUNT(*) counts rows, and the LEFT JOIN manufactures one all-NULL row for Divya — so COUNT(*) sees a row and reports 1. COUNT(o.order_id) counts non-NULL values of that column, and the manufactured row's order_id is NULL, so it correctly reports 0. With a LEFT JOIN, always count a non-nullable column from the right table.

Why an INNER JOIN would be wrong

An INNER JOIN keeps only rows that match on both sides, so Divya — with no order — vanishes entirely:

-- emp 4 missing from the result
SELECT e.emp_id, COUNT(o.order_id)
FROM employees e JOIN orders o ON e.emp_id = o.emp_id
GROUP BY e.emp_id;

The report would show three employees and quietly omit the one the review most needs to see. "Include employees with zero" is the phrase that mandates LEFT JOIN.

Grouping by emp_id when the report needs the name

The solution groups by e.emp_id alone, which Postgres allows here only incidentally. To also select e.name, it must be in the GROUP BY — or, cleaner, group by the primary key and let functional dependency cover the rest:

SELECT e.emp_id, e.name, COUNT(o.order_id) AS order_count
FROM employees e
LEFT JOIN orders o ON e.emp_id = o.emp_id
GROUP BY e.emp_id;   -- e.name is functionally dependent on the PK

That last form works because emp_id is the table's primary key; without a PK declaration you must list e.name explicitly.

The pre-aggregate alternative — and when it matters

For one count this is overkill, but counting in a subquery before the join avoids fan-out when you need several aggregates from different child tables:

SELECT e.emp_id, COALESCE(o.cnt, 0) AS order_count
FROM employees e
LEFT JOIN (SELECT emp_id, COUNT(*) AS cnt FROM orders GROUP BY emp_id) o
  ON e.emp_id = o.emp_id;

Here COUNT(*) is safe — it runs before the join, on real orders rows only — and COALESCE turns the missing-match NULL into 0.

Filtering on the right table breaks the LEFT JOIN

If the review only wants completed orders, a WHERE o.status = 'completed' silently demotes the join to an inner one — the NULL-filled row for Divya fails the predicate and she disappears. Put the condition in the ON clause instead: LEFT JOIN orders o ON e.emp_id = o.emp_id AND o.status = 'completed', which keeps Divya at 0.

Senior signal: use LEFT JOIN so zero-order employees survive, count o.order_id (never COUNT(*)) so the manufactured NULL row reports 0, and know that any filter on the right table belongs in the ON clause or the outer join silently becomes an inner one.
