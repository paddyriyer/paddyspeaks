An education platform wants to find "quiet" students — those who, across every exam they sat, were never the top scorer and never the bottom scorer. The interviewer plants two precise traps: a tie for top must disqualify all tied students, and a student who never took an exam should not be counted as quiet (you cannot be quiet if you never spoke). This is a window-function exclusion problem.

Trace student 3 (Cy): exam 1 he scores 80 — 3rd of 5, neither extreme; exam 2 he scores 72 — mid-pack; exam 3 he scores 80 — 2nd, not the 90 top nor the 40 bottom. Cy is never an extreme. The edge cases: a hypothetical tie for top should knock out both tied students (DENSE_RANK = 1 handles this), and student id 4 (Dee) is bottom twice — clearly loud — verifying the disqualification path.

2. The SQL — rank within each exam from both ends, exclude the extremes

WITH ranked AS (
  SELECT exam_id, student_id,
         DENSE_RANK() OVER (PARTITION BY exam_id ORDER BY score DESC) AS rk_high,
         DENSE_RANK() OVER (PARTITION BY exam_id ORDER BY score ASC)  AS rk_low
  FROM exam
),
loud AS (
  SELECT DISTINCT student_id FROM ranked WHERE rk_high = 1 OR rk_low = 1
)
SELECT s.student_id, s.student_name
FROM student s
JOIN exam e ON e.student_id = s.student_id
WHERE s.student_id NOT IN (SELECT student_id FROM loud)
GROUP BY s.student_id, s.student_name
ORDER BY s.student_id;

ranked tags every exam row with its rank from the top (rk_high) and from the bottom (rk_low). loud collects any student who hit rank 1 from either end in any exam. The final query keeps students who took an exam but never landed in loud.

4. Strategic explanations

Why DENSE_RANK and why ranking from both directions

"Top scorer" is rank 1 by score DESC; "bottom scorer" is rank 1 by score ASC. Two window functions over the same partition, opposite orderings, capture both ends. DENSE_RANK (or RANK) is chosen over ROW_NUMBER deliberately: with a tie for the top score, DENSE_RANK assigns both tied rows rk_high = 1, so both are flagged loud — exactly the spec's "they BOTH disqualify". ROW_NUMBER would arbitrarily give one of them rank 1 and the other rank 2, letting a genuine co-top-scorer slip through as "quiet".

The "took at least one exam" rule — why the JOIN matters

A student absent from exam never appears in ranked or loud, so NOT IN (loud) alone would wrongly classify them as quiet. The JOIN exam e is the guard: it restricts the result to students with at least one exam row. The trailing GROUP BY s.student_id, s.student_name then collapses the one-row-per-exam fan-out from that join back to one row per student. Here all five students sat exams, but the join is what makes the query correct for a roster that includes non-test-takers.

Set-difference logic: define "loud", then subtract

The cleanest way to express "never an extreme" is by complement: first compute everyone who was an extreme (the loud set), then take the students not in it. Trying to assert "quiet" directly — "for all of this student's exams, rank is neither 1 nor last" — needs an awkward correlated NOT EXISTS over a universally-quantified condition. Computing the positive set and subtracting is simpler and matches how an interviewer expects you to decompose the problem.

The NOT IN safety note and a cleaner finish

NOT IN (SELECT student_id FROM loud) is safe here because student_id is non-nullable, but NOT IN against a nullable subquery returns nothing — the usual footgun. NOT EXISTS, or an EXCEPT, sidesteps it. A senior might also skip the final JOIN + GROUP BY and instead drive off ranked: SELECT DISTINCT student_id FROM ranked already restricts to exam-takers, so (SELECT DISTINCT student_id FROM ranked) EXCEPT (SELECT student_id FROM loud) answers it in one set operation, then join to student only for the name.

Senior signal: rank each exam from both ends with DENSE_RANK (not ROW_NUMBER) so a tie for top disqualifies every tied student, build the "loud" set and subtract it, and use a join to exam to enforce "took at least one exam" so never-tested students are not mistaken for quiet ones.
