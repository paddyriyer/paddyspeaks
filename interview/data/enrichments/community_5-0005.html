A Community health clinic wants to flag no-show risk: which patients missed more than two appointments last quarter? A join, a two-part filter, a grouped count, a HAVING threshold — routine. The interviewer is watching whether you keep the status and date filters in WHERE (not HAVING), whether your count survives an outer join, and whether you read "more than two" as strictly greater than 2.

1. The source data — appointments (joined to patients)

Carol2exactly 2 — fails > 2
Dan11 missed + 1 Attended

Carol sits exactly on the boundary at 2 missed — she is the test of "more than two" (strict > 2 drops her). Eve has two missed appointments in the raw table, but one is in July and one in November, so the Aug–Oct WHERE filter zeroes her count. Alice's "Attended" appointment must not be counted.

2. The SQL — WHERE status + date, HAVING COUNT

SELECT
  p.patient_name,
  COUNT(a.appointment_id) AS missed_appointments
FROM appointments a
LEFT JOIN patients p ON a.patient_id = p.patient_id
WHERE a.appointment_date BETWEEN '2024-08-01' AND '2024-10-31'
  AND a.status = 'Missed'
GROUP BY p.patient_name
HAVING COUNT(a.appointment_id) > 2
ORDER BY missed_appointments DESC;

Both per-row predicates — the quarter window and status = 'Missed' — sit in WHERE, so only relevant rows ever reach the GROUP BY. HAVING then filters the grouped counts.

Eve's misses are both outside the window, so she has zero in-scope rows and never forms a group at all. A patient drops out for any of three reasons: too few misses, misses outside the window, or no misses of status 'Missed'.

4. Strategic explanations

Status filter: WHERE, not HAVING

status = 'Missed' is a property of a single appointment row, so it belongs in WHERE — evaluated before grouping. A tempting wrong approach counts conditionally instead:

-- alternative: count only missed ones via conditional aggregation
SELECT p.patient_name,
       COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE a.status = 'Missed') AS missed_appointments
FROM appointments a
LEFT JOIN patients p ON a.patient_id = p.patient_id
WHERE a.appointment_date BETWEEN '2024-08-01' AND '2024-10-31'
GROUP BY p.patient_name
HAVING COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE a.status = 'Missed') > 2;

That also works and is the right shape if you need several per-status counts in one query (missed, attended, cancelled side by side). For this single-status question the plain WHERE status = 'Missed' is simpler and lets the planner shrink the scan earlier.

"More than two" is strictly greater than 2

HAVING COUNT(...) > 2 keeps 3, 4, 5… and drops exactly-2 — that is why Carol is excluded. If the requirement were "two or more" it would be >= 2. This is the single most common off-by-one in threshold questions: read the English precisely. "More than", "over", "exceeds" all mean strict >; "at least" means >=.

The LEFT JOIN direction and COUNT(column)

The query drives from appointments and outer-joins patients. Counting a.appointment_id is correct — it is never NULL for a real appointment. Two subtleties worth voicing:

  • Driving from appointments means a patient with zero appointments simply never appears — which is fine, because zero misses can never exceed 2 anyway.
  • If an appointment row had a patient_id with no matching patient, the LEFT JOIN yields a NULL patient_name, and that orphan row would group under a NULL key. Counting COUNT(*) instead of COUNT(a.appointment_id) changes nothing here, but with the join reversed it would — counting the joined-from column is the safe habit.
Grouping by name vs id

GROUP BY p.patient_name works only because names are unique in this dataset. Two different patients both named "Alice" would be silently merged into one inflated count. Production-grade SQL groups by the key and carries the label: GROUP BY p.patient_id, p.patient_name. Strict engines force you to list patient_name anyway; MySQL's loose mode is where the duplicate-name bug slips through unnoticed.

Edge case: the inclusive date boundary

BETWEEN '2024-08-01' AND '2024-10-31' captures all of October only because appointment_date is a DATE. If it were a TIMESTAMP, an appointment at 2024-10-31 14:00 would fall after the literal 2024-10-31 00:00:00 and be dropped. The portable fix is the half-open range >= '2024-08-01' AND < '2024-11-01' — correct regardless of the column's time component.

Senior signal: push per-row predicates (status, date) into WHERE so grouping sees only relevant rows, read "more than two" as strict > 2, count the non-null joined column to stay correct under a LEFT JOIN, group by the patient key rather than the name, and prefer a half-open date range so a TIMESTAMP column cannot silently drop boundary rows.
