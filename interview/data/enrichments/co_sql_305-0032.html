Atlassian's people-analytics team wants each employee's pay rank within their department — "you are the 3rd highest paid on your team". The reference solution does it with a correlated subquery that counts higher earners. It produces the right numbers, but it also bakes in a specific tie convention and a quadratic cost, and the interviewer wants you to name both.

1. The source data — employees

Bob and Cara are tied at $120K in department 10. That tie is the crux — it decides what rank Ann gets, and whether the ranking skips a number.

2. The SQL — correlated COUNT subquery

SELECT emp_id, dept_id, salary,
  (SELECT COUNT(*)+1 FROM employees e2 WHERE e2.dept_id = e.dept_id AND e2.salary > e.salary) AS dept_rank
FROM employees e;

For each row, the subquery counts how many same-department colleagues earn strictly more, then adds 1. Zero higher earners → rank 1.

Bob and Cara both score 1 — correct, neither has anyone above them. But then Ann at $90K scores 3, not 2: the rank sequence for dept 10 is 1, 1, 3, 4. Rank 2 was skipped. That gap is a deliberate convention, not a bug — but you must know which one you shipped.

4. Strategic explanations

This is RANK() semantics, made by hand

"Count how many are strictly greater, plus one" is exactly the definition of competition ranking: ties share the top rank, and the next value jumps by the number of tied rows. That is what RANK() does. The cleaner, faster equivalent is a window function:

SELECT emp_id, dept_id, salary,
       RANK() OVER (PARTITION BY dept_id ORDER BY salary DESC) AS dept_rank
FROM employees;

Same 1, 1, 3, 4 for dept 10 — one pass over the rows, no subquery. The correlated form is the thing you write when you have forgotten window functions exist.

The skipped rank — RANK vs DENSE_RANK

If the business statement is "Ann is the 2nd-highest distinct salary on her team", the gap is wrong and you want dense ranking. The hand-rolled fix is to count distinct higher salaries instead of higher rows:

-- competition rank (skips):     COUNT(*)         where salary > e.salary, +1   => 1,1,3,4
-- dense rank   (no skip):       COUNT(DISTINCT salary) where salary > e.salary, +1 => 1,1,2,3
employeesalaryRANKDENSE_RANKROW_NUMBER
Bob$120K111

Confirm the wording with the interviewer before choosing. "Rank" usually means competition rank; "Nth highest salary" usually means dense rank.

The performance trap: this is O(N²)

A correlated subquery re-executes once per outer row. For N employees that is N scans of the table — quadratic. On 6 rows it is invisible; on 6 million it is a query that never finishes. The window-function version sorts once per partition: roughly O(N log N). When an interviewer asks "how does this scale?", that contrast is the answer they want.

The ROW_NUMBER alternative — and why it is wrong here

ROW_NUMBER() would give Bob and Cara different ranks (1 and 2) on an arbitrary tiebreak, because it never ties. That breaks "you are joint-top of your team". Use ROW_NUMBER only when you genuinely need a unique sequence (pagination, dedupe) and have a deterministic tiebreaker like ORDER BY salary DESC, emp_id.

Edge cases: NULL salary and single-person departments

If salary can be NULL, the comparison e2.salary > e.salary is never true for the NULL row, so a NULL-salary employee always lands at rank 1 — almost certainly wrong. Decide whether unpriced rows are excluded or ranked last. A one-person department (none here, but common) correctly gets rank 1 under either convention — that case is safe.

Senior signal: recognise the correlated COUNT+1 as a hand-rolled RANK(), rewrite it as a PARTITION BY window function, explain that the original is O(N²) while the window version is O(N log N), and pin down whether the business wants RANK (gaps) or DENSE_RANK (no gaps) before committing.
