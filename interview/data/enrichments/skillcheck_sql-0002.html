A retention analyst wants the list of customers who placed more than three orders during 2025 — the kind of cohort that feeds a loyalty programme. It looks like a one-line GROUP BY, but the interviewer is watching two things: how you fence the year boundary, and whether "more than 3" goes in WHERE or HAVING. Both have a wrong answer that still looks right on small data.

Two deliberate traps. Customer 100 and 102 each sit exactly at 4 — testing strict vs inclusive comparison. Customer 103 has a single order dated 2024-12-31: a careless year filter (or a BETWEEN with the wrong end) can let that boundary row leak into 2025.

2. The SQL — half-open range filter then HAVING

SELECT customer_id, COUNT(*) AS order_count
FROM orders
WHERE order_date >= '2025-01-01' AND order_date < '2026-01-01'
GROUP BY customer_id
HAVING COUNT(*) > 3
ORDER BY customer_id;

WHERE runs first, keeping only 2025 rows. GROUP BY customer_id collapses each customer's surviving rows into one group, COUNT(*) counts them, and HAVING COUNT(*) > 3 filters the groups — you cannot use an aggregate in WHERE because no group exists yet at that stage.

4. Strategic explanations

The half-open range beats BETWEEN for dates

order_date >= '2025-01-01' AND order_date < '2026-01-01' is a half-open interval: it includes all of 2025 and excludes everything from 2026 onward. The naive alternative, BETWEEN '2025-01-01' AND '2025-12-31', is inclusive on both ends — fine for a pure DATE, but if order_date is ever a TIMESTAMP it silently drops anything timestamped on 2025-12-31 after midnight. The < next-year pattern is correct regardless of whether the column is a date or a timestamp.

WHERE vs HAVING — they filter different things

WHERE filters rows before grouping; HAVING filters groups after aggregation. The date predicate is a per-row test, so it belongs in WHERE — and putting it there also shrinks the data before the expensive grouping. The count threshold is a per-group test on an aggregate, so it can only live in HAVING. Writing WHERE COUNT(*) > 3 is a hard error: the aggregate is not yet computed when WHERE runs.

Comparing date strings — why it works here

order_date is declared TEXT, yet the comparison still works because the dates are in ISO-8601 (YYYY-MM-DD), and ISO dates sort identically as text and as dates. This is a lucky property of the format, not a general rule — '01/15/2025' or '15-Jan-2025' would compare wrongly. The robust move is to store dates in a real DATE column; if you must keep text, cast it (order_date::date) so intent is explicit and non-ISO strings fail loudly.

Edge case: the boundary row and "exactly N"

Customer 103's order on 2024-12-31 is the classic off-by-one trap — an inclusive lower bound of >= '2025-01-01' correctly excludes it; a sloppy > '2024-12-31' would too, but BETWEEN '2024-12-31' ... would not. Separately, customers at exactly 4 orders qualify only because the test is strict >. Always pin down whether the spec means "more than 3" (> 3) or "3 or more" (>= 3) — they differ by a whole cohort.

Senior signal: fence the year with a half-open >= start AND < next-year range instead of an inclusive BETWEEN, put the row-level date test in WHERE and the aggregate threshold in HAVING, know that ISO-8601 text dates sort correctly only by luck of format, and confirm whether "more than 3" is strict > or inclusive >=.
