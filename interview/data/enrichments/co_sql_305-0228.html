Activision Blizzard's player-monetisation analysts want to know how spend breaks down by demographic — total revenue per gender and age cohort. The query is a join plus a group-by, and it looks routine. But the choice of join type quietly decides whether non-spending players vanish, and grouping on raw age instead of an age band is the modelling decision an interviewer is really probing.

1. The source data — customers and purchases

Three different customers (1, 3, 5) are all F, age 28 — they collapse into a single output row. Customer 1 has two purchases. Customer 5 has no purchase rows at all. Those facts drive the result.

2. The SQL — JOIN + GROUP BY (gender, age)

SELECT c.gender, c.age, SUM(p.amount) AS spend
FROM customers c JOIN purchases p ON c.customer_id = p.customer_id
GROUP BY c.gender, c.age;

An inner join pairs each customer with their purchase rows, then GROUP BY (gender, age) sums the amounts within each cohort. Customer 1's two purchases (120 + 30) both fall into the F/28 bucket.

F/28 totals 310 — that is 120 + 30 from customer 1 plus 80 from customer 3; customer 5 contributes nothing. The INNER JOIN dropped customer 5 entirely because they have no purchase rows. So this query reports spend among spenders, not spend across the cohort. Whether that is right depends on the question.

4. Strategic explanations

The inner-join trap — non-spenders disappear

If the analyst expects every demographic cohort to appear — including ones with zero revenue — the inner join is wrong, because cohorts made up entirely of non-spenders never surface. Use a LEFT JOIN and COALESCE the sum:

SELECT c.gender, c.age, COALESCE(SUM(p.amount), 0) AS spend
FROM customers c
LEFT JOIN purchases p ON c.customer_id = p.customer_id
GROUP BY c.gender, c.age;

Now customer 5's cohort appears with spend = 0 instead of being silently excluded. State the choice: "do you want all cohorts, or only cohorts that spent?"

Grouping on raw age is almost never what they want

Age 28, 29, 30 each become a separate row. Real demographic reporting uses bands — 18–24, 25–34, and so on. Bucket before grouping:

SELECT c.gender,
       CASE WHEN c.age < 25 THEN '18-24'
            WHEN c.age < 35 THEN '25-34'
            WHEN c.age < 50 THEN '35-49'
            ELSE '50+' END AS age_band,
       COALESCE(SUM(p.amount), 0) AS spend
FROM customers c
LEFT JOIN purchases p ON c.customer_id = p.customer_id
GROUP BY 1, 2;

Raising the age-band question unprompted signals you think about the consumer of the report, not just the literal columns.

SUM vs COUNT — revenue is not the same as buyers

"Spend" here is SUM(amount). But monetisation teams usually want several measures side by side — revenue, number of paying customers, and average ticket. SUM alone hides whether 310 came from one whale or three modest buyers:

SUM(p.amount)                       AS revenue,
COUNT(DISTINCT c.customer_id)        AS paying_customers,
SUM(p.amount) / COUNT(DISTINCT c.customer_id) AS arpu

Note COUNT(DISTINCT customer_id), not COUNT(*) — customer 1's two purchase rows must not count them twice.

NULL demographics

If gender or age can be NULL, those rows form their own group — GROUP BY treats NULL as a single bucket. That is often acceptable as an "unknown" cohort, but call it out: a NULL-age row will not fall into any CASE band above unless you add an explicit WHEN c.age IS NULL arm, and will instead drop into the ELSE '50+' bucket — a silent mislabel.

Senior signal: name the inner-join-drops-non-spenders behaviour and switch to LEFT JOIN when zero-revenue cohorts must appear, propose age bands over raw age for demographic reporting, use COUNT(DISTINCT customer_id) so multi-purchase customers aren't double-counted, and handle a NULL age explicitly so it isn't mislabelled into the wrong band.
