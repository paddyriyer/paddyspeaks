The Room · Scar File 1

What the interviewer is scoring right now

Every senior ads DE loop has some version of the question Amelia just asked. The interviewer watches for four moves in the first ninety seconds.

  1. Did you ask which definition of revenue she means? Billed, spend, attributed, settled — four numbers, four audiences. The candidate who answers "revenue" without disambiguating has already demonstrated the failure mode the incident is exposing.
  2. Did you separate "which is correct" from "which is most useful right now"? Both can be correct; neither may be fit for the 10:30 board prep. Senior engineers hold both ideas in the air simultaneously.
  3. Did you quantify your time commitment? "I need a while" is an amateur answer. "45 minutes, with a specific deliverable" is a senior one. The director could only stall for you because you were specific.
  4. Did you end with a recommendation, not a number? The output of the next 45 minutes is not "the revenue." It is "here is the revenue under definition X, here is what the $900K gap is composed of, and here is which number you should carry into the board prep given the audience."

Candidates who answer Amelia with a number have shown the interviewer they don't understand which room they are in. The answer is always a definition before a number.

The Landmine · 1 of many

"Revenue" is not a column in a table

At StreamCo, the word "revenue" refers to at least four different numbers, each stored in a different system, each produced by a different pipeline, each measuring a different population at a different time. When a senior engineer hears "the revenue number is wrong," they hear it as "which of the four revenues, for which audience, as of which day?" The junior engineer hears it as "I need to go query something."

This is the landmine that produced today's incident. Someone, months ago, built a dashboard labelled Revenue — September that reads sum(spend_cents)/100 from silver.fact_impression. That is spend revenue. Finance's $9.1M is settled revenue — post-refund, post-IVT-credit, post-partner-attribution-lag, sourced from the ledger in Salesforce. The pipeline was correct. The label was lying.

The fix for this incident is not in SQL. It is in the label.

Act 2 · What Is Revenue?

Four numbers, four audiences

09:58 AM. The dashboard says $8.2M. Finance says $9.1M. Both are correct. They are measuring different things. You need to name which thing is being measured before you can name the gap.

Before you write a single line of SQL, you pull out the paper notebook on your desk and draw four boxes. A senior ads DE at StreamCo should be able to name these four in their sleep — the interview asks for them directly in the first round.

Billed revenue
Who owns it: Finance
What advertisers have been invoiced for. Produced by the billing system (the Salesforce ledger, in StreamCo's case). Lags reality by the billing cycle — typically a month — and excludes anything the invoice hasn't been sent for yet.
When it updates: month-end close plus a 3-day settlement window.
Spend revenue
Who owns it: Ads pipeline
Sum of auction-clearing prices × served impressions, converted at the advertiser's contracted rate. Produced near-real-time by the impression pipeline. Knows about impressions the billing system does not yet know about; does not know about refunds or chargebacks.
When it updates: streaming, ~5-minute lag.
Attributed revenue
Who owns it: Ads measurement
Revenue credited to specific campaigns under an attribution model — last-click 7-day, last-touch, MTA linear, whatever the advertiser's contract specifies. Depends on the attribution window, the touch model, and the identity graph. Almost always differs from spend revenue because it excludes unattributable spend and includes post-attribution adjustments.
When it updates: nightly batch for the 28-day window; streaming for the 24-hour window.
Settled revenue
Who owns it: Finance
The number Finance closes the month on. Post-refund, post-chargeback, post-IVT-recovery credits, post-partner-attribution-lag, post-currency-revaluation. Sourced from the ledger after all adjustments have posted. This is what the 10-Q filing is built from.
When it updates: monthly, with quarter-end revisions.

Four numbers, one word, four audiences. The CFO's $9.1M is settled revenue. The dashboard's $8.2M is spend revenue labelled Revenue — September by somebody eighteen months ago who didn't know which word they were stealing. Everyone after them has been tripping over it.

The gap is $0.9M. You now have to explain where it went.

Your Turn · SQL

The reconciliation query

Before reading the walkthrough below, give yourself fifteen minutes. The table silver.fact_impression has one row per served impression with spend_cents, advertiser_id, campaign_id, impression_ts, is_valid (false = flagged as invalid traffic), currency, fx_rate_to_usd. The Finance ledger in finance.ad_revenue_ledger has one row per (advertiser_id, month) with invoiced_usd, refunds_usd, ivt_credits_usd, partner_attribution_adjustment_usd.

Write a single query that reconciles these for September and produces one row with columns:

  • spend_revenue_usd — from the pipeline
  • pipeline_ivt_flagged_usd — impressions where is_valid = FALSE
  • finance_ivt_credits_usd — from the ledger
  • finance_refunds_usd
  • finance_partner_adjustment_usd
  • settled_revenue_usd — what Finance closes on
  • unexplained_gap_usd — the part nobody can account for
Show the reference solution 
WITH pipeline AS (
  SELECT
    SUM(spend_cents * fx_rate_to_usd) / 100.0                                    AS spend_revenue_usd,
    SUM(CASE WHEN is_valid = FALSE THEN spend_cents * fx_rate_to_usd ELSE 0 END) / 100.0
                                                                                  AS pipeline_ivt_flagged_usd
  FROM silver.fact_impression
  WHERE impression_ts >= '2025-09-01'
    AND impression_ts <  '2025-10-01'
),
ledger AS (
  SELECT
    SUM(invoiced_usd)                                AS invoiced_usd,
    SUM(refunds_usd)                                 AS finance_refunds_usd,
    SUM(ivt_credits_usd)                             AS finance_ivt_credits_usd,
    SUM(partner_attribution_adjustment_usd)          AS finance_partner_adjustment_usd,
    SUM(invoiced_usd)
      - SUM(refunds_usd) - SUM(ivt_credits_usd)
      + SUM(partner_attribution_adjustment_usd)      AS settled_revenue_usd
  FROM finance.ad_revenue_ledger
  WHERE month = '2025-09-01'
)
SELECT
  p.spend_revenue_usd,
  p.pipeline_ivt_flagged_usd,
  l.finance_ivt_credits_usd,
  l.finance_refunds_usd,
  l.finance_partner_adjustment_usd,
  l.settled_revenue_usd,
  -- The unexplained gap: what the four adjustments can't account for.
  (l.settled_revenue_usd - p.spend_revenue_usd)
    - (p.pipeline_ivt_flagged_usd - l.finance_ivt_credits_usd)
    + l.finance_refunds_usd
    - l.finance_partner_adjustment_usd               AS unexplained_gap_usd
FROM pipeline p CROSS JOIN ledger l;

What the query should tell you, today:

  • spend_revenue_usd = $8.21M — matches the dashboard
  • finance_partner_adjustment_usd = $0.74M — programmatic partner attribution that lands in the ledger on a 14-day lag
  • finance_ivt_credits_usd = $0.21M (vs pipeline_ivt_flagged_usd = $0.05M) — Finance caught $160K of IVT our pipeline did not
  • settled_revenue_usd = $9.12M
  • unexplained_gap_usd = $0.01M — within rounding

The $0.9M gap decomposes cleanly: $740K partner attribution lag + $160K IVT delta. Neither is a bug in the pipeline. Both are expected because Finance's source of truth arrives later and covers some categories the pipeline doesn't.

The Room · Scar File 2

The interviewer who asks this exact question

Every senior ads DE loop at StreamCo-sized companies has this scenario, phrased about a dozen different ways. The short version: "the dashboard shows X, finance shows Y, which is right?"

The correct answer is never a number. It is always:

  1. Name the four definitions (billed / spend / attributed / settled).
  2. Ask which audience the consumer is serving.
  3. Point out that the labels on the dashboards are the bug, not the numbers.
  4. Propose that the labels be pinned to the consumer's workflow going forward.

Candidates who answer with SQL in the first thirty seconds have revealed they don't know what question they were asked. The interviewer moves on; the round is scored down before any technical depth gets tested.

You pass this question by refusing to answer it the way it was asked.

The Landmine · 2 of many

The dashboard label is the schema

The bug that caused today's incident was shipped eighteen months ago when someone built the first ads dashboard and labelled the column Revenue — September. There was no malice. There was a rush. There was no definition sheet. The dashboard went live. A hundred people started using it. The label calcified into the organization's mental model of what "revenue" means on the ads team, and the engineering team inherited a political debt it did not know it was carrying.

The senior fix is not to rename this one dashboard. The senior fix is to require every dashboard at StreamCo to carry a definition footer: which table, which attribution window, which revenue concept (billed / spend / attributed / settled), which currency basis. New dashboards without a footer fail code review. Existing dashboards get back-filled in Q4 as a team OKR.

This is the kind of fix senior engineers propose because they have lived through the consequence. Junior engineers propose query tuning.

Rule: if a number has a label, the label is part of the schema. Treat it that way.
