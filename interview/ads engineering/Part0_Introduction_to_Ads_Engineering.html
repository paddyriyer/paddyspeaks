Act 1 · Round 1 · 10:00 AM

You are in Round 1.

The video is on. The interviewer's name is Raj. He is Staff Engineer on the ads platform team at StreamCo. He has been in the chair on your side of this conversation four times in the past two weeks. He has seen enough candidates freeze on this round to know that the freeze is the thing being scored.

He clears his throat. He says:

System-design prompt · 45 minutes

"Design a unified ads metrics platform at StreamCo scale. Assume 260 million subscribers, ad-supported tier with roughly two billion impressions per day, four downstream consumers — Finance, Marketing, Advertisers, and Sellers. Four weeks to ship the first version. You have the floor."

Raj leans back. The timer in the corner of your screen starts counting up from zero.

You take a breath. You have done this before. You open your notebook app and sketch the standard boxes — Kafka at the left, Flink and Spark in the middle, a medallion lake, a serving layer. Three minutes in, you begin to talk. You name the event spine. You name the bronze. You start on the attribution strategy.

At 3:47 — you see the timer — Raj raises a hand.

Raj: "Pause. Good start. Let me make this more interesting. Something just happened in production this morning. I'm going to share my screen."

He shares his screen. It's a Slack channel. The timer keeps running.

#incidents-ads-de · live · 11 messages in the last 3 minutes
CFO Amelia
@channel the Q3 exec deck shows ad-supported revenue at $9.1M for September. Our finance dashboard pulls from ads_warehouse and shows $8.2M. Which one is correct? I need a single answer by the 10:30 board prep.
CMO Darren
Who shipped last night? Three advertiser campaigns just auto-paused because their ROAS dashboard tanked. The advertiser relationship team is getting tweets.
Partnerships · Priya
Two content partners are asking why their licensing royalty numbers on the partner portal jumped 14% overnight. They want a call this afternoon.
Director
Who's on this? I need something I can say in the 10:30 board prep. Any number with its definition.
Raj: "Assume you're the tech lead on the ads data-engineering team and you just got paged. It's 09:47 local time. Three separate incidents, or maybe one. You have thirty seconds before the director DMs you personally. What do you say to her, and why?"

You have thirty seconds. The whiteboard you drew is now behind you. The system-design prompt is still in the brief — Raj didn't retract it — but the clock on that stopped the moment the clock on this started. You are being tested on two things at once: what you would ship in four weeks, and what you would say in thirty seconds. Both answers have to survive the other.

You have three options. Each of them costs something. Pick one wrong and the cost compounds for the rest of the loop.

A"Both numbers are right. They measure different things."

This is very likely the correct answer. It is also the answer you earn on day 180, not day zero. Without receipts, the CFO hears it as hedging. Her last data-team lead got fired partly for hedging.

Cost if you say it now: low if you're right, catastrophic if you're slightly wrong. You have no receipts. Raj will take note.

B"Give me until 10:40. I'll have the reconciliation and a recommendation."

Buys fifty-three minutes. Commits you to delivering a specific output by a specific time. Ships nothing if you do not investigate immediately. It buys authority — the director can stall the board prep for you because you have been specific.

Cost if you pick this: you spend the next 45 minutes reconciling. The system-design prompt gets re-opened at 10:45 with less runway. You accept that trade. You say this answer.

C"Dashboard is the source of truth. $8.2M."

Fast, decisive, confidently wrong. The dashboard is not the source of truth; it is a view. If the CFO ships $8.2M to the board and Finance arrives independently at $9.1M in the monthly close, the data team's credibility is gone for two quarters. Raj has seen this answer three times in the last month. He moves the round into the scorecard column he did not want to move it into.

Cost: career-making in the short term, career-ending in the medium.

You pick B. Out loud, to Raj, steady:

You: "I tell the director I need until 10:40. Both numbers are probably correct under different definitions — at StreamCo scale there are at least four things that get called ad revenue, and nobody has labelled which is on the dashboard. I reconcile, and I come back with a recommendation she can carry into the board prep. That's the first thirty seconds. The rest of the answer depends on which of the four definitions we discover the dashboard is actually computing."

Raj nods once. The timer reads 4:31. He says:

Raj: "Good. Now walk me through the reconciliation. Assume you have warehouse access. I want the four definitions, the query shape, and the paragraph you send the director at 10:40. And Raj's voice flattens slightly here — that's the scoring signal. Not a gotcha. A depth-check."

You have forty minutes of interview left, three Slack fires to unpack, and a whiteboard behind you still carrying a half-drawn architecture for a system nobody in this story has yet agreed needs to exist. Welcome to Round 1.

The Room · What Raj is scoring in the first 90 seconds

Four moves. Any one failed drops the round.

  1. Did you refuse to answer the question as asked? The prompt said "which one is correct." The answer that wins is "neither; here is what each is measuring." Candidates who pick a number lose the round before they finish the sentence.
  2. Did you give a time commitment? "I need some time" is amateur. "Forty-five minutes with a specific deliverable" is senior. The director could only stall the board for you because you were specific.
  3. Did you end with a recommendation, not an answer? The output is not "the number." It is "here is the number under definition X, here is what the gap is made of, and here is which number to put in front of which audience going forward."
  4. Did you name what you don't yet know? You said "probably correct under different definitions" and "depends on which of the four the dashboard is actually computing." That humility is the signal. Candidates who fake certainty score lower than candidates who name what they haven't verified.

The trap in this round is that it looks like a system-design round and it is actually an incident-response round wrapped in a system-design brief. If you start drawing boxes, you miss it.

The Landmine · The word "revenue" is not a column

At StreamCo there are at least four numbers called "revenue."

Each sits in a different system. Each is produced by a different pipeline. Each measures a different population as of a different day. When a senior engineer hears "the revenue number is wrong," they hear it as "which of the four revenues, for which audience, as of which day?" The junior engineer hears it as "I need to go query something."

This is the landmine that produced today's incident. Someone eighteen months ago shipped a dashboard labelled Revenue — September that reads SUM(spend_cents)/100 from silver.fact_impression. That is spend revenue. Finance's $9.1M is settled revenue. The pipeline was correct. The label was lying. A hundred people started using the dashboard. The label calcified into the organization's mental model. The engineering team inherited a political debt it did not know it was carrying.

The senior fix is not to rename one dashboard. It is to require every dashboard at StreamCo to carry a definition footer — which table, which attribution window, which revenue concept, which currency basis. New dashboards without the footer fail code review. Existing dashboards get back-filled this quarter as a team OKR.

Rule, memorized: if a number has a label, the label is part of the schema. Treat it that way.

At 4:42 on Raj's clock you begin the reconciliation. That is Act 2. It is where the four definitions get named on the record, and where the quarter is quietly bought.

Where this is going · The system spine

One system, threaded through the whole round.

Raj's system-design prompt — build a unified ads metrics platform — is not going away. The incident he dropped on you is the reason the platform has to be built. Every architectural choice you make from here has to prevent the kind of silent label-drift that caused this morning. Every objection Raj raises has to survive a comparison to the specific thing that went wrong today. Every query you write in the SQL round has to be runnable against the platform you just described. And when the system breaks — and it will break, on Black Friday, live, while the interviewer watches — you will be debugging the same platform you designed in Act 3.

You are not touring concepts. You are inside one system, with one team, under one clock, for the rest of the article. Read it that way.
