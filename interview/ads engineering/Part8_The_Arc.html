You Just Got Hired. Now Survive.

Day one. Marketing says revenue is under-reported by 12%. Finance expected $9.1M, the dashboard says $8.2M. Campaigns are pausing. Slack is on fire. No theory. Just the room you just walked into.

Act 1 — The Incident

Tuesday · 09:47 AM
CFO The Q3 exec deck has revenue as $9.1M. Our dashboard shows $8.2M. Which is right?
CMO Who shipped last night? Three campaigns just auto-paused because of the ROAS drop.
Seller Ops Top 5 sellers all flagging their promoted-listing revenue is off. Getting phone calls.
Director · DM to you Need a number by 11. Any number. But the right one.
You — ads DE tech lead, four days into the job. You have not even merged your first PR. Your laptop is still being provisioned for one of the data warehouses.

You have three choices right now. Each one costs something.

Option A — "Both numbers are right, they measure different things."

This is the senior answer. But it is the answer you earn on day 180, not day 4. Without receipts, the CFO hears it as hedging.

Cost now: low if true, catastrophic if wrong. Do not pick this until you have the receipts.

Option B — "Let me have until 11. I'll find the number."

Buys ~70 minutes. Ships nothing if you do not actually deliver. The only viable move if you start investigating now, not after the 10am standup.

Cost now: you spend the morning in the query tool. Everything else on your plate slips a day. Acceptable.

Option C — "Dashboard is the source of truth. $8.2M."

Fast, decisive, confidently wrong. The dashboard is not the source of truth — it is a view. If the CFO ships $8.2M and Finance independently arrives at $9.1M in the monthly close, the credibility of the data team is gone for two quarters.

Cost now: catastrophic if you don't verify.

You pick B. You message the director: "Give me until 11. I need to see both queries and reconcile." She sends back a thumbs-up. The clock is 09:52.

Act 2 — What Is Revenue?

Before you write one line of SQL, you have to answer a question nobody in Slack has asked out loud. What, precisely, is the number?

Four things "revenue" could mean. They do not have the same value.
  • Billed revenue — what advertisers have been charged for (invoiced). Lags reality; excludes unpaid.
  • Spend revenue — auction-clearing prices for served impressions, converted to platform revenue. Near-real-time; doesn't account for refunds.
  • Attributed revenue — revenue credited to specific campaigns via attribution. Depends on window, touch model, and identity stitch.
  • Settled revenue — the number Finance closes the month on. Post-refund, post-chargeback, post-IVT-recovery credits.
Which one is the CFO's $9.1M?

Settled revenue. Finance runs a monthly close from the ledger in Salesforce/NetSuite, which includes refund adjustments the pipeline hasn't propagated yet. It also uses the vendor's own attribution statement for the affiliate channel, which runs on a 14-day lag.

Not the same number the dashboard computes. Not even close.

Which one is the dashboard's $8.2M?

Attributed revenue, last-click-7-day, excluding the affiliate channel (because the pipeline doesn't ingest affiliate postbacks — that sits in a separate system nobody wired into the warehouse). Excludes ~$0.6M in cross-device conversions that the identity graph couldn't stitch.

Both numbers are correct for their purpose. Neither is "the revenue."

The candidate mistake most people make here.

Trying to "reconcile" the two. There is no reconciliation. They measure different populations with different methodologies. The correct move is to surface the definition of each number on every dashboard and let consumers pick the view fit for their decision.

The junior candidate writes a query. The senior candidate writes a one-sentence definition and labels every dashboard with it. That is the moment the CFO stops asking you the question every month.

You write the clarification note at 10:38. You send it to the CFO and the director. You ask the director to convene the CFO, CMO, and Finance controller for a 15-minute meeting tomorrow to agree on which number lives on which dashboard. The director forwards it. The CFO replies in four minutes: "Thank you. This is the first time anyone has explained this to me."

You have just bought yourself the quarter. You did not write a single line of SQL.

Act 3 — The Architecture

Now that the room cares, you earn the right to draw boxes. In the tomorrow-morning meeting you present the pipeline as it exists, and the three gaps that produced yesterday's incident. Then you propose the minimum change that closes those gaps without breaking anything that works.

The three gaps you're closing

Gap 1Affiliate postbacks are not in the warehouse

What exists: the affiliate vendor sends daily S2S postbacks to a legacy receiver that writes to an Airtable — yes, Airtable — that nobody queries.

What you build: a two-file Kafka Connect bridge: Airtable → Kafka topic affiliate_postback. Flink job materializes into silver.fact_affiliate_conversion. Schema is dictated by the vendor's postback spec.

Why this goes first: the missing $0.9M is almost entirely this channel. Closing the gap recovers 90% of the discrepancy.

Gap 2Attribution is upsert-into-daily-roll-up

What exists: the nightly job computes "yesterday's revenue by campaign" and UPSERTS into a daily table. Late-arriving conversions rewrite yesterday's row. Every time they do, dashboards that cached yesterday drift against dashboards that re-queried it.

What you build: append-only conversions table with both event_time (world-time) and attribution_time (book-time). Two views on top — one keyed on event_time for cohort analysis, one on attribution_time for daily reporting. Finance reads the second; BI reads the first.

Why second: this is the fix that eliminates the "the numbers changed" class of incidents for the next five years. It's a schema change with a migration window — one week — so it cannot ship in the first week.

Gap 3Identity graph coverage is not monitored

What exists: the identity team pushes a new graph weekly. Your pipeline joins to it with an INNER JOIN. When confidence thresholds change, your cross-device conversions silently drop.

What you build: a contract test that compares today's covered-user count to a 7-day baseline. >2% drift fails the pipeline and pages the identity team's on-call. LEFT JOIN with an 'UNSTITCHED' bucket so the drop is a visible metric instead of a silent mystery.

Why last: it is additive, doesn't require a migration, and gives you a scheduled surprise protection mechanism for the quarter.

Three changes. None of them green-field. Each closes a specific wound visible in this week's incident. That is the shape of a senior architecture proposal — named incidents, named fixes, named rejection of the work you are not doing.

Act 4 — The Defense

You present the three-gap plan in the Thursday architecture review. Five people show up. Each one has a sharp question. You've rehearsed.

Raj (staff eng): "Why not streaming for billing? Kappa everything."

"Finance will not sign off on a revenue number that depends on a Flink checkpoint. If the checkpoint is corrupted and we rebuild state, yesterday's number might shift — even if reality didn't. That is the single fact that kills Kappa for billing. Streaming owns pacing; batch owns the ledger. I will fight for this one."

Darren (CMO): "Why is pacing thirty seconds behind? I want real-time."

"Thirty seconds is the tolerance the bidder accepts as normal. Sub-ten-second pacing costs roughly three times the infrastructure — dedicated Flink cluster plus sub-partition sharding. We'd be spending $40K/month to be thirty seconds more current on a decision that is already being made at five-minute granularity by your campaign managers. If an experiment shows the sub-ten-second number moves revenue materially, I'll build it. Today it doesn't."

Amelia (CFO): "Why do I need both event_time and attribution_time? It feels like a made-up complexity."

"Because your quarterly close needs 'revenue as reported on the day' — attribution_time — so the audit trail is reproducible. BI's cohort analysis needs 'revenue as now understood' — event_time — so late-arriving purchases flow back to the click week that earned them. Both are correct, for different decisions. Without both, every time a late conversion arrives, someone's dashboard silently drifts. One column cost, two audiences served."

Ayesha (legal): "You're joining to the identity graph. Are all those edges consented?"

"The identity graph team owns the consent filter upstream. I consume the already-filtered graph. What I don't do is re-derive identity from raw events on my side — that would route around their governance. If the consent filter shifts, my contract test catches it as a coverage change. I will add a note to the contract that coverage drops attributable to consent shifts are expected and not treated as incidents."

Priya (seller): "Sellers are asking why their promoted-listing revenue jumps between hours."

"Two reasons, both fixed by this plan. Hourly gold refresh means a seller who checked at 10:03 saw the 10:00 snapshot; at 10:59 they saw the same one. If they refreshed at 11:00, the number jumped by a whole hour of activity. I'm adding a 'last updated' timestamp to every number on the seller dashboard and a one-line explanation. Second: the attribution change above means late conversions no longer rewrite yesterday — sellers' numbers stop drifting at the day boundary."

Notice the pattern. Every answer names the specific pain the alternative causes. "Kappa for billing" isn't rejected on principle — it's rejected because Finance will not sign. "Sub-ten-second pacing" isn't rejected — it's deferred until we can show it moves revenue. Senior defense is always specific. Never categorical.

Act 5 — Everything Breaks

Three weeks later. The three-gap plan shipped. Affiliate channel flowing. Attribution table append-only. Identity contract test live. You are quietly optimistic. Black Friday is Friday.

Thursday 23:47 PT. You are eating dinner. The pager goes off.

T+0 min — Page: pacing_counter_stale. Flink consumer lag 48s. Over tolerance (30s).
T+3 min — You log in. Kafka consumer lag on impression_events is climbing linearly. One partition is hot.
T+7 min — You identify the hot partition. It's 87% of traffic. A top seller's promo creative went viral. Partition key is advertiser_id. The hot advertiser has saturated one broker.
T+12 min — Second page: conversion_dedupe_anomaly. The suspected-duplicates sidecar is filling 4× faster than baseline. Something about tonight's traffic is triggering dedupe false positives — or real dupes are surging.
T+18 min — Third alert: seller-dashboard Trino queries timing out. The identity graph team has just pushed a new version of the graph with stricter confidence thresholds. Your contract test fails. Coverage down 9%.
T+22 min — Slack DM from the CMO: "The pacing counter on Campaign X shows we've over-delivered by $18K in the past hour. Is the number real?"
T+25 min — Director joins the incident channel. Black Friday opens in 7 hours.

You have three incidents at once — Kafka hotspot, dedupe anomaly, identity coverage drop. Each one is degrading a different consumer. You cannot work all three in parallel. You have to triage.

How do you triage three live incidents on the eve of Black Friday?

Rank by money-at-risk over time, not by which alert is loudest. Three questions, in order:

  1. Is any of these causing advertiser overspend? Yes — the stale pacing counter is letting Campaign X overspend. Overspend at Black Friday traffic is money that is already gone. This one first.
  2. Is any of these causing irreversible data loss? No. The dedupe sidecar is accumulating audit rows; the identity drop is producing "UNSTITCHED" labels on real conversions. Both are recoverable later.
  3. Is any of these breaking a dashboard that someone will see in the next 12 hours? Yes — seller-dashboard queries. Fix second because sellers will open the site at 8am Friday.

The dedupe sidecar anomaly goes third. It is not causing incorrect output right now; it is a signal that something is unusual. Investigate after the page is quiet. Write down the timestamp so you can pick up the thread tomorrow.

Incident 1 — the Kafka hotspot, fixed live

The junior move, the senior move.

Junior: rebalance Kafka partitions. Takes 40 minutes in this cluster size, and the rebalance itself adds latency to the consumer group during the transition. Meanwhile the over-delivery continues.

Senior: flip the bidder's fallback policy now. Your pacing design already has a degradation path — when consumer lag exceeds 30s, the bidder derates its bids to 80%. But the flag requires a manual override to engage automatically during an incident. You engage it. Over-delivery rate drops from $18K/hr to $3K/hr within two minutes. Then you work on the Kafka rebalance with the pressure off.

The fallback mechanism you built in Act 3 is the reason you are not hemorrhaging money at this moment. Everything senior engineers do under pressure was designed while no one was panicking.

Incident 2 — the identity coverage drop

Why contract tests earn their keep tonight.

Your contract test failed the pipeline at 23:58 — before the bad graph reached production silver. Seller-dashboard queries are timing out because they are reading the old silver (correct) and joining to a new gold (empty — because gold refresh was blocked). You have two options:

  • Roll back the identity graph push. Requires paging the identity team's on-call, who may take 20 minutes.
  • Fail-open the graph join for tonight — use the 7-day-average coverage as the floor, let the pipeline run, accept the 9% under-stitched cost as a quarantine category visible in the dashboard.

You fail-open. The 9% of conversions that can't be stitched land in the UNSTITCHED bucket that Act 3 added. Seller dashboards refresh with a caveat banner. You still page the identity team — but you did not wake them up to save the pipeline. You woke them up to debug their push on Friday morning.

The reason this works: your pipeline is designed to fail gracefully. The LEFT JOIN + UNSTITCHED bucket pattern from Act 3 is the reason you didn't need a hotfix at midnight.

Incident 3 — the dedupe anomaly, investigated cold

What the sidecar is telling you.

Friday morning, 07:30. Pager silent. You pour coffee and pull the sidecar.

SELECT sdk_version, COUNT(*) AS dropped,
       ROUND(COUNT(*) * 1.0 /
         (SELECT COUNT(*) FROM bronze.impression
          WHERE dt = CURRENT_DATE - 1 AND sdk_version = s.sdk_version), 4) AS drop_rate
FROM ops.suspected_duplicates s
WHERE dt = CURRENT_DATE - 1
GROUP BY sdk_version
ORDER BY drop_rate DESC;

Top result: sdk_ios=4.21.0, drop rate 3.8% (baseline 0.1%). Confirmed with the SDK team: 4.21.0 shipped a retry-on-transient-error bug 36 hours ago. The dedupe logic caught it. Revenue counts are correct. Suspected-duplicates sidecar saved $2-3M of potential over-reporting.

You file a P2 with the SDK team to ship 4.21.1 by Monday. You also add a metric-on-metric: if any SDK version's drop rate exceeds 1% for two consecutive days, page the SDK team automatically. That adjustment ships Tuesday.

This is the loop Act 3 was designed to create. The pipeline produces its own exception stream that the engineer can monitor; the stream caught the SDK bug before anyone outside the team noticed.

Act 6 — Recovery

Black Friday closes. Revenue for the day: $47M against a target of $44M. You were on-call for the whole thing. You did not need to touch the pipeline once between 01:00 and 06:00 because the graceful-degradation paths in Act 3 did their job.

What you reconcile over the weekend

The ~$2.4M of conversions that landed in UNSTITCHED during the identity drop

The identity team pushes a corrected graph Monday morning. You re-run Friday's stitch against the corrected graph for the impacted window, producing a supplemental table: silver.fact_conversion_identity_reprocessed. Seller dashboards pull from a UNION of the original plus the reprocessed — users see corrected numbers with a "revised" badge.

Crucially: the original table is unmodified. History stays history. The supplemental is an additive, visible, auditable correction — exactly the pattern Act 3 wrote into the design doc.

The campaign over-delivery from the Kafka lag

Finance will refund the affected advertisers. Your reconciliation job computes the per-advertiser over-delivery from the per-minute pacing log and the actual impressions delivered. It writes to ops.pacing_incident_refunds keyed by advertiser_id and incident_id. Account management sends automated emails. Revenue adjusts on the following invoice.

Total refund: $31K across seven advertisers. You offered the refund proactively, which preserved every one of those accounts.

The Airtable-to-Kafka bridge that was a tactical hack

Three weeks of Black Friday, the bridge held. But it is a two-file script running on a single VM with no retries. Your Q1 roadmap: replace it with a real Kafka Connect source against the affiliate vendor's S2S API. Retry, exponential backoff, dead-letter topic. The hack bought you Black Friday; the replacement buys you the next five years.

The four artefacts you publish

  1. The incident post-mortem. Blameless, structured: timeline, what broke, what held, what we fix. Published to the engineering wiki by Tuesday.
  2. The data contracts. Identity graph contract. Affiliate postback contract. SDK event contract. Each is one page. Published to the data catalog.
  3. The runbook. One runbook per alert. What the alert means, what to check first, what to run, when to escalate. Pager links to the runbook.
  4. The dashboard definitions. Every dashboard now carries a data-definition footer: which table, which attribution window, which "revenue" definition (billed, spend, attributed, settled). Every future "the numbers don't match" conversation opens with pointing at the footer.

Act 7 — The Lesson

Six months later, a new PM joins the team. She asks you, over coffee, how you knew what to build on day one.

You tell her the truth.

You didn't. You walked into a burning room on day four and bought yourself seventy minutes by writing a paragraph. The paragraph was correct because you had been in this kind of room twice before at other companies and you recognised the shape of the fire. The architecture came later, built around the specific fires the job was having. Defense came later still, rehearsed against the specific people who would push back. And the incident response was designed the weekend before Black Friday, on paper, with sticky notes, for exactly the scenario that ended up happening. Nothing was improvised under pressure — everything was rehearsed under quiet.

The interview is not testing whether you can build a pipeline.

The interview is testing whether you know what to do when the pipeline lies.

And what you do is: you ask what "revenue" means. You name the cost of every choice you are not making. You put the failure modes in the design doc before the failure happens. You design the graceful degradation in January so you don't have to invent it in November. You keep the sidecar table. You write the runbook. You never, ever, mutate yesterday's numbers.

And when someone asks you, years later, why senior data engineering matters — you tell them: because the numbers that run the company are produced by the pipelines, and the pipelines lie, and someone has to be awake when they do.

That was the job. That is always the job.
