The Room · Scar File 4

The pushback grid is the scorecard

Every senior-DE loop at a platform company has a version of this. The interviewer prepares six-to-eight challenges per architecture, each one a real failure pattern they've watched candidates lose on. The grid is not improvised.

  1. Tactic challenges — "why Kafka not Kinesis," "why dbt not X." Low stakes. Wrong answer: defending the resume bullet. Right answer: concede and re-scope.
  2. Cost challenges — "storage is 3x," "joins are expensive." Medium stakes. Wrong answer: folding on the design. Right answer: measure the cost, keep the design, offer a mitigation.
  3. Principle challenges — "why not one wide table," "why not just UPDATE." High stakes. These are the ones where folding loses the round. You must hold the line — ideally in a sentence the interviewer wants to write down.
  4. Stakeholder traps — "the CFO asks for X by Friday." The trap is framing an organisational question as a technical one. The answer names the cost in her terms and offers a smaller honest win.

The grid tests whether you can tell a tactic from a principle in under forty seconds.

The Landmine · 4 of many

"I'll make it configurable."

Under pressure, candidates discover configurability as an escape hatch. Raj pushes on the wide-table pattern — candidate proposes "let's make it a config flag: enable_legacy_revenue_default = true." Raj pushes on append-only — candidate proposes "we can toggle bi-temporal per table." Raj pushes on the semantic layer — candidate proposes "teams can opt out if they need to."

Every config flag in this layer is a decision deferred to a future maintainer who has less context than you do. The flag calcifies, becomes a compatibility constraint, and the v3 architect inherits a system where every design choice is negotiable. You shipped the bug as a feature.

The senior move is to refuse the flag and pay the cost of the decision now. If the decision is wrong, you'll rewrite. If you make it a flag, nobody will.

Rule: a principle is a thing you are willing to rewrite later if wrong. A flag is a thing you will never remove.
