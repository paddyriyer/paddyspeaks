The Equidistance Trap

A 14th-century logic puzzle explains why analysis paralysis never resolves with more data — in careers, relationships, health, admissions, or architecture decks. Perfect information doesn't break the tie. Something else has to.

PaddySpeaks — Notes on Decisions, Systems, and Getting Unstuck
The donkey doesn't starve for lack of hay. It starves for lack of a tiebreaker.

Jean Buridan never actually wrote about a donkey. The 14th-century French philosopher was working through a much older problem — one Aristotle raises in De Caelo — about whether a rational will, faced with two options of exactly equal merit, can move at all. Later critics of Buridan's determinism sharpened it into the image that stuck: a donkey, equally hungry, placed at the exact midpoint between two identical bales of hay. No reason to go left. No reason to go right. The animal, having no basis for preference, starves precisely at the center of two good outcomes.

It's a cruel little thought experiment, and also the most accurate diagnosis available for what actually happens inside a stalled decision. Not "too many options." Not "not enough data." A tie that no amount of additional analysis can break — because the analysis is what produced the tie in the first place.

01The Donkey Never Left

Give the paradox modern clothes and it stops looking medieval and starts looking familiar. It shows up in the same four places, over and over:

Career

Two offers, comparable comp, comparable scope. A spreadsheet gets built to force a winner. It returns a number within 2% either way — not a signal, just confirmation the tie was real. Weeks pass. One offer expires while the other is still "under consideration."

Relationships

Waiting for a partner who clears every criterion, while someone genuinely compatible is treated as a placeholder. The list of criteria keeps growing precisely because no one on it has ever lost. Perfection isn't a standard here — it's a way to avoid choosing.

Health

Keto or Mediterranean, intermittent fasting or high protein, plant-based or none of the above. Months go into researching the optimal plan. Meanwhile there's no exercise and no change to how anyone actually eats, because the research substitutes for the decision it was meant to inform.

Admissions

Which school, which field, engineering or the arts, which one maximizes future earning versus fit. A year of deliberation later, the deposit deadline has already passed on both — the choice got made by default, by the calendar, not by either applicant.

Notice the pattern: in every case the paralysis shows up after the analysis starts, not before it. It's tempting to blame indecision on insufficient information. But the donkey had perfect information — it could see both haystacks clearly, measure the distance to each precisely, verify the hay was identical down to the stalk. Total information didn't produce a decision. It produced the tie.

02Why More Data Doesn't Help

Herbert Simon, the economist who coined satisficing, spent a career arguing that human decision-making was never built for optimization. We don't have the processing capacity to evaluate every option against every criterion to true completion. We evaluate until an option clears a "good enough" bar, and take it. Optimizing is what a calculator does between two well-defined numbers. Satisficing is what a person does when they still have to go home for dinner.

The trouble is some environments actively punish satisficing and reward the appearance of optimization. Enterprise architecture decisions are a clean example. Nobody gets criticized for producing a 40-slide vendor comparison. People do get asked, later, why they picked without one. So the deck grows — cost, latency, ecosystem maturity, hiring pool, lock-in risk. And here's the part that rarely gets said out loud in the room: adding comparison axes to a genuinely close decision doesn't resolve it. It adds more ties. A platform that's 8% cheaper but has a smaller talent pool and better time-to-first-query isn't a winner — it's the same hay, now measured on three scales instead of one, still balanced at the center.

Barry Schwartz's research on the paradox of choice found the same shape at the level of individual psychology: past a certain point, more options don't produce better decisions, they produce more regret and a measurable increase in non-decision, where the chooser simply defers rather than commits. The number of options was never the actual constraint. The absence of a tiebreaking mechanism was.

03What Frozen Decisions Actually Cost

The spreadsheet feels like progress. It isn't neutral — every week it stays open has a price, and the price rarely gets itemized:

Opportunity Passed

What was on the table stopped being on the table while it was still being weighed.

Decision Fatigue

Every hour spent re-weighing the same two options draws down the same mental budget the decision itself needs.

Confidence Shrinks

Doubt compounds the longer a choice stays open — each day undecided reads as new evidence that it must be hard.

Life Stands Still

Staying busy re-analyzing isn't the same as moving. The clock keeps running on everything the decision was blocking.

04A Problem Older Than Any One Answer

The frozen-decision-maker isn't a modern character. It's old enough that most major wisdom traditions independently produced a version of the same fix — not "compute harder," but "stop requiring a guarantee before you're willing to act." None of these owns the insight; they converge on it from different directions, which is itself the more interesting fact.

Stoicism

Epictetus's dichotomy of control: sort what's yours to determine (the effort, the choice) from what never was (the outcome), and stop spending will on the second category.

Buddhism

Attachment to a specific result — not the choosing itself — is treated as the source of suffering. Acting skillfully and releasing the craving for a particular fruit are two separate disciplines.

Taoism

Wu wei, effortless action: forcing a decision through exhaustive calculation is itself treated as a kind of resistance to the moment, not a form of diligence.

The Gita

"You have a right to the action, never to the fruits" (2.47) — one of the more direct statements of the same idea, addressed to a warrior who has, for very different reasons, also frozen mid-decision.

Decision Science

Simon's satisficing and the reversible/irreversible framing popularized in modern management: the secular version of the same move, dressed in spreadsheets instead of scripture.

Strip the theology from all five and the mechanism underneath is identical: the paralysis was never really about which option was better. It was about needing proof of "better" before being willing to move — and that proof, in a genuine tie, was never going to arrive.

The Reframe

The donkey's real error isn't indecision. It's the belief that a decision needs to be justified by a provably superior outcome before it can be made. Remove that requirement, and equidistance stops being a trap — it becomes irrelevant. If both haystacks are genuinely equal, the choice was never going to be won on the merits. It has to be won on something else: a deadline, a bias toward motion, a willingness to own the walking instead of the destination.

05Escape the Trap

None of this is an argument against analysis. Most decisions aren't ties, and the analysis is what reveals that. This is specifically for the residue — the close calls that survive the spreadsheet and still won't resolve.

01

Set a Deadline

No decision lives forever, and an open-ended one behaves exactly like the donkey's midpoint: perfectly stable, going nowhere. A fixed date converts an infinite search into a bounded one.

02

Choose the Better Option, Not the Perfect One

Satisfice on purpose. The moment a third comparison axis is needed to separate two options, that's confirmation of a genuine tie, not evidence one more benchmark will resolve it.

03

Improve After Choosing

Great decisions are usually made great through commitment and iteration afterward, not through certainty beforehand. Treat the choice as a starting draft, not a final verdict.

04

Remember: Not Deciding Is Also Deciding

Deferral isn't neutral — it's a choice for the status quo, made by default instead of on purpose, usually at a worse moment than the one that was avoided.

The part worth sitting with

Every path closes eventually — the second offer, the other haystack, the road not taken all stop being available whether or not a choice was ever made about them. A good decision today outperforms a perfect decision that arrives after the option is already gone. The donkey didn't need a better haystack. It needed permission to walk toward one without a guarantee attached.

Every path closes eventually. A good decision today is often better than a perfect decision tomorrow.
