Smarter Insulin, Healthier You: Analytics and Exploratory Data Analysis

Evaluate patient progress: Track blood sugar control, identify trends, and assess response to therapy adjustments.

By PaddyFebruary 4, 20243 min read

3.1 Analytics Goals and Use Cases:

  • Evaluate patient progress: Track blood sugar control, identify trends, and assess response to therapy adjustments.

  • Optimize insulin delivery: Analyze glucose patterns and device settings to personalize insulin delivery for better control.

  • Predict potential complications: Identify early warning signs of hypoglycemia or hyperglycemia for proactive intervention.

  • Improve user experience: Analyze app usage patterns and feedback to enhance usability and adherence.

  • Generate clinical insights: Support research and development of improved treatment strategies for diabetes management.

3.2 Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA):

  • Descriptive statistics: Summarizing key metrics like average glucose levels, insulin dosage, and app usage frequency.

  • Data visualization: Creating charts and graphs to visualize trends, relationships, and outliers in the data.

  • Correlation analysis: Identifying potential relationships between glucose levels, insulin delivery, patient activity, and other factors.

3.3 Advanced Analytics and Machine Learning:

  • Time series analysis: Forecasting future glucose levels based on historical trends and device settings.

  • Clustering: Grouping patients with similar characteristics to personalize treatment approaches.

  • Predictive modeling: Identifying patients at high risk of complications for targeted interventions.

EDA for HbA1c Improvement/Deterioration in Wearable Insulin Switchers

Here‚Äôs how you can conduct an Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) to understand HbA1c changes in patients who switched to wearable insulin, focusing on factors that might influence improvement or deterioration:

1. Data Preparation:

  • Ensure relevant data tables are clean and well-organized. This includes tables for:

  • Patients: Demographics, diagnoses, etc.

  • Treatments: Details of wearable insulin and other treatments.

  • Lab Results: HbA1c measurements before, during, and after the switch.

  • Additional Factors: Potential influencing factors like age, comorbidities, treatment adherence, etc. (if available).

2. Initial Exploration:

  • Descriptive Statistics: Calculate basic statistics (mean, median, standard deviation) for HbA1c values before and after the switch.

  • Visualization: Create histograms or boxplots to visualize the distribution of HbA1c changes across the entire population.

3. Segmentation Analysis:

  • Segment patients based on:

  • HbA1c Change: Group by improvement, no change, or deterioration.

  • Potential Influencing Factors: Segment by demographics, clinical factors, treatment details, etc.

  • Compare HbA1c Changes: Compare mean/median HbA1c changes across segments using boxplots or violin plots.

4. Visualization and Insights:

  • Line Charts: Track individual patient trends in HbA1c over time, highlighting improvement/deterioration patterns.

  • Scatterplots: Explore relationships between potential influencing factors and HbA1c changes (e.g., age vs. HbA1c improvement).

  • Heatmaps: Identify potential correlations between multiple influencing factors and HbA1c changes.

5. Deeper Analysis:

  • Survival Analysis: Use Kaplan-Meier curves to compare the time to HbA1c improvement/deterioration across segments.

  • Multivariate Analysis: Use regression techniques (e.g., logistic regression) to identify significant predictors of HbA1c improvement/deterioration, considering multiple influencing factors simultaneously.

6. Considerations:

  • Data Quality: Ensure accurate and complete data in all relevant tables.

  • Clinical Interpretation: Consult healthcare professionals to interpret findings in the context of clinical guidelines and individual patient factors.

  • Ethical Considerations: Maintain data privacy and avoid drawing conclusions that violate ethical principles.
