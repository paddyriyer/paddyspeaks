Your AI Is Brilliant. Then the Bill Arrives.

A plain-English and technical guide to what really drives enterprise Gen AI costs — and how to control them.

PaddySpeaks 12 min read Plain English + Under the Hood
TOKEN METER — WEEK 9 OF THE PILOT: STILL "CHEAP," ALREADY CLIMBING

Six weeks ago, the pilot was a triumph. Legal, HR, and the sales team got access to Nova, an internal AI assistant that could summarize contracts, draft outreach emails, and answer "what does our expense policy say about hotel upgrades" without anyone digging through the employee handbook. The demo cost pennies. The rollout got applause at the town hall.

Nine weeks later, the CFO is holding an invoice that reads like a phone bill from a decade nobody misses, and asking — with admirable calm — "How did a helpful chatbot quietly become one of our fastest-growing technology expenses?"

The chatbot did not ask for a salary, health insurance, or a corner office. But it developed an impressive appetite for tokens — and nobody ever put it on a budget.

Here is the mental model worth keeping for the rest of this article. Every time someone asks an AI system a question, it's a bit like sending a consultant into a meeting room. Before that consultant says a single word back to you, they're handed the company handbook, a full transcript of everything said in the conversation so far, a stack of documents someone thinks might be relevant, and a long memo on how to behave. The consultant reads all of it — every single time, even if they read the same handbook nine minutes ago — and then hands you back a paragraph.

You are not billed for the paragraph. You are billed for everything the consultant had to read to produce it.

That's the whole story of enterprise Gen AI economics. Everything below is just detail.

One clarification before we go further: none of this runs on a strict "one word equals one token" rule. Tokenization differs by model and by language — a technical term, a rare name, or non-English text can consume more tokens than a plain English sentence of the same length. Treat every number in this article as illustrative, not as a law of physics.

01 The AI Bill Is More Than the User's Question

Eleven words go in. Thousands of tokens go to work.

An employee types one sentence into Nova: "Can I expense a hotel upgrade for a client trip?" Eleven words. But by the time that sentence reaches the model, it's traveling with company. The application typically also sends the system instructions that tell Nova how to behave, the running history of the conversation so far, snippets pulled from the expense policy through retrieval, sometimes the entire uploaded policy document instead of the one relevant page, results from any tools or APIs it called along the way — and only then, at the very end, the eleven words the person actually typed.

FIG. 1 — What actually travels behind one eleven-word question

If the workflow is agentic, that whole reading-and-generating cycle can repeat several times before the user ever sees an answer. Retries after a failed call, safety re-checks, and multi-step reasoning passes on certain models add computation nobody asked for and nobody sees. One sentence in. Thousands of tokens processed. That gap is where the invoice is born.

Under the Hood

What a request payload can include, end to end:

  • System instructions (persona, guardrails, formatting rules)
  • The user prompt itself
  • Conversation history, often resent in full on every turn
  • Retrieved RAG content
  • Uploaded documents, in full or in part
  • Tool and API call results returned to the model
  • Images, audio, or other multimodal inputs, which are tokenized differently by provider
  • The model's generated output
  • Retries, failed calls, and agent-loop iterations
  • Reasoning or internal-compute tokens, on models that meter and bill for them

Several of these are invisible in the chat UI. They are not invisible on the invoice.

02 Tokens: The Meter Behind the Machine

Not quite a word. Not quite a character. A metered unit that adds up.

A token is a chunk of text the model works with — sometimes a whole word, sometimes a fragment of one, sometimes punctuation. It is not a fixed exchange rate against English words, and it is even less predictable across other languages or specialized vocabulary. What matters for a CFO is not the definition — it's that every token processed, in and out, is metered and billed.

Let's build one clean, original example. Picture a mid-size logistics company — call it Riverstone — with 400 employees using Nova for policy questions and quick email drafts.

Notice what did the multiplying: not one dramatic mistake, but 2,400 ordinary requests a day, each one quietly carrying 3,200 tokens of company, history, and paperwork before it ever reaches the eleven words that mattered. That's the meter behind the machine — and it runs whether or not anyone is watching it.

03 Context Window: Capacity Is Not a Consumption Target

A bigger desk doesn't ask you to fill it.

Think of the context window as the model's working desk. A large desk can hold more material spread out at once — more documents, more history, more instructions. But placing 150 files on that desk for every single question doesn't make the person sitting there smarter. It can make them slower, more expensive to employ for the hour, and more likely to grab the wrong page when it's time to answer.

FIG. 2 — Context capacity vs. useful context

Four things are easy to conflate and worth separating: the maximum context capacity a model supports, the actual tokens processed in a given call, the useful context that genuinely informs the answer, and the irrelevant context that's along for the ride and being paid for anyway.

A bigger context window is a larger suitcase — not permission to pack the entire house.

04 Why AI Costs Suddenly Explode

It's rarely one bad decision. It's fourteen small ones, compounding.

Costs don't usually explode because someone did one dramatic, foolish thing. They explode because a dozen small, individually-reasonable choices stack on top of each other: fast user adoption, long conversations resent in full on every turn, system prompts that grew a paragraph at a time and were never trimmed, RAG that returns too many chunks "just in case," entire documents attached instead of the relevant excerpt, a flagship model used for questions a smaller one could answer, replies that ramble past what the question needed, agentic workflows quietly making several model calls per visible request, tool calls repeated because nothing was cached, retries triggered by transient errors, development and evaluation traffic billed at production rates, duplicate queries nobody deduplicated, and — underneath all of it — no one actually watching the meter.

The user sees one question and one answer. The invoice sees six model calls, five of them invisible from the chat window.

05 Same Question, Two Architectures

Two systems can give the same answer and cost wildly different amounts to produce it.

An employee asks: "What's our policy on international data transfer for the new EU client?" Watch what two different architectures do with that one sentence.

🐘 Architecture A — Brute Force
  • Resends the entire 40-page data-governance manual, every turn
  • Resends the full chat history verbatim
  • Retrieves 12 passages with no reranking — several irrelevant
  • Routes straight to the flagship model, regardless of the question's difficulty
  • No caching of the manual or the system prompt
  • Generates a long, thorough, over-explained answer
🦉 Architecture B — Engineered
  • Classifies the request as a routine policy lookup
  • Routes to a smaller model sized for that task
  • Retrieves and reranks — passes only the top 3 relevant passages
  • Caches the manual's static content, which rarely changes
  • Summarizes older turns into a running digest, not full history
  • Caps output length; escalates to a premium model only if flagged high-stakes

Both can hand the employee a correct, similar-sounding answer. One does it by paying, every time, for a stack of material the model barely needed. The other does it by deciding — before spending anything — how much reading this particular question actually deserves. Same output on screen. Very different cost, latency, and scalability underneath.

06 The Two-Layer Explanation

A leader and an engineer need different dashboards, aimed at the same problem.

For the Business Leader
  • Cost per successful task, not just cost per token
  • Cost per active user, tracked over time
  • Cost broken out by team and use case
  • Business value produced against that cost
  • Error and retry rates — rework is a hidden cost
  • Share of requests that require a premium model
  • Unit economics as adoption scales, not just total spend
For the Architect & Engineer
  • Prompt and context profiling — what's actually in each call
  • Model routing by task complexity
  • Semantic caching for repeat or near-duplicate queries
  • Conversation summarization instead of full replay
  • RAG relevance, reranking, chunk size, and top-k tuning
  • Output length limits
  • Batch processing where the workload tolerates delay
  • Rate limits, budgets, and observability by user, feature, model, and workflow
  • Loop limits for agentic workflows
  • Ongoing evaluation of quality vs. cost vs. latency

07 Ten Practical Ways to Reduce the Bill

Not every fix costs the same effort. Start where the leverage is highest.

TierMove
Quick winTrim the system prompt to what's actually load-bearing — remove a clause, then test whether anything broke
Quick winSet sensible output-length caps per task instead of one global default

GovernanceAssign a named owner to every AI feature's unit economics — the way cloud spend already has one

None of this means minimizing tokens for its own sake. A shorter prompt that quietly drops the instruction keeping the assistant accurate or safe isn't a saving — it's a liability with a lower invoice attached.

08 Cheap AI Can Become Expensive AI

The sticker price and the total cost are not the same number.

Choosing the cheapest available model is not automatically the cheapest system. A weaker model that gets things wrong more often can quietly generate more retries, more corrections, more human review, and longer prompts written to compensate for its limits — all of which cost more than the per-token discount saved. Run the same logic in reverse and it still holds: routing every simple request to the most capable model on the market wastes money on tasks that never needed that much horsepower.

Use the smallest model that can reliably complete the task — not merely the smallest model available.

09 Every Useful Token Matters

"Every token matters" is a slogan. It needs one more word.

"Every token matters" makes a tidy poster, but taken literally it points teams toward token starvation — stripping context until answers get worse, not cheaper to fix. The more useful version is one word longer: every useful token matters. The goal was never to spend as few tokens as possible. It's to stop paying for the ones that add neither accuracy, nor safety, nor context, nor value — and to keep, without apology, every one that does.

TOKEN METER — SAME ADOPTION, SAME VALUE, NEEDLE BACK IN THE GREEN

Gen AI does not become expensive merely because people use it. It becomes expensive when the architecture allows every request to carry yesterday's conversations, tomorrow's possibilities, and the entire company library on its back.
