Day Four

The arc of a senior ads data engineer — one story, seven acts, at a subscription streaming platform on a Tuesday morning.

By Paddy Draft · building in public
Prologue · Before We Begin

Day Four.

You start the new job on a Thursday. By Tuesday morning the CFO is in your Slack asking why the ad revenue number on the dashboard does not match the one the exec deck has been reporting for three months. You have not merged your first pull request. Your laptop is still being provisioned for one of the warehouses. Somebody has to be the adult in this room. That somebody is you.

This article is one story. Not a syllabus, not a checklist, not a tour of concepts. One company, one problem, seven acts. The interview you're preparing for is not a knowledge test — it is a test of whether you would be useful in this room on Tuesday morning at 9:47 am. Read it that way.

Everything in this story happens at StreamCo — a large subscription streaming platform, 260M+ paying members across 190+ countries, ~$17B annual revenue. They launched an ad-supported tier 18 months ago. You are joining the ads data-engineering team at Senior / L5.

Where the story pauses for a concept, you'll see a sidebar labelled "The Room" (verbatim interview transcripts), "The Landmine" (production failure modes you would actually see here), or "Your Turn" (a SQL or Python problem you should try yourself before continuing). You can skip the sidebars — the story reads without them — but the sidebars are where the interview prep actually lives.

Act 1 · The Incident

09:47 AM · Tuesday

#incidents-ads-de · 11 messages in the last 3 minutes
CFO Amelia
@channel the Q3 exec deck shows ad-supported revenue at $9.1M for September. Our finance dashboard pulls from ads_warehouse and shows $8.2M. Which one is correct? I need a single answer by the 10:30 board prep.
CMO Darren
Who shipped last night? Three advertiser campaigns just auto-paused because their ROAS dashboard tanked. The advertiser relationship team is getting tweets.
Partnerships · Priya
Two content partners are asking why their licensing royalty numbers on the partner portal jumped 14% overnight. They want a call this afternoon.
Director (DM to you)
I need something I can say in the 10:30 board prep. Any number with its definition. Twenty minutes to get your head around this; I'll stall until 10:40.
You
Day four. Three confirmed incidents. Two of them may be the same incident. The director just bought you 53 minutes, but only if you actually deliver.

You take your hands off the keyboard. You stare at the ceiling for eight seconds. You have three choices, right now. Each of them costs something. Pick one wrong and the cost compounds for the quarter.

A"Both numbers are right, they measure different things."

This is very likely the correct answer. It is also the answer you earn on day 180, not day 4. Without receipts, the CFO hears it as hedging. Her last data-team lead got fired partly for hedging.

Cost if you say it now: low if you're right, catastrophic if you're slightly wrong. You have no receipts. Do not pick this.

B"Give me until 10:40. I'll have the reconciliation and a recommendation."

Buys fifty-three minutes. Commits you to delivering a specific output by a specific time. Ships nothing if you do not investigate right now — not after standup, not after coffee.

Cost if you pick this: you spend the morning in the query tool. Everything else on your plate slips a day. Your first design-review prep slides will ship Thursday instead of Wednesday. Acceptable.

C"Dashboard is the source of truth. $8.2M."

Fast, decisive, confidently wrong. The dashboard is not the source of truth; it is a view over the warehouse. If the CFO ships $8.2M to the board, and Finance arrives independently at $9.1M in the monthly close four weeks from now, the data team's credibility is gone for two quarters.

Cost: career-making in the short term, career-ending in the medium.

You pick B. You reply to the director at 09:54:

You: "Give me until 10:40. Both numbers may be right for different definitions of 'ad revenue' at StreamCo — we have four in the system. I'll reconcile and come with a one-line recommendation you can carry into the board prep."

The thumbs-up arrives in twelve seconds. The clock reads 09:55. You have forty-five minutes to do the work and five minutes to write a paragraph a non-engineer can carry into an executive meeting. You open the query tool.

The Room · Scar File 1

What the interviewer is scoring right now

Every senior ads DE loop has some version of the question Amelia just asked. The interviewer watches for four moves in the first ninety seconds.

  1. Did you ask which definition of revenue she means? Billed, spend, attributed, settled — four numbers, four audiences. The candidate who answers "revenue" without disambiguating has already demonstrated the failure mode the incident is exposing.
  2. Did you separate "which is correct" from "which is most useful right now"? Both can be correct; neither may be fit for the 10:30 board prep. Senior engineers hold both ideas in the air simultaneously.
  3. Did you quantify your time commitment? "I need a while" is an amateur answer. "45 minutes, with a specific deliverable" is a senior one. The director could only stall for you because you were specific.
  4. Did you end with a recommendation, not a number? The output of the next 45 minutes is not "the revenue." It is "here is the revenue under definition X, here is what the $900K gap is composed of, and here is which number you should carry into the board prep given the audience."

Candidates who answer Amelia with a number have shown the interviewer they don't understand which room they are in. The answer is always a definition before a number.

The Landmine · 1 of many

"Revenue" is not a column in a table

At StreamCo, the word "revenue" refers to at least four different numbers, each stored in a different system, each produced by a different pipeline, each measuring a different population at a different time. When a senior engineer hears "the revenue number is wrong," they hear it as "which of the four revenues, for which audience, as of which day?" The junior engineer hears it as "I need to go query something."

This is the landmine that produced today's incident. Someone, months ago, built a dashboard labelled Revenue — September that reads sum(spend_cents)/100 from silver.fact_impression. That is spend revenue. Finance's $9.1M is settled revenue — post-refund, post-IVT-credit, post-partner-attribution-lag, sourced from the ledger in Salesforce. The pipeline was correct. The label was lying.

The fix for this incident is not in SQL. It is in the label.

You check the query tool is authenticated. You open the BI dashboard URL Amelia linked, then open its data source, then open the saved query. You screenshot the query. You timestamp the screenshot. You copy it to a personal channel. From this moment forward, the number and its definition are versioned in your notes — whatever the dashboard shows tomorrow, the 09:56 version is preserved.

This is not paranoia. Within three hours, someone will ask you "the dashboard said $8.2M at 9:47, did it really?" You will open your screenshot. You will answer. You will keep the job.

The clock reads 09:57. You take the first sip of coffee. You have been in role for ninety-six hours. Let's reconcile.
