Prodigy or Reincarnation?

Darwin or Dharma. A child of three does not learn the piano — sometimes it looks like he remembers it. What is the secret behind talent that arrives fully formed: a genetic jackpot, or the Bhagavad Gita's verse 6.43?

By PaddyMay 30, 202624 min read

Sialkot, 1989. A sixteen-year-old boy takes a Waqar Younis bouncer flush on the nose. Blood spills onto the white shirt, onto the pitch. The physio runs out with the stretcher. The boy waves him away, dabs the blood, and says four words that India would replay for the next quarter-century: "Main khelega." I will play. The next ball, he hammers for four. The boy was Sachin Tendulkar, and the question that has followed every gifted child since is the same one that follows him: where does that come from?

Look at the parents. Sachin's father was a gentle Marathi poet and professor. Look at the siblings — pleasant, capable, unremarkable in the precise sense that none of them could bat. Look at the village courtyards where fathers built makeshift pitches for sons who, somehow, already knew what to do with a bat the way a salmon knows the river. Then fast-forward to April 2025, when a fourteen-year-old named Vaibhav Suryavanshi faced his first ball in the IPL and deposited it into the stands for six — and later carved a hundred off thirty-five deliveries, the youngest to do so in the history of the league. Again the cameras panned to the family box. Again: ordinary, beaming, baffled people.

This essay is about that bafflement. It is about the gap between the family tree and the fruit it somehow produced. The infographic above frames the whole debate in two words — Darwin or Dharma? — and that is exactly the fork we will walk down. One path says genetic jackpot. The other points to the Bhagavad Gita, chapter six, verse forty-three, and says something far stranger: that the child is not learning at all. He is remembering.

Before we theorise, let us simply look at the evidence — the specimens that any theory of talent has to explain. Notice the ages.

And then the ones who needed no childhood stage at all — Srinivasa Ramanujan, a self-taught clerk from Kumbakonam who arrived at theorems he said the goddess Namagiri whispered into his dreams, results so deep that Cambridge mathematicians a century later are still proving he was right. The pattern is consistent and it is maddening: a capacity that is too large, too early, and too sudden for the slow arithmetic of upbringing to explain.

The puzzle, stated plainly

If talent were simply inherited, prodigies would have prodigious parents and prodigious children. They rarely have either. If talent were simply practised, no three-year-old would arrive already fluent — there has been no time to practise. So what is the thing that is neither clearly inherited nor clearly learned?

Part One · The Scientific Reading

Darwin — The Body's Inheritance

Genes, recombination, the brain, and why the family tree lies.

Science does not reach for the miraculous, and it should not. Before invoking past lives, we owe the materialist account its full and serious hearing — because it explains more than the sceptics of spirituality admit, and less than the worshippers of the gene believe.

Why the parents look ordinary: regression and the recombination lottery

The first instinct — "if the child is a genius, the parents must be too" — is simply wrong, and statistics tells us exactly why. Most exceptional traits sit at the far tail of a bell curve. The children of tall parents tend to be tall, but on average a little closer to ordinary than their parents; this is regression to the mean, and it runs both directions. The corollary is liberating and strange: the truly extreme outlier usually comes from parents who were merely above average, not extreme themselves. Genius is not a torch passed hand to hand. It is a lightning strike.

Figure 1 · Regression to the Mean

The child does not stand beside the parents on the curve. He stands far past them — and his own children will, on average, drift back toward the crowd. Genius does not breed true.

But regression only explains distance from the parents. It does not explain how the lightning struck at all. For that, we turn to the lottery inside every conception. A human being is not a blend of two parents like mixed paint; he is a fresh shuffle of some twenty thousand genes, recombined into a hand of cards that has never been dealt before and will never be dealt again. Most hands are ordinary. But the deck is enormous, and once in a great while the cards fall into a configuration of staggering, unrepeatable advantage.

Emergenesis: the lock that needs every tumbler

Here the geneticist David Lykken gave us the single most useful idea in this entire debate, and almost nobody outside psychology has heard of it. He called it emergenesis. Some traits, he argued, are not the simple sum of many genes — they are the product of a precise configuration of them. They require not a few good genes but a specific combination of dozens, all present at once, like a combination lock where every tumbler must align or the door stays shut.

Figure 2 · Emergenesis — The Combination Lock

Emergenesis explains the family-tree paradox in one stroke: a configural trait will not reliably appear in parents, siblings, or children, because the exact combination almost never survives the shuffle of reproduction. The genius is real, genetic — and effectively non-heritable.

This is the scientific bombshell hiding in plain sight. Emergenesis predicts exactly what we observe: that genius can erupt from a placid family and then vanish again, leaving no dynasty behind. The genes were real. The inheritance was real. But the configuration was a one-time event — a hand of cards reshuffled the instant it was played. Darwin, properly understood, already explains why the parents look ordinary.

The prodigy's brain: faster wiring, deeper memory

Genes build hardware, and the hardware of prodigies is measurably different. When the psychologist Joanne Ruthsatz studied child prodigies in music, art, and mathematics, she found one trait elevated almost universally and off the charts: working memory — the mind's ability to hold and manipulate many pieces of information at once. Pair that with relentless attention to detail and an early, almost magnetic pull toward one domain, and you have a brain optimised to absorb a field at a rate that looks, from the outside, like magic.

Figure 3 · The Prodigy Brain

Bigger working memory, faster (myelinated) pathways, and a domain region that specialises absurdly early. The brain is not doing more work — it is doing less, because it perceives in whole patterns. This is what chess masters call "chunking."

There is a darker, more honest footnote here. The neuropsychiatrist Darold Treffert spent his life studying savant syndrome — individuals, often with autism, who play a concerto after one hearing or name the day of the week for any date in history. Treffert concluded that some knowledge appears to be, in his unsettling phrase, "factory-installed." He called it genetic memory: ability that was never learned in this lifetime, somehow already present in the wiring. A materialist coined that phrase. Hold onto it. We will meet its spiritual twin in a few minutes, and the resemblance is uncanny.

The honest limit of the gene

So how far does Darwin take us? Remarkably far. Regression explains the ordinary parents. Recombination and emergenesis explain the sudden, non-heritable eruption. Working memory and myelination explain the speed. And early specialisation explains the age-three fluency. The materialist account is not a strawman; it is a genuine, powerful, partial answer.

Where science goes quiet

But press it on the hardest cases and it grows uncomfortably silent. Why this child and not the one beside him with the same advantages? Why does the talent so often arrive with an emotional certainty of vocation — Mozart did not merely play, he belonged at the keyboard? Why do prodigies describe the knowledge as recognised rather than acquired? Genetics tells us the lock can open. It does not tell us what is standing on the other side of the door.

Part Two · The Spiritual Reading

Dharma — The Soul's Inheritance

Bhagavad Gita 6.43, the unfinished yogi, and the memory that survives death.

Five thousand years before the bell curve, the same question was put to the same kind of teacher. Arjuna, on the battlefield, asks Krishna a beautifully practical thing: what happens to the seeker who tries sincerely, makes real progress on the spiritual path — and then dies before reaching the goal? Is his effort wasted, scattered like a torn cloud? Krishna's answer, across verses 6.40 to 6.45, is the heart of this essay.

The striving never perishes, Krishna says. The fallen yogi (the yoga-bhraṣṭa) is reborn — perhaps in a pure and prosperous home, or, rarer still, directly into a family of the wise. And then comes the verse that changes everything. Verse forty-three:

tatra taṃ buddhi-saṃyogaṃ
labhate paurva-dehikam
yatate ca tato bhūyaḥ
saṃsiddhau kuru-nandana

Bhagavad Gita 6.43

"There, O joy of the Kurus, he regains the intelligence of his former body — and from that point he strives yet again toward perfection."

Read it slowly. Paurva-dehikam — "belonging to the former body." Buddhi-saṃyogam — "the union with, the re-connection to, his developed intelligence." The verse states, without metaphor, that the developed faculties of one life are recovered in the next. Not re-learned. Recovered. And the very next verse drives the nail all the way in:

pūrva-abhyāsena tenaiva
hriyate hy avaśo 'pi saḥ

Bhagavad Gita 6.44

"By that former practice alone he is carried onward — even against his own will."

Read that line and then look again at the four-year-old who will not leave the piano, the child who weeps when taken from the chessboard, the boy in the Mumbai courtyard who picks up the bat as if reuniting with an old friend. Avaśo 'pi — "even helplessly," even though he could not tell you why. The Gita is not describing a hobby. It is describing a current, a momentum from a previous life that sweeps the child along before reason or choice can intervene.

Figure 4 · The Cycle of 6.43 — The Returning Yogi

The prodigy, in this reading, is not at the start of a journey. He is at stage four of an old one — a yogi who ran out of life mid-stride and has resumed exactly where the thread was cut.

Saṃskāra and vāsanā — the file that survives the device

How is such a thing carried? The Indian tradition has a precise vocabulary for it. Every action, every disciplined repetition, leaves a saṃskāra — a deep imprint on the subtle mind. Accumulated tendencies become vāsanā, the latent dispositions that incline us toward what we have long done. In Patañjali's Yoga Sūtras, these impressions are explicitly described as surviving death and conditioning the next birth, ripening according to place, time, and circumstance (jāti-deśa-kāla). The body decays; the saṃskāras do not. They are the soul's saved file, waiting only for new hardware capable of running them.

And note who is qualified to carry the most: the verse says the fallen yogi is reborn into a home that can support the gift — a pure, often prosperous family, sometimes a lineage of the wise. Read that against the data. Prodigies do tend to appear where the gift can be noticed and fed: a household with a piano, a father who builds a courtyard pitch, an uncle who keeps a chessboard. The Gita predicted the demographics of talent three millennia before anyone collected them.

A careful distinction — prodigy is not avatar

We should resist a tempting confusion. An avatāra — Rama, Krishna — is a descent of the divine into form, fully conscious of its own nature and mission. A prodigy is something far humbler and more relatable: an ordinary soul who simply did a great deal of work in a previous life and is now cashing the cheque. The child genius is not a god. He is us, a few lifetimes further down the same road. That is not a smaller idea. It is a more hopeful one.

The whisper of evidence

Does anything outside scripture point the same way? Cautiously, yes. For decades at the University of Virginia, the psychiatrist Ian Stevenson documented thousands of young children — most around the age of three, the same age our prodigies ignite — who spontaneously described detailed, verifiable memories of a previous life: names, places, manners of death, occasionally birthmarks corresponding to old wounds. His successor Jim Tucker continues the archive. This is not proof; it is data that the materialist frame struggles to digest, gathered by careful scientists who began as sceptics. Set it beside Treffert's "genetic memory," and two researchers from opposite ends of the philosophical world end up pointing at the same impossible thing: knowledge that arrived without being learned.

Part Three · The Synthesis

The Body Is Darwin's. The Fire Is Older.

Why this is not an either/or — and the metaphor that unites the two.

We have been told to choose: gene or soul, Darwin or Dharma, jackpot or rebirth. But the smartest answer this essay can offer is that the question itself contains a false fork. The two readings are not competitors describing the same layer. They are descriptions of different layers — and they fit together with a precision that ought to make both camps uncomfortable.

The Darwin reading — the hardware

  • Genes are recombined into a rare, unrepeatable hand.
  • Emergenesis: a configuration that won't survive to the next generation.
  • A brain with vast working memory and fast, early-myelinated pathways.
  • Answers: how is the gift physically possible?
  • Goes silent on: why this soul, why this certainty?

The Dharma reading — the software

  • A yogi's unfinished practice (abhyāsa) carried as saṃskāra.
  • Reborn into a family able to support the gift (Gita 6.43).
  • The faculties are recovered, not acquired — "carried onward against his will."
  • Answers: why this soul, why so early, why the vocation?
  • Goes silent on: the physical mechanism of recovery.

Each lens is blind exactly where the other sees. So let us stop forcing them to fight and instead let them compose. Here is the metaphor PaddySpeaks offers for the whole mystery — and the infographic below states the closing line outright: "the body is Darwin's; the fire inside it is older than that."

Figure 5 · Hardware & Software — The Loaded Device

A phone with no apps is a slab of glass. The richest software is useless without a chip to run it. The prodigy is the rare moment when an exceptional device (the genetic jackpot) boots up carrying an exceptionally developed file (the soul's accumulated practice). You need both. Either alone is ordinary.

This synthesis dissolves the paradoxes that defeated each lens alone. Why do the parents look ordinary? Because they supplied only the device, and emergenesis guarantees the device is a fresh, non-heritable build — while the file was never theirs to give; it belongs to the incoming soul. Why so early? Because a young brain is uncluttered — fewer competing habits, less ego, less interference — so the old file loads cleanly, with the volume turned all the way up, before the noise of ordinary life drowns it out. Why the certainty, the vocation, the "main khelega"? Because to the child it does not feel like ambition. It feels like returning.

A child of three does not learn the piano.
Sometimes, he remembers it.

Why the gift so often comes with a wound

One more pattern the synthesis explains, and it is the most poignant. Prodigies are frequently lopsided — dazzling in one dimension, fragile or delayed in others. The Darwin reading attributes this to the same neural configuration that gifts the talent. The Dharma reading offers something gentler: a soul that poured a thousand lifetimes' devotion into one pursuit arrives rich in that single currency and ordinary in all the rest. The fire is concentrated. It illuminates one room of the house brilliantly and leaves the others in shadow. This is not a defect. It is the signature of a long, narrow, faithful practice.

What This Means For The Rest Of Us

It would be easy to read all this as a verdict on the unchosen — proof that the gifted were simply dealt better cards, in this life or a former one, while the rest of us were not. That reading misses the entire point of verse 6.44, and it is worth correcting before we close.

For the parent of an ordinary child

Stop comparing the fruit to the neighbour's tree

If genius is a non-heritable configuration or a soul's private ledger, then your child's pace is not a referendum on your parenting, and the prodigy next door is not a rebuke. Every child carries a different file. The cruellest thing you can do to a slow-ripening soul is to measure it against a fast one. Let every child ripen at its own pace.

For the prodigy

The gift is borrowed, not earned-in-this-life

If your talent is a recovered file, it asks for humility, not arrogance — you are standing on the shoulders of someone you used to be. And it can be squandered. A jackpot of hardware running no further practice goes nowhere. Vaibhav still has to face the next ball.

For the rest of us — the real lesson

You are writing this lifetime's file right now

Here is the quiet thunderclap of 6.43, the part the gifted-versus-ungifted framing erases entirely: every prodigy was once an ordinary person who practised. The saṃskāra that makes a genius did not fall from the sky; it was earned, one disciplined repetition at a time, by a soul that was once exactly as unremarkable as you feel today. Your abhyāsa — your practice now — is not lost. By 6.44, it carries forward, and will one day carry you, helplessly, irresistibly, toward something that a future family will call a miracle.

The Gita's actual promise

Krishna never tells Arjuna to envy the accomplished yogi. He tells him that no sincere effort is ever wasted — na hi kalyāṇa-kṛt kaścid durgatiṃ tāta gacchati (6.40), "one who does good never comes to ruin." The prodigy is simply the proof of the mechanism, made visible. The mechanism is available to everyone. It just usually takes longer than one lifetime to show.

The five forces, where both lenses agree

Strip away the metaphysics and there is a band of common ground — five forces that every honest observer, materialist or mystic, watches converge in a single child. They are the five themes the hero image arranges along its base, and they make a useful checklist for the mystery:

① Unique Genius

The raw, unrepeatable configuration — the emergenic hand of cards that science calls a one-in-millions build.

② Deep Focus

An attention so total it looks like trance. The child does not try to concentrate; the world simply narrows to the one thing.

③ Relentless Passion

The pull of 6.44 — avaśo 'pi, "even against his will." Not chosen, not negotiable, felt as destiny rather than decision.

④ Saṃskāras & Past Impressions

The carried-over file — the soul's developed intelligence (buddhi-saṃyogam) recovered, not re-learned.

⑤ Nurture, Environment & Grace

The courtyard pitch, the household piano, the family that could notice. The Gita's "worthy family," without which even the richest file never loads.

Remove any one and the prodigy does not appear. The genius without focus dissipates; the passion without environment starves; the file without grace is never opened. It takes all five aligning at once — which is, of course, exactly why prodigies are rare.

So — Prodigy, or Reincarnation?

Both. And neither, alone. The genetic account is true and is not the whole truth; it builds the instrument but cannot explain the music that pours out of a child who has had no time to compose it. The spiritual account is unprovable and is not therefore false; it names the musician where science can only describe the violin. The honest answer to "Darwin or Dharma?" is the one the infographic whispers at the bottom of the frame: the body is Darwin's, and the fire inside it is older than that.

The next time a fourteen-year-old swings at his first ball and sends it into the night sky, or a three-year-old's fingers find a melody they were never taught, do not rush to settle which lens is correct. Stand for a moment in the bafflement. It is one of the few places left where science and scripture, the gene and the Gita, lean in toward the same impossible child — and, for once, fall silent together.

Genius is not the gift of being born great.
It is the recovery of greatness you once worked for —
in a life you no longer remember,
by a self you are still becoming.
